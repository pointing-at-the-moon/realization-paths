## 1.2 The Last Thought

Picture a long mountain road. The walker has covered the whole mileage of the ten grounds; only the final stretch remains, running to the summit. The natural question: how many more steps? As if the road's ending were the spending of the remaining steps one by one, the last footfall landing on the peak.

But the "last step" the texts present is not a stretch of road at all. It is a severance completed in a single instant, and it bears a proper name: the adamantine concentration—the concentration likened to adamant. The previous chapter established what equal-awakening lacks: one definite portion—the beginningless-nescience abiding-ground, its end known, its beginning unexhausted. This chapter asks: how is that portion severed? And the shape of the answer will push the question one level deeper: when this last portion of nescience is ended in one instant, what happens to the *road itself*?

### Crushing All, Crushed by None

The *Yogācārabhūmi* gives the concentration its clearest definition:

> What is the samādhi likened to adamant? It is the last samādhi at the trainee's edge. Because this samādhi is the very first, the most honored and supreme, utterly firm; because no defilement above can crush it, and because it crushes every defilement—therefore this concentration is named "likened to adamant."

"The last samādhi at the trainee's edge"—the concentration arising at the outermost limit of the trainee's stage. A "trainee" is one still learning, still with defilements to sever; this concentration arises at the trainee's last moment. Why liken it to adamant? The treatise gives two reasons, opposite in direction and complementary: it crushes every defilement, and no defilement can crush it.

Chew the simile slowly. An everyday blade cuts things, and itself dulls, and can be broken by something harder; adamant is the simile precisely because it cuts all and is cut by nothing. Returned to the context of severing defilements, what does that mean? If there is a concentration that can sever every defilement, with nothing above it able to overcome it, then after such a concentration there can be no "defilement awaiting severance"—all have been crushed by it; nor any "keener concentration"—nothing above can best it. So the name "adamantine concentration" already defines it as severance's terminus. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* says it in one line: it "breaks every fetter of defilement, with no remainder left"—and where breaking reaches "no remainder," the business of breaking is at its end.

### One Instant

How fast does it sever? The *Cheng weishi lun* leaves no room. The bodhisattva of the tenth ground still carries the two subtlest things: the innate, subtle cognitive hindrance, and the spontaneous seeds of the afflictive hindrance. Then:

> When the adamantine concentration comes to presence, these are all severed at once, and one enters the Tathāgata-stage.

"Severed at once," "enters the Tathāgata-stage"—the moment this concentration manifests, both are ended together, and buddhahood is entered on the spot. The treatise marks the time-measure expressly: "in one instant, the three realms severed at once"—and the seeds of the cognitive hindrance likewise "only in one instant all severed to the last."

Why must it be one instant rather than a gradual whittling? The nature of what is severed decides it. The severing on the earlier road was mostly gradual—the śrāvaka severing fetter by fetter, the bodhisattva ground by ground, like pruning, branch after branch. But the beginningless-nescience ground is no branch; it is the root: every defilement arises from the four abiding-grounds, and the four abiding-grounds stand upon it—it is the very root from which the defilements arise. A root is not severed in installments: cut the root, and everything that stood upon it loses its footing at once and ends together. Hence the scripture's mark, "in one instant the three realms severed at once"—not one portion first and another after, but root cut and branches gone in a single thought.

This rules out the picture of a gradual transition at the last. Were the passage from equal- to sublime-awakening gradual, there would have to be a halfway state, half equal and half sublime; and the texts say plainly there is none: before that thought, equal-awakening; after it, sublime-awakening; no third position between. The *Keyūra* says the equal-awakening bodhisattva, knowing the nescience-ground has an end, "flows naturally into the ocean-ground of sublime-awakening"—and "flows naturally" rewards attention: not one more laborious stretch, but knowing the end *is* the flowing in; between severing and entering there is no room for a third act.

### The Trainee's Last Thought

Now the chapter's deepest layer. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* assigns the adamantine concentration its place:

> It is the trainee's very last thought.

The trainee's—last—thought. A "trainee" is one still on the road, still with something to learn and to sever; the "last thought" is his final one. Those words say the great thing outright: the thought of the adamantine concentration is still "training"—still road, still the causal stage; yet it is training's final thought—the next thought is beyond training, is fruit.

So this one thought holds a double office. As the severing mind, it belongs to the road—it is the road's last act. And precisely because it severs the last defilement, it is at the same time the road's terminating thought—after it there is nothing left to sever, and the road has no work remaining. See the structure clearly: the road does not stop after reaching its end; the road, in its own last act, because nothing remains to sever, uses itself up on the spot. When the severing thought falls, there is no road underfoot—for what makes a road a road is "something still to sever, somewhere still to walk"; defilement's end and road's end share one instant.

The *Cheng weishi lun* watches that same instant at still finer grain. Severance properly has two paths: the thought in the act of severing is called the uninterrupted path; the thought that, severance done, realizes the cessation is called the path of liberation. The two vehicles, of duller faculty, must raise the two separately—first the uninterrupted, then the liberation; the bodhisattva, of keen faculty, "severs and realizes instant by instant"—in one and the same instant both severing and realizing, with no need to raise them apart. Returned to the adamantine concentration: in the bodhisattva's last thought, the stroke that severs is the stroke that realizes. The road's last act is, at once, the fruit's first presence.

That is the most exact statement of "the path's self-completion": the road is not finished—it is used up, in the last severing act of its final thought, turning into fruit on the spot because nothing remains to sever.

### Not a Concentration to Dwell In

Lastly, one wrong turning to bar. Hearing the four words "adamantine concentration," the readiest image is of some vast, solemn state of absorption one might abide in—like the four dhyānas and eight attainments, enterable, dwellable, sustainable. The texts define it along precisely the opposite line: its mark is *severing*—the last uninterrupted path, the stroke that crushes every defilement; its time-measure is *one instant*—the road's final thought, no place of long abiding. It is a mechanism of severance, not a state of experience. Whoever, at the very last pass, still reaches for "dwelling in some great adamantine absorption" is asking of this concentration the one thing it does not have. What the practitioner should take to heart is "the uninterrupted is the liberation"—the stroke that severs is the stroke that realizes; seek no dwellable halfway house between them.

(A note on terms in passing: later writers often speak of severing "the last grade of nescience," "the primal nescience"—"grade" and "primal" are the usage of Tiantai and other later classifiers; the treatises and sūtras this chapter rests on nowhere contain the phrase. The scripture's own word is "the beginningless-nescience abiding-ground." This book keeps the scripture's word.)

The last thought has fallen. The place where it settles has a name. Cross the bridge; Part Two speaks of it.
