## 1.1 Off by One

The bodhisattva's fifty-two positions—the Ten Faiths, Ten Abodes, Ten Practices, Ten Dedications, Ten Grounds, and at the last equal-awakening and sublime-awakening—that number is a later classifiers' synthesis of the scriptural schemes; the *Bodhisattva-keyūra-sūtra*'s own frame is the "forty-two worthies and nobles" and the "six lineage-natures." This book borrows "fifty-two positions" because everyone knows it today; but remember: it is the classifiers' number, not the scripture's letter. Wherever the doctrine matters, this chapter stands on the scripture's own words.

By that familiar count, equal-awakening is the fifty-first: above the ten grounds, below sublime-awakening (the Buddha), the position adjacent to the fruit. And an almost reflexive picture follows: equal-awakening is "higher" than the ten grounds—merit rounder, powers broader, experience nearer the Buddha's. As if the ten grounds were ninety-nine points, equal-awakening ninety-nine point nine, the Buddha one hundred; as if the position were made by *addition*—one more measure of attainment, closing on full marks and not yet full.

This chapter means to exchange that picture. When the texts define equal-awakening, their eye is never on what it has amassed beyond the ten grounds, but on what still parts it from the Buddha—and that gap is exactly one. Equal-awakening is not the "ninety-nine point nine" of a high score; it is the threshold position "short by exactly the last portion." It is defined, entirely, by that single remaining thing.

### Equal, Not Yet Sublime

Begin with the name. The *Keyūra* gathers the bodhisattva positions under six lineage-natures, and the last two are precisely these:

> Sons of the Buddha! The natures are: the nature of habituation, the nature of disposition, the nature of the way, the nature of the noble, the nature of equal-awakening, the nature of sublime-awakening.

The nature of equal-awakening stands just after the noble and just before sublime-awakening—second from the end, with only sublime-awakening beyond it. The sūtra also closes its positions in a list of forty-two grounds: "the ten abodes, ten practices, ten dedications, ten grounds, the stainless ground, the ground of sublime-awakening"—where "the stainless ground" is equal-awakening's other name. (Note this at once: this "stainless ground" stands *above* the ten grounds and below sublime-awakening; the second of the ten grounds, the "ground of freedom from stain"—rendered in some translations by the same characters—is a different thing entirely. Same name, different positions; tell them apart by context.)

The "equal" of equal-awakening means equal to, on a level with, awakening—it describes a awakening already *almost* equal to the Buddha's sublime one. But "almost equal" is exactly not "the same." The very order of the names is a doctrinal announcement: equal stands before sublime; equal is not sublime. This seems trivial, yet it has already ruled the picture of addition out. If equal-awakening were only "a ten-grounds with a little more merit," the gap to the Buddha would be one of quantity—amass a little more and draw nearer, an asymptote. But "equal-awakening, sublime-awakening" are not marks on a gauge; they are the two banks of a threshold: "equal" is standing at awakening's edge without stepping in; "sublime" is the step taken. A position defined as "this side of a certain threshold" is defined by the threshold—by the fact of *not yet having crossed*—not by how much it has gathered on this bank.

### Deliberately Withheld

Beyond the name, the position's most solid content is "the penultimate birth"—one span of life remaining, the next birth filling the Buddha's place. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s definition of it is itself a sentence about being off by one:

> There are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who cultivate the four absorptions up through the eighteen distinctive attributes, and have not realized the four truths: know that such a bodhisattva is of the penultimate birth.

"Cultivating the four absorptions up through the eighteen distinctive attributes"—of the Buddha's qualities, he possesses nearly every one. Then where does he differ from the Buddha? In "not having realized the four truths." And the treatise at once unpacks those words:

> "Not realized the four truths" means: deliberately withheld, not realized. Were he to take the realization, he would become a pratyekabuddha; wishing to become a Buddha, he does not realize.

The weight of this passage is immense. "Not realized" is not "unable to realize"; it is "deliberately withheld"—kept in reserve, held untaken. Why deliberately? To take, at this point, the realization nearest to hand is to fall into the fruit of the two vehicles ("become a pratyekabuddha"); and his aim is the larger thing ("wishing to become a Buddha"), so he precisely does not take it.

A comparison. One who has trained at a craft long since meets every condition for the certificate, yet does not go and collect it—not because he would fail the test, but because he knows that to take a certain license is to be filed into a smaller trade; his aim is to open something larger, so he leaves the license uncollected for now. Such is the "withholding" of the penultimate birth. And so the position's mark turns out to be a most remarkable one: not "what he has realized," but "the one realization he deliberately keeps unrealized." The height of his station shows not as having taken the most, but as being the most entitled to take—and taking last. This "off by one" is, half of it, of his own keeping: not a defect but a largeness of frame. Only one whose aim is the full fruit of buddhahood will stay his hand before a lesser ultimate lying within reach.

The treatise asks one more question: why speak only of "one birth remaining," never of two or three? Because "one" is already the limit—one birth left, and the Buddha's place is filled; there is no further rung to add. The "one" here is no casual number; it is the seal of a position at its utmost.

### The End Known, the Beginning Not

The withholding is his own; but the remaining portion has its objective referent too. What exactly lies in the hair's-breadth between equal- and sublime-awakening? The *Keyūra* gives the most precise sentence in the sūtra. It first says: all defilements arise from the four abiding-grounds, and before the four abiding-grounds nothing further arises—hence the name "the beginningless-nescience abiding-ground," the deepest stratum of nescience. Then:

> The adamantine cognition knows that this first-arisen single mark has an end, but does not know whether, before its beginning, there is anything or nothing ... only the Buddha knows the beginning and knows the end.

The equal-awakening bodhisattva's cognition, keen to the utmost, the sūtra calls "adamantine cognition," "adamantine mind." That keenest of cognitions knows that the beginningless-nescience ground "has an end"—knows it can be severed, knows its terminus; yet "does not know its beginning"—cannot exhaust its furthest source. Only the Buddha knows beginning and end alike. The *Renwang Prajñā-sūtra* draws the line still shorter: "the mark of nescience ending is the adamantine; the ending-mark itself markless is sarvajñā." Nescience on the point of ending and not yet wholly ended—that is the adamantine, and there the equal-awakening cognition stands; when even the mark of "ending" is gone—that is sarvajñā, the Buddha's omniscience. The equal-awakening cognition is the blade "in the act of ending it, with one portion yet remaining"—not the Buddha's cognition for which it is "already ended."

So the "off by one" is not a distance but this one portion: with respect to the beginningless-nescience ground, the end known, the beginning unexhausted. What remains is definite and has a name—a point the next chapter will put to great use.

### Three "Ones"

Set the three faces together. In position: the name "equal" itself means equal-and-not-yet-sublime—the one step on this side of the threshold, not yet crossed. In conduct: the penultimate birth "deliberately withholds"—the one realization held untaken. In severance: the adamantine cognition "knows the end, not the beginning"—the one nescience whose beginning is unexhausted. One step, one realization, one nescience: three faces of the same "one." Equal-awakening as a position is defined entirely by it: not constituted by more, but standing by one remainder.

And the stakes of this verdict are the whole road's ending. Read equal-awakening by addition, and the road's end is an endless asymptote, buddhahood a limit forever approached by one more measure and never touched—the walker spends a life inside "almost there." Read it by "off by one," and the road's end is a definite threshold: what remains is a specific, final, severable object.

Then—how is that one severed? The texts answer with surprising unanimity: by one concentration, in one instant. The next chapter looks at that instant.
