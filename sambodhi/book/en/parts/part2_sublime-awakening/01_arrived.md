## 2.1 Arrived

The first two chapters drove the road's ending to a single point: equal-awakening off by one; the adamantine concentration severing that one in a single instant; and the severing thought, being the road's last step, turning into fruit on the spot because nothing remained to sever. The place where the last step settles is called sublime-awakening.

But "what it is called" is easy; "how it is to be read" is what matters. The natural reading takes sublime-awakening for "a higher rung above equal-awakening": equal-awakening already close to the Buddha, sublime-awakening closer still, indeed the Buddha—one more step on the same rising staircase. This chapter says: that reading puts sublime-awakening in the wrong place. **Sublime-awakening is not a higher rung on the road; it is the road's end**—the place where "walking" itself ceases.

### The Staircase Stops Here

First think through what the word "rung" presupposes. A rung is a rung because above it another rung is possible—a staircase is a staircase because above every step there is a step. If sublime-awakening were "a higher rung," the question would follow: what is above sublime-awakening?

And the texts, three lines of them, answer with one voice: above sublime-awakening, no position. The *Keyūra* sets sublime-awakening as the last of its forty-two grounds, and describes it head-on:

> The forty-second ground is named the ground of sublime-awakening, the mind of quiescence: ever abiding in one mark, first and limitless, still as empty space; the omniscience of all modes illumining the beginning and end of birthless truth. Only the Buddha exhausts utterly the root of beings, its beginning and its end.

Mark three phrases. "First and limitless"—utmost, with nothing above. "Illumining the beginning and end"—Part One said equal-awakening knows the end and not the beginning; here sublime-awakening illumines both: the two sentences answer each other exactly. "Only the Buddha exhausts utterly"—exhausted, without remainder. Earlier the sūtra glosses the very name in three characters: "the unsurpassed ground"—unsurpassed: above it, nothing. The Yogācāra line does not use the name "sublime-awakening"; it calls the same buddha-fruit "the ultimate stage"—the fifth and last of the five stages of cultivation, "abiding in unsurpassed perfect bodhi," its mark "the obscuration ended, the clarity full": the fullness shown when the hindrances are out. The *Avataṃsaka* speaks yet another language, "the Tathāgata's full awakening," describing it as "without two, without mark, without walking, without halting"—even "walking" and "halting" surpassed. Three lines, three names—sublime-awakening, the ultimate stage, the Tathāgata's full awakening—each speaking its own tongue, all naming the one buddha-fruit terminus; this book records the three names side by side and forces them into none.

The words the three lines use are all words for an *end*: last, unsurpassed, exhausted, limitless. Not one is a word for a *step*. So the difference between sublime- and equal-awakening is not the difference between a lower and a higher step on one staircase, but between "the staircase's second-to-last step" and "the place where the staircase stops, with no step left to climb." Equal-awakening is a position on the road—above it one step remains; sublime-awakening is the road's terminus—above it, no step at all. Sublime-awakening is not on the road. Sublime-awakening is the road's end.

### Walking Exhausted

Second question: why does the road end exactly here—no sooner, no later?

Answer: because the road's two defining activities run out of object here. What is done on a road is two things and two only: severing—severing defilements; and walking—going on. As to severing: the last chapter showed the adamantine concentration ending the final portion in one instant; at sublime-awakening, "the obscuration ended, the clarity full," "only the Buddha exhausts utterly"—the two hindrances forever ended, not one defilement left to sever. Severing requires a defilement awaiting it as object; the defilements exhausted, severance has nowhere to land. As to walking: going on requires a "place not yet reached" as object—one walks by walking *toward*; sublime-awakening being ultimate, no unreached place remains, and walking has nowhere to go. The *Avataṃsaka*'s four characters, "without walking, without halting," may stand as this chapter's eye.

Severing without object, walking without object—the road stops of itself. And note: this is not "at the road's end stands a city called Sublime-Awakening, and one halts at its gate"—not an end in space; it is "the business of walking the road has, at this point, nothing left to do"—an exhaustion of activity. The last chapter said the final severing thought turns into fruit on the spot because nothing remains to sever; this chapter names the ground where that turning lands: the road does not stop *outside* sublime-awakening—in the same instant of reaching it, having no work left, it ceases. Walking exhausted: hence, the ground where walking ends.

### One Home, Two Ledgers

The third layer is the joint of the whole series. The same sublime-awakening can be entered in two ledgers.

As with a traveler come home. One ledger records "he walked up to the door"—the arrival, the journey at its ultimate. The other records "his ease once home"—nowhere further to go, and every movement unhindered. The same homecoming: the one states the walking-to, the other the dwelling-in.

This series keeps exactly those two ledgers. The Realization axis's three volumes write, all the way, "how one walks there": how the four fruits sever the fetters, how the fifty stations amass the provisions, how equal-awakening is off by one, how the adamantine concentration severs it in an instant—and writing up to sublime-awakening, the Realization axis reads one word: **arrived**. While the same sublime-awakening, the Attainment axis's twin volume reads from the other face: **the freedom with no further step to walk**. Where the Realization axis sees "the terminus reached," the Attainment axis sees "no terminus left to head for—hence nowhere to go and nowhere obstructed," the fullness itself.

The two ledgers do not conflict; they are two faces of the one buddha-fruit. As for how the two faces divide and reconcile in fine—that belongs to the seam between the axes; this volume does not adjudicate it alone, and only marks it here. This volume's office runs to "arrived" and no further: the full breadth of the fruit *as* fruition-virtue—the Tathāgata's ten epithets, three bodies, four wisdoms, ten powers, ten masteries—this volume unfolds not one line; that is where the twin volume sets out. Where this chapter has touched "the two hindrances forever ended" or "the omniscience of all modes," it was only to name what the road arrives at, not to open the view inside the door.

### Three Volumes, Walked

Here ends not only this book but the whole road of the Realization axis's three volumes. Look back once down the length of it.

The first volume, *The Four Fruits of the Path*: a ruler of subtraction—each fruit defined by which fetters are severed, severance complete being arhatship; it measures what has grown less. The second, *The Bodhi Path*: a ruler of accumulation—the gate opening at the arousing of bodhicitta, the fifty stations measuring what mind arose, what provisions were amassed, for whose sake the realization within reach was left untaken. This third volume, a third ruler: **the last remainder and its dissolution**—what one portion is left (equal-awakening off by one), how it is erased (the adamantine concentration's single instant), and where, with it gone, the road comes to rest (sublime-awakening, walking exhausted). Three rulers, and the one road of the Realization axis is measured out.

And the place where the road stops leaves the walker one last lesson. One heading for the ultimate most easily pictures buddhahood as "a layer higher and brighter than equal-awakening," and practice becomes a climbing of rungs without end, the summit forever overhead. "The ground where walking ends" is the medicine: sublime-awakening is not overhead; it is the place with no "up" remaining. The road's end is not the topmost step climbed, but walking carried to where no step is left to climb, no defilement left to sever, no ground left to head for—and there it ceases. What is needed is not something higher, but to walk this road until, with nothing left to do, it ends of itself.

The resting is the ground; walking exhausted is itself awakening.

The Realization axis's three volumes lay down the pen here. The view inside the door—what manner of freedom that "no further step to walk" finally is—begins with the twin volume. The road hands on its baton at the door.
