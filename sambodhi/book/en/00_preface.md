# Preface · The Last Two Stations

The previous volume, *The Bodhi Path*, set its pen down at a door.

The bodhisattva of the Dharma-Cloud Ground had received the office and fallen within the number of the buddhas—entered in the register, yet still parted from buddhahood by one most subtle obstacle of cognition, and so not yet Buddha. That book said: the last two stations on the doorstep, equal-awakening and sublime-awakening, belong to a volume of their own. This is that volume. It does one thing only: it walks you up the road's last two stations—and shows you clearly what kind of "station" each one is.

### An Anxiety, Named First

Whoever has walked long knows a certain taste: so close, and yet, it seems, forever short by a little.

The work has been done, the good amassed, the road learned by heart—and still the word *ultimate* hangs ahead, neither near nor far. As if buddhahood were an asymptote: add one more measure of merit and draw one measure closer, yet always short by a hair. In that picture the road's end is an endlessly divisible remainder, approached forever and never touched; and the walker spends a whole life inside three words—*almost there*.

Few think to ask the prior question: how does the canon itself speak of this last stretch?

### A Thesis in One Sentence

This book has a single thesis, sayable in one sentence: **equal-awakening stands by "one thing still remaining"; that one is severed in the single instant of the adamantine concentration; and sublime-awakening is the ground where walking ends—the road's end is not a higher rung climbed, but the road finishing itself in its own last act.**

Unfolded, it is three chapters' work. Equal-awakening, the position adjacent to buddhahood: the texts define it not by counting what it has amassed beyond the ten grounds, but by pointing to what still parts it from the Buddha—and that gap is exactly one: one nescience unexhausted, one birth unspent, one realization within reach and deliberately left untaken. Equal-awakening is the position *off by one*. How is that one severed? By one concentration, in one instant—the adamantine concentration, the road's last thought; when that thought falls, severance has no object left, and the road ends itself within that very thought. Where it settles bears a name: sublime-awakening. Sublime-awakening is not the staircase's topmost step; it is the place where the staircase stops, with no step left to climb.

So the medicine this book offers for that anxiety is not "try harder." It means to tell you: what remains is not an endless distance but a definite, final, severable one—severable in a single thought. The thing that remains has a name; the concentration that severs it has a name; the ground reached when it is gone has a name. The asymptote is imagined; what the canon set there is a threshold.

### Three Rulers, Three Volumes

This is the third volume of the series' Realization axis. The first, *The Four Fruits of the Path*, measured by subtraction—which fetters severed, which fruit defined. The second, *The Bodhi Path*, measured by accumulation—what mind aroused, what provisions amassed. This third volume is the thinnest, three chapters only, yet its ruler is one neither of the others used: **the last remainder, and its dissolution**—what one thing is left, how that one is erased in a single thought, and where, with it gone, the road comes to rest. Three rulers, three volumes; the one road of the Realization axis, measured to its end.

### The Relation to the Twin Volume, Said Plainly as Ever

On the axis of Attainment stands this volume's twin, on "the Tathāgata's mastery"—what splendor the fruit of buddhahood is, as ten epithets, three bodies, four wisdoms, ten powers, ten masteries, the full breadth of perfected freedom. The two volumes read the same sublime-awakening: this one reads "arrived"; that one reads "the freedom with no further step to walk." Realization writes the walking; Attainment writes what is attained. So this volume's pen stops at the word *arrival*; of the full fruition-virtue inside the door it unfolds not one line—that is where the twin volume sets out.

And, as ever, what this book does not treat: how the fifty stations are walked belongs to the previous volume; how the four fruits are severed belongs to the first. This volume takes up its pen at equal-awakening and lays it down at sublime-awakening, crossing no boundary on either side.

### This Book Is Written for You

This book is not written for scholars. It does not debate, does not compare schools; it does one plain thing: it takes the canon's own words on the last two stations—what the remaining one is, what the severing instant is like, where the ground lies on which walking ends—and tells them to you, passage by passage.

It is written above all for you who walk the road and know the taste of "forever short by a little." What this book most wants to put into your hands is the exchange of one sentence: from "I am forever short by a little" to "what remains is this one last portion." The first sentence is an asymptote, and it wearies; the second is a threshold, and once seen clearly, the walking grows calm.

You need no Sanskrit and need memorize no scripture numbers. Numbers and romanization are gathered in the appendices at the back; the main text keeps itself clean, and tells you this last stretch of road.

### Walk Slowly

The whole book is two parts and three chapters, readable in one sitting. Best read it straight through—the three chapters are a single argument: first the one that remains, then the instant that severs it, last the ground where it settles. Miss none, and the threshold comes clear.

### At the Last

What the walker needs at the utmost point is not something higher, but to see plainly: where there is no "up" left, the resting is the ground—walking exhausted is itself awakening.

May you finish this thinnest of volumes, set down the words *almost there*, and recognize that one definite portion; and from then on walk unhurried, ungrasping after height, all the way to the ground where walking ends.

Homage to our original teacher, Śākyamuni Buddha.
