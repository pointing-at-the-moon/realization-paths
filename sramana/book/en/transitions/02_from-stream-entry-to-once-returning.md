# Transition Two · From Stream-Entry to Once-Returning

Part Two is walked. Count back what now stands in the stream-enterer.

Direction: the three fetters—identity-view, doubt, rite-grasping—severed. The subject set true, the departure resolved, the road discerned. The jailers withdrawn; the person in the right current. Certainty: no falling, because the hook that could pull one back no longer exists; at most seven returns, because a continuum of the noble path's kind ripens as the ripe fruit must fall—at most twenty-eight existences, then the end of suffering. Neither was given by anyone; both grew out of the severance itself.

But one matter Part Two deliberately held down, untouched.

The one who has entered the stream still has greed and anger. The earlier part said it plainly: craving and ill will are the latter two of the five lower fetters—fetters of the journey. They do not block direction; they block going far within the desire realm. The direction now set, the subtraction's blade turns next toward them. And exactly here appears the first true difficulty of the whole road.

Look at the definition of the second fruit: the three fetters severed, and greed, hatred, and delusion *attenuated*.

The first half is familiar grammar—three fetters severed, inherited from stream-entry. The second half is strange: attenuated. Not "severed"—*thinned*. Greed remains, only thinner; anger remains, only thinner. Up to this point the book has measured nothing but "severed"—a wall stands or is down for good, and Part One said it without softening: establishment admits no seventy percent; a wall does not stay half a wall at "seventy percent torn down." Yet "attenuated" sounds like exactly that—like a wall taken down halfway.

That is the next station's question: what is a wall half torn down? If "attenuated" meant only "not yet severed enough," then the second fruit would be a blur between stream-entry and non-returning—not even a marking on the scale. Yet the canon, in full solemnity, gives it a fruit of its own and a name of its own. Evidently, in the canon's eyes, "attenuated" is not a blur but a unit of subtraction with exact content. What that exact content is—how thinning, too, is a severance—is what Part Three answers head-on.

The name "once-returner" is also worth carrying in. The sotāpanna: at most seven returns. The sakadāgāmin: one return—at most once more among humans. From seven to one, the journey's ceiling tightens sharply. What kind of loss compresses twenty-eight existences into a single return? And the names themselves betray the scale's own language: the first fruit names its entry by the *stream*—direction; the second names its course by the *return*—the journey's remainder. The fruit's name switches from the vocabulary of direction to the vocabulary of mileage, and that switch happens exactly where the word "attenuated" appears. The "one" in the name and the "thinned" in the definition—are they two faces of the same thing?

One intimate contrast, too, before the door. "My greed and anger have thinned" is precisely the most common self-observation of the long-practiced—"my temper is smaller than it was," "fewer things I cannot put down." But the case in the Bamboo Grove settled this long ago: reports of experience do not count. "Seems thinner" is a feeling; the "attenuated" in a fruit's definition, if it is to stand, must be no feeling but a fact that can be measured. Where does felt thinning part from severed thinning? This question runs on the same thread as Part Two's "how is loosened told from severed"—and at "attenuated," it presses harder still.

One last contrast. The three fetters stream-entry severs are tied to *seeing wrongly*—fetters of view; the seeing once set right, what was tied to it unbinds. Greed and anger are different: they are not a wrong seeing but the habit-force of many lives; the seeing set right, they remain, to be worn away in contact with things, pass after pass. Here the subtraction's blade turns from *view* to *habit*. The severing of view hangs on one correction; the wearing of habit hangs on long grinding—and "attenuated" is the first mark that long grinding leaves on the scale of the fruits.

Part Three has a single chapter, and its question is the finest-grained in the book: thinning as severance. Go in and see.
