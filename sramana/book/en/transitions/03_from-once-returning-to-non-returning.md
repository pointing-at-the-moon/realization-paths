# Transition Three · From Once-Returning to Non-Returning

The battle of Part Three is fought. Take stock of what was held.

"Thinned" has rejoined the ranks: not a degree but a count—six of nine grades permanently severed, only the lower grade remaining. The ruler did not crack; it grew one level finer: the unit of severance ground down from the fetter to the grade, and each grade's severance still the true kind that never returns upon meeting conditions. "Once-returning" has been matched to its source: the strength of three remaining grades suffices for one return; the "one" in the name is the "thinned" of the definition, shadowed on the ledger of births.

Now look at the three parts of the wall still standing.

The lower three grades of greed and hatred—the subtlest residue of the nine. What the next station does can be said in one sentence: sever these three as well. The seventh grade, the eighth, the ninth, grade by grade, until of the desire realm's greed, hatred, and delusion not one grade remains. Together with what fell long ago—identity-view, rite-grasping, doubt—the five lower fetters are, at that point, exhausted entire.

But if the next station were only "more of the same severing," it would not deserve a new name. What deserves attention is what happens once the severing completes: a change of kind.

The once-returner differs from the stream-enterer by decrement of quantity—seven returns tightening to one; the number shrinks, but the person still comes back to the desire realm. The non-returner is different. The five lower fetters are the fetters "of the lower portion"—five ropes binding a person to the desire realm; all five severed, between the person and the desire realm not one rope remains. So the sutta's name for the third fruit is no longer a number but a negation: non-returning. Never coming back. From "once more" to "never again" is not the arithmetic extension of seven-to-one-to-zero—it is a change of realm. The question of rebirth turns from "how many times remain" to "whether one remains *here* at all."

And from this arises a question neither earlier station raised: one who does not return to the desire realm—where does he go? The sutta's answer is a destination that has not appeared before: the Pure Abodes. Born there, attaining final nibbāna there, never coming back. And the sutta divides the manner of "final nibbāna there" with great care: in the interval, at birth, with formations, without formations, and the upstream course—five kinds of final nibbāna. A road walked as far as non-returning, and the canon classifies even the gait of its last stretch.

So Part Four has two chapters. Chapter one watches the five lower fetters reach exhaustion—the finishing of the lower three grades, and the weight of the threshold called "leaving the desire realm": for one born in the desire realm, practicing in the desire realm, what kind of thing it is to sever desire for it entirely. Chapter two looks past the threshold at the destination and the gait—where the Pure Abodes stand, and how the five kinds of final nibbāna divide. One chapter for the severing; one for the journey after.

One sentence to carry through the door. For three stations, the ruler has measured "how far from the end"—seven returns, one return. From this station on, it begins to measure "how near to this realm"—non-returning. The subtraction's blade, having measured the length of the journey, now falls on the boundary of the realm itself.
