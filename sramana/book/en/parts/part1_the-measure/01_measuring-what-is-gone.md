## 1.1 Measuring What Is Gone

Sooner or later, a practitioner asks: how far have I come?

The question is natural. Light arises on the cushion—does that count as progress? Years pass without a single taste of meditative joy—does that mean standing still? Someone nearby enters absorption with ease, while one's own concentration stays dry—is that a poverty of faculties? Behind all these questions hides one and the same ruler: measuring progress by experience—how deep the absorption, how bright the light, how high the state. Experience becomes the currency of progress, and a fruit of the path looks like a medal that is exchanged automatically once enough experience has been saved up.

But this is not the ruler the Buddha used to measure the fruits.

In the Saṃyukta-āgama, the World-Honored One defined the four fruits of the renunciant's path with His own words. The four fruits are the four attainments of the śrāvaka path: sotāpanna, sakadāgāmin, anāgāmin, arahant—transliterated names whose meanings are, in order, "stream-enterer," "once-returner," "non-returner," and the one in whom defilements are forever exhausted. This is how the World-Honored One defined them:

> What are the fruits of the renunciant? The fruit of stream-entry, the fruit of once-returning, the fruit of non-returning, the fruit of arahantship. What is the fruit of stream-entry? The severing of the three fetters. What is the fruit of once-returning? The three fetters severed, and greed, hatred, and delusion attenuated. What is the fruit of non-returning? The five lower fetters exhausted. What is the fruit of arahantship? Greed, hatred, and delusion forever exhausted—all defilements forever exhausted.

Read the passage once more, and watch its grammar. "What is the fruit of X? It is—": these are defining sentences; the World-Honored One is answering, head-on, what a fruit *is*. And after every "it is," without a single exception, stands a severing or an exhausting: three fetters severed; three severed and greed-hatred-delusion attenuated; five lower fetters exhausted; all defilements forever exhausted. Nowhere does it say "the stream-enterer is one who attains a certain radiance"; nowhere, "the arahant is one who abides in a certain great bliss."

A "fetter" (saṃyojana) means a binding—a knot that ties the mind to the round of birth and death. The canon counts ten fetters, grouped into five lower and five higher; which ten, and how they group, the later stations will untie one by one. For now it is enough to see one thing clearly: the scale of the fruits, from first to last, is *which knots are severed*.

This ruler counts what is gone, not what is gained.

A fruit is less like reaching a height and more like tearing down walls. The stream-enterer tears down three; the non-returner, five; the arahant tears until nothing is left to tear. Even the highest fruit's self-attestation keeps this grammar. The arahant's declaration, as the Chinese āgamas customarily render it, runs in four phrases: "Birth is exhausted; the holy life has been lived; what was to be done is done; there is no further becoming." The anchors are the first phrase and the last—birth *exhausted*, becoming *no further*. Both are "already gone" and "never again." An arahant does not say, "I have attained such vast radiance." What he says is: here, the fuel of the round has burned out.

### Why Subtraction, of All Things

Why is this ruler a subtraction? To say it to the bottom, one must look at the *substance* of a fruit—what a fruit itself actually is.

In the treatises, the Abhidharmakośa says it plainly: the substance of a fruit of the renunciant's path is precisely the fact that certain defilements have ceased. The Dharma gives this cessation a proper name: *pratisaṃkhyā-nirodha*, cessation through discernment—by the power of discerning wisdom, a defilement is made never to arise again, and that very "never again" is the cessation. It is not a mood, not a state, not even something that arises; it is the standing-established of the fact that *this knot is no longer there*.

Since the substance of the fruit is "the knot already severed," to measure the fruit by severance is not to lay some foreign ruler against it—the severance *is* the fruit itself. It is like asking how much space has been cleared in a room: the only possible answer counts what has been carried out, because space just is the absence of things. A fruit is a fruit precisely as the fetter's being-severed.

Experience, by contrast—meditative joy, radiance, states of absorption—is mental phenomena that arise and pass. To measure the unconditioned cessation with conditioned experience is to measure length with a thermometer: the ruler and the thing measured belong to different kinds. The reading comes out wrong not for lack of precision, but because the kind is wrong.

### A Case in the Bamboo Grove

This is not only a conclusion reasoned out in the treatises. While the Buddha was in the world, one episode played it out in full.

At that time the Buddha was dwelling at Rājagaha, in the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove. Kings, ministers, brahmins, householders, and laypeople all came to revere and support the Buddha and the assembly of disciples; the offerings to the other teachers' followers had dried up. The many wanderers of other paths gathered in their hall and deliberated: who among us is clever enough to slip into the renunciant Gotama's order, go forth, learn the teaching, and bring it back—so that we may use it to win over kings and householders and recover our support? They settled on a young man of sharp intelligence, named Susīma.

Susīma came to the Bamboo Grove and asked the monks walking in the open air to let him go forth. The World-Honored One knew what was in his mind, and still told the monks: let him go forth.

Half a month after his going forth, a monk declared in Susīma's presence: "Birth and death are exhausted; the holy life has been lived; what was to be done is done; I myself know there is no further becoming." Susīma thought: one who can say this must possess unfathomable depths of absorption. So he asked, one by one: Venerable, have you attained the first jhāna? The second, the third, the fourth? The peaceful formless liberations, touched with the body? To every question the monks answered: "No, Susīma."

Susīma was stunned, and asked:

> How is it that what the venerable ones say does not hang together, the latter contradicting the former? How can one not attain the absorptions, and yet declare so?

The monk answered with a single sentence: "We are liberated by wisdom."

More puzzled still, Susīma went to the Buddha. The World-Honored One's ruling was brief: "They first know the standing of the Dhamma; afterward they know nibbāna."

The weight of this passage can hardly be overestimated. It sets before us a class of arahants formally ratified by the World-Honored One—the wisdom-liberated (paññāvimutta): they state outright that they do not attain the absorptions, do not enter the attainments, and their exhaustion of the taints is real; the World-Honored One set His seal on it. Which is to say: the experience of deep absorption is not a necessary condition of the taints' exhaustion. A person without deep meditative experience can be a complete arahant.

Now hold the two rulers side by side. Measure by states of absorption, and this whole class of noble ones reads as "not yet arrived"—true arahants, graded as failing. Measure by fetters severed, and the reading comes out exact: it is precisely because their knots are severed and their taints exhausted that they are acknowledged arahants, with no reference to whether absorption is present. The ruler of subtraction reaches them; the ruler of addition does not.

### A Wall Torn Down Does Not Grow Back

The ruler of subtraction has one more property that no other ruler can supply: it does not slide back.

States of absorption regress. Today one enters the fourth jhāna; tomorrow, scattered, one cannot. Today there is light; tomorrow, dullness. Experience is conditioned, and what is conditioned rises and falls. If the scale of the fruits were made of something that can regress, the fruits would regress with it—and then the canon's word that the stream-enterer "does not fall to the lower destinies and is bound for nibbāna" would have nothing to stand on. That word "bound" would hang in the air.

Severance does not regress. The fetter severed is a cessation through discernment—unconditioned; the unconditioned does not arise and pass, and there is no such thing as "severed, then grown back." The stream-enterer can carry the guarantee "bound for nibbāna" not because he has saved up some stock of merit—stocks deplete—but because three walls have been permanently torn down, and the tearing-down itself cannot be undone. A wall does not grow itself back.

### Two Things to See Clearly Here

First, this ruler does not slight meditative absorption in the least. The World-Honored One praised everywhere the paired cultivation of calm and insight; above the wisdom-liberated stands the "both-ways liberated," who joins deep attainment to the exhaustion of taints—an adornment of its own. Absorption is a powerful provision on the road: for ease in this very life, for the depth and breadth of wisdom, its benefit is great. What this chapter says is only this: provision is provision, and scale is scale. One may prize absorption highly, and at the same time hold that the scale of the fruits is severance alone.

Second, "severing a fetter" is not a feeling. "I feel that my anger is gone" is a report of experience—take it as proof of severance, and one has walked a circle back to measuring fruit by experience. Whether a knot is severed is judged by wisdom that sees things as they are, not by how one feels about oneself. The monks in the Bamboo Grove made their declaration by the knowledge of the taints' exhaustion, not by "I feel I have severed it." The ruler is objective; but reading the ruler takes right knowing.

### Exchanging the Question

This whole chapter comes down to exchanging one question.

From "what state have I attained" to "which wall, here in me, has truly come down—and can never be rebuilt." The first question leads a person into the comparing of experiences and the anxiety of gain and loss. The second leads to seeing one's own fetters as they are. For the practitioner whose concentration is dry, who again and again suspects that no progress has been made at all, this ruler is medicine: your progress is not entered in the ledger of experiences; it is entered in whether identity-view, doubt, and the grasping of rites have loosened in you—or fallen.

The ruler is right. Next, learn how its markings divide. When the canon counts the noble ones, it does not count four; it counts eight—four pairs, eight persons. Why eight? The next chapter says.
