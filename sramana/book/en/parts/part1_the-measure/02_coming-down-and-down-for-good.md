## 1.2 Coming Down, and Down for Good

The last chapter exchanged the ruler: the fruits are measured by severance. Following this ruler, the canon keeps a number worth pausing over—when it counts the noble ones, it does not count four. It counts eight.

And this "eight" does not come from some scholastic table. It comes from a sitting on daily practice.

### A Recollection Taught at Jeta's Grove

The Saṃyukta-āgama records: the Buddha was dwelling at Sāvatthī, in Jeta's Grove, and the venerable Mahākātyāyana was in the park as well. Mahākātyāyana said to the monks: the Buddha, the World-Honored One, the Tathāgata, knowing and seeing, has taught six things that lead out of suffering and up to the place of excellence—one single path that purifies beings: the recollection of the Buddha, of the Dhamma, of the Saṅgha, of virtue, of generosity, of the devas. He taught them one by one; and when he came to the third, the recollection of the Saṅgha—what is it that a noble disciple calls to mind when he recollects the Saṅgha?

> Those approaching stream-entry and those who have obtained the fruit of stream-entry; those approaching once-returning and those who have obtained once-returning; those approaching non-returning and those who have obtained non-returning; those approaching arahantship and those who have obtained arahantship. These four pairs, these eight persons, are the World-Honored One's community of disciples—accomplished in virtue, accomplished in concentration, in wisdom, in liberation, in the knowledge-and-vision of liberation; worthy of offerings, of honor, of veneration: an unsurpassed field of merit for the world.

Notice two things. First, the setting: this is not a classroom taxonomy; it is the very content of the daily exercise of recollecting the Saṅgha—when a noble disciple calls the Saṅgha-jewel to mind, what rises in his mind is exactly these pairs of the *approaching* and the *obtained*. The four pairs and eight persons are the structure of the Saṅgha-jewel; the Saṅgha is worthy of offerings and veneration, an unsurpassed field of merit, precisely because its members are these eight.

Second, the grammar. The sutta will not settle for naming four fruits and being done; every fruit is double-marked: "approaching stream-entry, obtaining the fruit of stream-entry"—one *approach*, one *obtainment*, four pairs, eight in all. The Madhyama-āgama's parallel renderings use "tending toward" and "realization of the fruit"; the structure is identical. Several passages, one construction: every fruit is a pair.

Why eight, and not four? Where exactly does the doubling lie?

### Approach Is Not a Waiting Room

First, set the word "approach" upright.

"Approach" is easily imagined as the fruit's waiting room—not yet passing, still trying, certificate not yet in hand. Read so, it is not entirely wrong, but it is far too light. On that reading, approach and fruit differ only in degree: seventy percent severed is approach, a full hundred is fruit. If that were all, the distinction would scarcely matter—and why would the canon so solemnly establish eight?

Approach is the severing *in progress*; fruit is the same act *completed*. Approach is the instant the wall is coming down; fruit is the wall down for good. The difference is not shallow against deep. It is moving against still.

Take a homely comparison: erasing a line of chalk from a blackboard. "Still erasing" and "erased clean" do not stand as fifty percent to a hundred. "Erased clean" is the establishment of a state—the line is no longer there; "still erasing" is the motion toward that establishment. Nobody says, "I have erased my way to eighty percent of that line's fruit." Either one is still erasing, or it is clean.

Why can a fruit not be given in percentages? The last chapter already laid the ground: the substance of the fruit is the cessation through discernment—the standing-established of "this knot is severed." Establishment admits no seventy percent: either it stands, or it does not. A wall does not stay half a wall at "seventy percent torn down." So the difference between approach and fruit is not, at root, a quantity of severing, but the two phases of the one event of severance: being-severed, and severed-and-abiding.

### The Sutta Sets the Frame; the Treatise Works the Mechanism

That one turn—from coming down to down for good—at exactly what instant does it happen?

That the āgamas list approach and fruit as pairs is the World-Honored One's explicit word. But the fine location of the turn was worked out later, by the treatise masters, on our behalf. The Abhidharmakośa says: when the practitioner reaches the sixteenth mind of the path of seeing—the knowledge of the kind of the path—he is called "abiding in the fruit," and is no longer called "approaching." The flip from moving phase to still phase is located at a single moment of mind.

Reading this layer, remember its station: it is the treasury's fine elaboration upon the sutta's frame, not the World-Honored One's own utterance. The sutta gives the skeleton—approach and fruit in pairs; the treatise adds the graduation—down to one thought-moment. Let each stand in its place, and both serve.

### Eight Names, Five Things

The Kośa leaves one more number worth turning over. Having counted the eight noble persons, the master says:

> Though the names are eight, the things are only five: namely, abiding in the four fruits, and the approach to the first fruit; for the approaches to the latter three fruits are not apart from the prior fruit.

Eight names; in fact, only five things. Why does eight fold into five? Because "the approaches to the latter three fruits are not apart from the prior fruit": the one approaching the second fruit is standing, underfoot, on the first; the one approaching the third stands on the second; the one approaching the fourth stands on the third. Only the approach to the first fruit has no fruit yet to stand on, and so counts as a thing of its own. Four fruits, plus the first approach: five.

This number speaks for itself. If approach and fruit were two independent things, eight would stay eight; there would be no folding into five. They fold precisely because each neighboring "prior fruit" and "next approach" are two faces of one place: the stair on which one stands firm is the very stair from which one lifts the next step. The stillness of the prior fruit is what the motion of the next approach rests on; moving and still interlock at every stair.

So the four pairs and eight persons are no ornamental doubling of four marks. They are the joints of this subtraction: wall after wall, each with its two phases—coming down, and down for good—linked one into the next, and only so does the road become walkable.

### For Those Long in the Approach

Setting approach and fruit as moving and still phases gives the walker one intimate benefit.

One who stays long in the approach, with no sign of the fruit, easily loses heart, feeling forever stuck at "not yet passing." Here is a steadying: you are standing inside the moving phase of a knot being severed. This is not failure; it is the in-progress phase that tearing down a wall inherently has—the wall is coming down, and its coming to rest will follow of itself.

And the sutta gives the one in the approach two standings of his own. One concerns rebirth. At another sitting in the same Jeta's Grove, the World-Honored One taught from the five aggregates, and of the one who has not yet obtained the fruit but ponders, examines, and accepts this teaching with wisdom—the faith-follower, the Dhamma-follower—He said something of great weight:

> He has risen clear, gone beyond the stage of the worldling; he has not yet obtained the fruit of stream-entry, and in the interval he does not die before he obtains the fruit of stream-entry.

Not yet obtained, already beyond the worldling's ground; the stream of his life will not break off before the fruit is reached. Even the approach to the first fruit—the one of the eight with no fruit yet underfoot—carries the canon's word: he *will* obtain. The other standing concerns the Saṅgha. Look back at the passage that opened this chapter: these four pairs, these eight persons, are the World-Honored One's community of disciples. Counted into the Saṅgha are not the four fruits only; the four approaches are counted too—and what the standing of the Saṅgha is, that same passage finishes saying: worthy of offerings, of honor, of veneration, an unsurpassed field of merit for the world. A person approaching the first fruit, the fruit not yet obtained, is already ranked within the Saṅgha-jewel that noble disciples recollect daily. That the canon grants such standing to one "still tearing down" is precisely because the approach is no waiting room: the moving phase itself already stands among the noble.

And the order runs one way only. "Abiding in the fruit, no longer called approaching"—once turned, there is no turning back. A wall down for good does not revert to coming down; as the last chapter said, what is severed is unconditioned, and the unconditioned does not regress. The hinge of approach and fruit is a hinge of motion-then-stillness, not a ring in which the two trade places. The one in the approach should know that the stillness ahead can bear his weight; the one on the fruit should know that the stair beneath him cannot be lost.

### How the Rest of This Book Reads

This pair of terms is the grammar of every station from here on.

From Part Two onward, every fruit is walked the same way: first its approach—which knots are being severed, and why these and not others come first; then its fruit—severed and abiding, and how that "no regress" is established. So with stream-entry; so with once-returning; so with non-returning.

This grammar will be tested head-on at one of the stations ahead. The definition of the second fruit contains the word "attenuated"—three fetters severed, and greed, hatred, and delusion *attenuated*. Attenuated sounds like a percentage, like "seventy percent torn down," as if it ran straight against this chapter's claim that establishment admits no seventy percent. Is it the exception to the two phases? We will look closely when we reach that station, in Part Three; here, only one sentence set down in advance: attenuation has its own reading as a severance—it is not half a fruit.

And one thing should be said in advance. The last station of this book, the arahant, is written only to the *approach*—to the five higher fetters being severed, the taints on the point of exhaustion, the person standing on the threshold—and there the pen stops. What lies inside the door—the freedom of mind and wisdom that opens once the taints are exhausted—belongs to another volume: this book's twin on the attainment axis, which writes precisely "after the arriving." Walking and arriving are the two faces of one threshold: this volume writes the walking-to; that one writes the having-arrived.

The ruler is in hand; the markings are read. Time to set out for the first wall.
