## 5.2 The Last Cut

Nine walls; eight are down.

Look back along the road. The first fruit: the first three fetters severed—the direction set right. The second: the desire realm's greed and anger thinned—six grades of nine. The third: the five lower fetters exhausted—out of the desire realm. This station: the five higher under the blade—the finest five, the knife already moving in the most hidden places. The subtraction has come to its final cut: the instant the last grade of the five higher fetters is severed.

This cut has a property none of the eight steps before it had. It is an end—the last wall down, nothing left to tear; and it is a beginning—the freedom that the exhaustion of taints opens unfolds from here. One instant: looking forward, "all torn down"; looking onward, "nothing in the way." This is a book of the walking, and writes only the first face. But precisely because both faces share this one instant, every sentence this chapter writes has another volume writing the other face at the very same place. First, finish this face.

### The Taints, and Their Root

What the last cut exhausts, the suttas call the taints. Their origin and their ending are stated cleanly in an exchange between two chief disciples. The Madhyama-āgama records: the Buddha was staying at Rājagaha, in the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove. The venerable Sāriputta rose in the late afternoon from his seclusion, went to the venerable Mahākoṭṭhita, exchanged greetings, sat down, and said: I would ask something—will you hear my questions? Koṭṭhita answered: ask what you will; I will hear and consider. So it went, topic by topic—how does one know the unwholesome, and its roots? The wholesome, and its roots? Nutriment, as it really is? And at each answer Sāriputta exclaimed: excellent, excellent, friend Koṭṭhita! When the questioning reached the taints:

> There are three taints: the taint of sensuality, the taint of being, the taint of ignorance—this is called knowing the taints as they really are. And how does one know the taints' arising as it really is? Because of ignorance there are taints—this is called knowing the taints' arising as it really is. And how does one know the taints' ceasing as it really is? When ignorance ceases, the taints cease—this is called knowing the taints' ceasing as it really is. And how does one know the way to the taints' ceasing as it really is? It is the noble eightfold path, right view through right concentration—this is called knowing the way to the taints' ceasing as it really is.

The taints are three: of sensuality, of being, of ignorance—the desire realm's seeping, the seeping of clinging to existence, the seeping of ignorance. Hold two clauses. "Because of ignorance there are taints"—ignorance is the taints' cause; this joins the last chapter exactly: ignorance is the old root of all the fetters. "When ignorance ceases, the taints cease"—the root gone, the taints go. So the last cut is "last" not because it stands tenth in a row, but because it cuts the root: the root severed, the residue of the nine has nothing left to stand on, and the taints end together. The "exhaustion" is therefore total—pulled up by the root, there is no ground left for sprouting. The "cessation that does not regress," argued the whole book through, here reaches its summit. And the way to the taints' ceasing holds no suspense in Koṭṭhita's answer: the noble eightfold path, right view through right concentration—still that one stream.

### That Passage

The standard scripture for the exhaustion of taints stands in the third of the three knowledges—the realization-knowledge of the taints' exhaustion. The Saṃyukta-āgama records: the Buddha, dwelling at Sāvatthī in Jeta's Grove, addressed the monks: there are three knowledges of the adept. What three? The adept's realization-knowledge of past lives, of passing-and-arising, of the taints' exhaustion. The knowledge of past lives—knowing one's own and beings' course through hundreds, thousands, millions of births; the knowledge of passing-and-arising—the purified divine eye seeing beings die and arise according to their deeds; and then, of the third:

> What is the realization-knowledge of the exhaustion of taints? The noble disciple knows as it really is: this is suffering; this is its arising; this is its cessation; this is the way to its cessation. Knowing thus, seeing thus, his mind is liberated from the taint of sensuality, liberated from the taint of being, liberated from the taint of ignorance; and in the knowledge of liberation he knows: "Birth is exhausted; the holy life has been lived; what was to be done is done; I myself know there is no further becoming." This is called the realization-knowledge of the exhaustion of taints.

Read the passage slowly. The four truths known as they are, seen as they are—then the three taints, one by one, the mind liberated from them. Then the four sentences rise: birth is exhausted; the holy life has been lived; what was to be done is done; there is no further becoming.

These four sentences the book quoted in its first chapter, when only their grammar was in view: exhausted, no further—the words of finishing. A whole book later, standing before them again, every word is now familiar. "Birth is exhausted"—the fuel of birth, the ten fetters, one by one, from the first three to the five higher, gone. "The holy life lived, what was to be done, done"—not the announcement of something newly gained, but of nothing left owing. "No further becoming"—the binding gone, rebirth without cause, becoming at an end. The whole declaration is a statement of accounts settled, not a list of acquisitions. It does not say, "I have attained vast radiance and freedom." It says: here, the account is closed.

The highest fruit's self-attestation keeps, to the last, the grammar of subtraction—the book's ruler showing itself one final and most thorough time. And in the discourse immediately following, someone happened to bring the other kind of ruler to compare. A brahmin came to the Buddha, sat down, and announced: this is the brahmin's threefold knowledge, this is the brahmin's threefold knowledge. The World-Honored One asked him: what is the brahmin's threefold knowledge? He answered: father and mother of flawless lineage, seven generations unblemished; the scriptures recited, the names of all things, their classes and divisions, mastered through and through; and a fine complexion. Blood, learning, looks—three columns, all of them entries of *having*. The World-Honored One answered him: "I do not call threefold knowledge what is so in name and word"—the true threefold knowledge of the noble Dhamma and discipline is the knowledge of past lives, of passing-and-arising, of the taints' exhaustion. Two rulers held face to face: one counts what has been gathered; one counts what has been ended. And at the top of the ruler that counts endings stand those four sentences.

### Not an Eleventh Thing

One more layer to see straight: the exhaustion of taints is not an "eleventh fruit" beyond the ten fetters.

The ten fetters exhausted *is* the exhaustion of taints—it is the name the one subtraction takes when it reaches "nothing left to tear," not a further object to be acquired. As with demolishing a house: when the last brick comes down, one says "demolished"; "demolished" is not an eleventh brick, but the name of the completed state in which the bricks are gone. And the sutta places it in exactly that position—virtue, concentration, wisdom fulfilled in sequence, "the wisdom-cleansed mind attains even liberation, exhausting the three taints": the exhaustion of taints stands at the end of the whole path, the path's completion, not one of its stations.

### The Threshold

Now stand before the instant itself.

The last cut settles—"the last wall is down." The same state has another description: "no wall remains in the way." Two descriptions, one looking back, one looking on; one and the same instant. Looking back is this book's eye: the subtraction complete, the ten fetters ended, the writing at its period. Looking on is another pair of eyes: no wall remaining, the liberation of mind and wisdom, the working of the three knowledges and six powers, unfolding from here unobstructed—and that is called mastery.

The subtraction's last step and freedom's first step are one and the same step.

That is the exact meaning of "threshold." A threshold is not a stretch of road between two things; it is a line, and the two sides of the line are the two faces of a single instant. Nine chapters have brought a person to this line—not a step short. What lies beyond the line, this book does not write a word of—not a step past.

Only one practical word should be said before the line: the freedom inside the door is not gained by going on to seek a freedom. To seek freedom as one seeks a thing is to tie one more knot. Tear the walls to their end, and the door opens of itself; there is no need, beyond the tearing, to seek a door. The practitioner's work, first to last, is one work—tearing walls. Where the walls end, the door opens unasked.

### The Book, Gathered

Nine chapters walked; count the pack once through.

One ruler: the fruits are measured by severance—counting what is gone. One pair of terms: approach and fruit—coming down, and down for good. Then four stations. The first fruit: direction—who walks, whether to walk, which road, three checkpoints passed, no-falling and seven returns built in. The second: by grades—thinned is not faded; six of nine severed for good. The third: across the realm—the five lower gathered and exhausted, no return to this world, the five attainments dividing only its timing. The fourth's approach: the five finest walls—embers and shadows, moved at the root by the perception of impermanence, down to the last cut.

Nine chapters, one meaning throughout: the four fruits of the renunciant's path are a measurable subtraction. From the severing of the first three fetters to the exhaustion of taints in which all ten are ended—wall after wall, torn until nothing is left to tear. It never asks what you have gathered; it asks what is gone. It does not weigh the brightness of experience; it counts the standing or falling of the fetters. For everyone on the road who has wondered, "have I actually made any progress"—this ruler is the one the canon itself hands down.

### Handed to the Next Volume

This book is written to the threshold.

The view inside the door—the liberation of mind and wisdom that the exhaustion of taints opens, the three knowledges, the six powers, the worthiness of offerings, the one beyond training—this book has not written, because that is not the walking-to; it is the having-arrived. The volume that writes the having-arrived is this book's twin on the attainment axis: it will open from this very passage—the same three taints liberated, the same four-sentence declaration—reading the other face: not "the last cut completes," but "freedom begins here."

Walking writes the going; attainment writes the arrival. One threshold, two volumes, each holding one face.

The last cut settles. This pen is set down here.

The door is ahead.
