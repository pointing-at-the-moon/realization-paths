## 2.1 What Stream Is Entered

Part One exchanged the question: not "what have I attained," but "which wall has come down." Now the question can be asked concretely: which walls come down first?

The sutta's answer we have already met: stream-entry severs three fetters—identity-view, doubt, and the grasping of rites and observances. But spread the ten fetters out, and this answer holds a surprise. Among the ten, in tightness of binding and violence of outbreak, craving and ill will stand far in front. For a beginner, the most intimate suffering is greed and anger: what is loved cannot be put down; what galls cannot be pressed down. By common sense, a subtraction should demolish the heaviest first—craving and ill will should fall before all. Yet the canon places them later, and what falls first are three that look far more "bookish": a view, a hesitation, a clutching.

Why?

First, watch how the sutta describes the person in whom these three have fallen. The Saṃyukta-āgama says:

> This is called the fruit of stream-entry: not falling to the lower destinies, definitely set toward right awakening, seven existences among gods and humans, and then the final end of suffering.

Weigh the words one by one: *not falling*, *set toward*, *the end of suffering*. Not one word speaks of how high a state; every word speaks of direction—the downward road sealed, the bearing toward the end fixed, the journey certain to finish. The sutta describes the first fruit in the language of direction. That is the clue: the three fetters this station severs are the ones that govern direction.

### Three Fetters, Three Questions of Direction

Stand the three fetters up one by one, and they show themselves a single cluster.

Identity-view is the view that takes this body-and-mind of five aggregates as "I." What it blocks is *who is walking*. If practice sets out from "I want liberation," and that "I" is itself a misidentification, then the road's point of aim is off from the very first step—like an arrow aimed at a mirage. Until identity-view breaks, the fiercest effort only makes offerings to a center that does not exist. It does not block some stretch of the road; it blocks the road's point of origin.

Doubt is the root hesitation toward the Buddha, the Dharma, the Saṅgha, and this path. What it blocks is *whether one walks at all*. Doubt is not a blank in knowledge—what is not known can be learned; doubt is a gap in trust—uncertainty whether this road leads anywhere, whether it is worth walking. A person carrying that gap either does not set out, or looks back at every third step. He cannot enter the stream, because he does not believe there is a stream to enter.

The grasping of rites and observances is the clutching of precepts, austerities, and rituals that have nothing to do with liberation, in the belief that they alone purify. What it blocks is *which road is walked*. The rite-grasper is not unwilling to walk—often he walks with tremendous effort; he walks wrong, taking a road that does not lead to liberation for the road that does, and the harder he strives, the further he strays.

Who is walking; whether one walks; which road is walked. The three fetters are exactly the three checkpoints of *setting out rightly*: the subject must be true, the departure resolved, the road discerned. Until these three are passed, "how far along" is not even the question—one has not aimed, not moved, or not found the road. That is why they are one cluster, and why that cluster falls first: direction is the precondition of any journey.

### The Fetters That Hold You in Place, and the Fetters That Drag You Back

The treatise masters press this one layer further. The Abhidharmakośa counts the five lower fetters—identity-view, rite-grasping, doubt, craving, ill will—and, having counted, marks the division of labor: the latter two, craving and ill will, keep a being from rising out of the desire realm—fetters that hold one in place. The former three are fiercer: "even should one rise out, by the former three one is brought back down"—even if a person climbs out for a time, while the first three are unsevered, they pull him back. The master gives a simile: like jailers guarding a prison, patrolling the walls—the prisoner may scale them for a moment, and is marched back in.

And the master adds one sentence that says outright why these three fall first:

> Deluded as to the path and doubting the path, they obstruct the trending toward liberation; hence only the severance of three is taught.

Deluded as to the path—the grasping of rites, taking the wrong road. Doubting the path—doubt itself. Add identity-view's misidentified walker, and the three obstruct precisely the *trending toward liberation*—the setting-out itself. Hence the definition of stream-entry "teaches only the severance of three": not because these three are heaviest, but because these three guard the road.

This layer comes from the treasury; remember its station as you read. The sutta establishes the three fetters and the marks of direction; the treatise lays bare the mechanism of "why these three." The sutta gives the bones, the treatise the sinews—one and the same meaning.

### The Buddha Examines Sāriputta: What Is the Stream?

If direction is the whole of this station, then the name "stream-entry" cannot be passed over lightly. Entered into—what stream, exactly?

While the Buddha was dwelling at Sāvatthī, in Jeta's Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika's park, He examined the Venerable Sāriputta on precisely this. The World-Honored One asked: that which is called the stream—what is the stream? Sāriputta answered:

> The stream of which the World-Honored One speaks is the noble eightfold path.

The World-Honored One asked again: the factors of stream-entry—the conditions by which one comes into this stream—what are they? Sāriputta answered: there are four—drawing near to good people, hearing the true Dharma, right attention within, and practice in accordance with the Dharma. A third time He asked: one who has entered the stream—how many things does he possess? Sāriputta answered: four—unshakable confidence in the Buddha, unshakable confidence in the Dharma, unshakable confidence in the Saṅgha, and the noble virtues fulfilled. Three questions, three answers; the World-Honored One sealed each one: it is as you have said.

This exchange nails "stream-entry" down. The stream is no mystical current of states; the stream is the noble eightfold path—right view through right concentration, the path itself. To enter the stream is to enter the current of the right path. The on-ramps are four: drawing near the good, hearing the Dharma, attending rightly, walking accordingly. And one who has entered carries four marks: confidence in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṅgha that no longer shakes, and the noble virtues fulfilled. Not one item is the depth of absorption; not one is the brightness of experience.

### With the Current, Not Without Waves

The image of the stream is exact in one more place: it says the direction is settled, not that the water is calm.

One who has entered the right current is carried by the water toward the lower reaches. He paddles fast or slow; the surface rises and falls; but the current does not reverse, and the river's mouth is settled. The sutta says this itself, describing the stream-enterer:

> Though grief and pain arise for that noble disciple in the meantime … one does not see in that noble disciple a single thing unsevered that could make him take birth back in this world.

Though grief and pain arise in the meantime—the stream-enterer is not henceforth without sorrow. He still grieves, still suffers; the waves of greed and anger still cross his days. But search him through, and no fetter is found still standing that is strong enough to haul him back upstream. Grief is experience; it rises and falls. Direction is severance; it stands and does not move. The sutta keeps the two layers perfectly apart: the fruit lies not in experience free of sorrow, but in direction free of reversal.

### Three Things to See Clearly Here

First, the sotāpanna is not "considerably advanced"; he is "rightly directed." A person whose concentration is dry and whose experience is plain, but in whom identity-view, doubt, and rite-grasping can never return, has entered the stream. A person of deep absorption and great radiance who still makes the false "I" his center, still looks back at the path every third step, still clutches fruitless observances, has not. State and fruit part company most plainly at this station.

Second, falling first does not mean lighter. The first three fetters are the jailers—their weight is immense; and craving and ill will, standing later, are not one bit less important. The order is structural—first set the direction, then walk the distance—not a ranking of gravity.

Third, severing identity-view is not the attainment of some sublime experience of "no-self." To think so is to circle back to measuring fruit by experience. Severing identity-view is the ceasing of a misidentification: no longer taking the falsely grasped "I" as the center of practice, seeing in accordance with fact that in the five aggregates there is no self and nothing belonging to self. Likewise, severing doubt is not the arising of some feeling of conviction, but the ceasing of hesitation; severing rite-grasping is not a summit experience of the true path, but the ceasing of taking the wrong one. All three severances are subtraction—the ceasing of a wrong direction—not addition, not some new state in hand.

### After Direction

This chapter in one sentence: the subtraction's first wall takes down the possibility of walking the wrong road. Who walks—the subject set true; whether to walk—the departure resolved; which road—the way discerned. The direction once right, the person is in the stream, moving with the current.

To the one who has entered, the sutta hands two exceedingly weighty sentences: no falling to the lower destinies; at most seven returns among gods and humans, then the end of suffering. They sound like two certificates—an insurance policy against falling, and a promise of arrival within seven. Who exactly issues these guarantees? Or are they not guarantees at all? The next chapter takes the two sentences apart.
