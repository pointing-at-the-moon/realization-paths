## 3.1 Thinned Is Not Faded

First, set the second fruit's definition back into its file. The Saṃyukta-āgama counts the sequence of the fruits:

> The three fetters exhausted: the fruit of stream-entry is obtained. The three fetters exhausted, and greed, hatred, and delusion attenuated: the fruit of once-returning is obtained. The five lower fetters exhausted: the fruit of non-returning is obtained.

The definition of the sakadāgāmin comes in two parts. The first part, "the three fetters exhausted"—the stream-enterer's severance, inherited whole, not a grain less. The second part, "greed, hatred, and delusion attenuated"—this is what the once-returner's station newly adds. So "attenuated" does not replace "severed"; it is a further operation laid upon the foundation of the three fetters already wholly severed.

The trouble lies in the operation's name. Thinned.

Up to here, the book's ruler has carried one kind of marking only: severed. A wall stands, or is down for good; established, or not established; Part One said it without softening—establishment admits no seventy percent. But the word "thinned" sounds like exactly seventy percent: greed remains, only fainter; anger remains, only lighter. If that were so, "thinned" would be a degree, an impression—and impressions are precisely what the Bamboo Grove case has been ruling out since the beginning. A fruit measured in shades would split the whole ruler down the middle.

There is only one way to hold the ruler: go inside the word and find its exact content.

### One Slab, or Nine Grades

The key is a structural fact: the desire realm's greed, hatred, and delusion are not one indivisible slab.

The treasury divides them with complete clarity: nine grades. From the coarsest, top-of-top, to the subtlest, bottom-of-bottom—top-top, top-middle, top-bottom; middle-top, middle-middle, middle-bottom; bottom-top, bottom-middle, bottom-bottom. Nine. The division belongs to the Abhidharma: the sutta establishes the *name* "thinned"; the treatise does the *arithmetic*. The Abhidharmakośa settles the account in one passage:

> The stream-enterer, advancing, severs the desire realm's cultivation-defilements from the first grade up to the fifth, and is then to be known by the changed name "approaching the fruit of once-returning." When he severs the sixth, he becomes a once-returner … This is also called "greed, hatred, and delusion attenuated," because only the lower-grade greed, hatred, and delusion remain.

Read it clause by clause. The stream-enterer presses on against the desire realm's greed and hatred—severs the first grade, the second … to the fifth; along this stretch he is named "approaching the once-returner's fruit"—still cutting. Severing the sixth, he becomes a once-returner. Then the decisive clause: why is this position called "greed, hatred, and delusion attenuated"? "Because only the lower-grade greed, hatred, and delusion remain"—of the nine grades, the upper six are severed; only the lower three, the "lower grade," are left.

The exact content of "thinned" now stands plain: not "greed and anger have faded as a whole," but "of nine grades, six are permanently severed; only the weakest three remain." Thinned is a count—six of nine gone; not a felt magnitude—faded by so many shades.

A homely comparison. A string of nine lamps: six put out, three still burning. You would not say "the string has dimmed by sixty-seven percent"—that is the language of impression. You would say "of nine lamps, six are out; three burn"—that is the language of counting. Putting out one lamp is an all-or-nothing event: that lamp burns, or is out; there is no lamp "seventy percent extinguished." Thinned says how many lamps are out, not how dim the string looks.

### The Six Parts Taken Down Are Truly Down

One layer deeper, and "thinned" parts company with "weakened" for good.

The six grades severed—their severance is the same severance as the stream-enterer's severing of the three fetters: cessation through discernment, true severance, never to arise again upon meeting conditions. When the first grade is severed, that grade's greed and anger are extinguished for good—not pressed low by concentration, to surge back when one rises from the cushion and meets the world. Grade by grade to the sixth, every severance is that clean. And the remaining lower three grades are truly unsevered—fetters untouched, left for the next station to gather.

Set the two kinds of "thinning" side by side and the difference shows itself. One is weakening of the whole: the slab of greed and anger still stands entire, only its intensity pressed down—a thinning that breaks at the first provocation, faint in meditation, thick again outside it, with not one grade truly severed. The other is partial total-severance: the upper six grades truly extinguished, the lower three untouched—what is severed, wholly severed; what is unsevered, wholly present; the boundary clean. The canon's "thinned" is the latter. The wall is not half-fallen; it is a wall of nine parts, six permanently taken down, three still standing. "Six parts down" is a counted fact; "the wall looks fainter" is an impression. The two have nothing to do with each other.

So "thinned," far from breaching the subtraction, is its most refined stroke: it shows that the blade of "severance" can land on a unit finer than "one fetter"—the grade—and that every grade's severance is still the true, irreversible kind. Part One's sentence stands untouched: establishment admits no seventy percent. Only now it is seen more finely: the unit of establishment can be as fine as one grade; and in a nine-grade wall, the fall of each grade still admits no seventy percent.

### "Once-Returning": the Reduction's Shadow on Rebirth

The second fruit's name: once-returning—once up among the gods, once back among humans, and there final nibbāna. The treatise continues from the same passage: "He goes to the heavens and comes once to the human world, and attains final nibbāna; he is named the fruit of once-returning, because beyond this there is no further birth."

Where does this "once" come from? The reasoning runs in one line with the last station's "seven returns": the number of rebirths is a corollary of severance, not an allotment of grace. The force that binds one to rebirth in the desire realm is tied to the desire realm's greed, hatred, and delusion. The stream-enterer, with all nine grades unsevered, has a journey-ceiling of at most seven returns. The once-returner, six of nine severed, finds the binding force greatly reduced, and the ceiling tightens with it—one return. The strength of the remaining three grades suffices to draw one return only; that return walked, "beyond this there is no further birth."

Line the three stations up and the scale's logic shows itself: nine grades unsevered—at most seven returns; six severed, three remaining—one return; all nine exhausted—the next station's affair. As the count of severed grades rises, the ceiling of rebirths falls. "Once-returning" is the shadow of "thinned" cast on the ledger of births: the "thinned" in the definition (six grades gone) and the "once" in the name (strength left for one return) are two faces of a single reduction. The transition's question—whether the "one" in the name and the "thinned" in the definition are the same thing—has its answer: they are.

And the grammar of Part One runs here as usual—indeed, with uncommon clarity. Severing the first through the fifth grade is the once-returner's *approach*—the treatise says in so many words, "to be known by the changed name 'approaching the fruit'"—still cutting. Severing the sixth is the *fruit*—down for good, and abiding. Even the approach's mileage, the treasury marks out to the grade.

### Three Things to See Clearly Here

First, "thinned" is not faded experience. "My greed and anger are lighter than they were"—everyone says it, and it is often true; but it is a report of experience: it can be wrong, and it can reverse—off the cushion, meeting conditions, the thinness can thicken again. The fruit's "thinned" has a far stricter criterion: not "how much lighter do I feel," but "of the nine grades, is there one truly extinguished for good, never to return upon meeting conditions." A faded feeling is not the fruit; one grade truly severed is.

Second, the sakadāgāmin is not "halfway to anāgāmin." "The once-returner stands between the first fruit and the third" invites the image of a midpoint on a continuous slider—a halfway house. In truth it is a determinate position of reduction: six of nine permanently severed, the boundary clean. Its difference from the non-returner is "three grades remaining" against "all nine exhausted"—a discrete difference, countable; not "faint" against "fainter"—a difference of degree, only guessable.

Third, the "lower grade" of "only the lower grade remains" is not "too slight to count." The lower three, though weakest of the nine, are real unsevered fetters, still binding to the desire realm, still drawing rebirth—else why "one return" at all? Subtraction does not recognize "almost": unsevered is unsevered. The once-returner's unfinished business is truly unfinished, not "as good as done."

### The Ruler, Held

Look back at the battle this chapter fought. The word "thinned" came on hard, as if to tear a degree-shaped gash in the ruler of subtraction. Walked into, it tore nothing; it ground the ruler's markings one level finer—from the fetter down to the grade. What is measured is still what is gone: six grades gone. Establishment still admits no seventy percent: each grade's extinction, all or nothing. And the image of the wall has held: not a half-fallen wall, but a wall of nine parts, six permanently down.

The three parts still standing are the last roots of the desire realm's greed and anger. The next station takes them out, roots and all—and that station's name is the non-returner.
