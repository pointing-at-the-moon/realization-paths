# Part Three · The Sakadāgāmin · The Subtraction's Finest Cut

This part has a single chapter, and it must answer the finest-grained question in the book.

The definition of the second fruit: the three fetters severed, and greed, hatred, and delusion *attenuated*. The first half is familiar—inherited from stream-entry. The second half is strange—attenuated. Not severed: thinned. Greed remains, only thinner.

The transition has already laid the difficulty bare: this book's ruler has measured nothing but "severed"—a wall stands or is down for good; establishment admits no seventy percent. Yet "attenuated" sounds like exactly seventy percent—like a wall taken down halfway, like a degree measured in shades. If a fruit could truly be defined by "how much it has faded," the whole ruler raised in the first two parts would split down the middle.

So this chapter is a defense—of subtraction itself. And the defense is not to walk around the word "attenuated," but to walk straight into it and see its exact content. Seen clearly, it turns out that thinning is no exception to the subtraction; it is the subtraction's most refined stroke. The key hides in one overlooked structural fact: greed, hatred, and delusion are not a single slab.

Before going in, a scene is waiting: the full-moon uposatha night of the rains retreat at Jeta's Grove, the World-Honored One seated before the great assembly, calling the roll of the noble ones in its midst—and the once-returners are sitting in that night's assembly.

One chapter, one key. Go in.
