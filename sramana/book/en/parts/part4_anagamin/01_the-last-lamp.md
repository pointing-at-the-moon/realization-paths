## 4.1 The Last Lamp

On paper, this station is simple: the last station put out six lamps; this one puts out the remaining three—the seventh, eighth, and ninth grades. Together with what fell long ago—identity-view, rite-grasping, doubt—the five lower fetters are, at that point, exhausted without remainder.

And the station's epithet was settled in an exchange of the homeliest kind.

### An Old Friend at Nālanda Village

The Saṃyukta-āgama records: the Buddha was dwelling at Nālanda village in Magadha, and the venerable Sāriputta was there as well. A wanderer of another path named Jambukhādaka—the sutta sets down a particular line: he was "an old good friend of Sāriputta," an acquaintance from before the going-forth—came to see him, exchanged greetings, sat to one side, and opened with: in the noble Dhamma and discipline, what is hard? Sāriputta answered: going forth is hard. What is hard about going forth? Delighting in it is hard—to have gone forth, and to love the life, is hard. What is hard about delighting? Delighting in the constant cultivation of good is hard. At the end of an exchange, the sutta sets down one line: "Then the two upright men, having discussed together, each rose from his seat and departed." Another day he came again, asked again, departed again—and so back and forth, some forty rounds. What is nibbāna? Greed forever exhausted, hatred forever exhausted, delusion forever exhausted. What is "gaining relief"? The three fetters exhausted and known. Station by station the questions climbed, until they reached this station's name:

> Jambukhādaka asked Sāriputta: "That which is called coolness—what is coolness?" Sāriputta said: "Coolness is the exhaustion of the five lower fetters: identity-view, the grasping of rites, doubt, sensual greed, and ill will."

Coolness. The name carries the grammar of subtraction with it. It does not speak of gaining some cool sensation; it speaks of the desire realm's fever—the fire of greed and anger—gone out. Less burning, therefore cool. Even the fruit's epithet is built in the syntax of "what is gone." And each time Jambukhādaka asked after a name, he pressed one question more: is there a path, a course, by whose cultivation one reaches it? Forty rounds, and Sāriputta's answer never varied: "There is. The noble eightfold path—right view through right concentration." The old friend asked his way through the whole of the noble Dhamma, and the map that came back had many station-names and a single road—the very stream Part Two fixed.

But if this station were only "three more lamps," the canon would not have given it a new name. What is worth stopping for is the sentence that follows—one neither earlier fruit received. The Saṃyukta-āgama:

> Having severed these five lower fetters, one attains final nibbāna upon rebirth there: an anāgāmin, not returning to this world.

Not returning to this world. The Madhyama-āgama marks the same: "Born in that place, one there attains final nibbāna, gains the state of no regress, and does not return to this world." Born *there*—in the form realm—one finishes there, and does not come back to this desire realm.

Set the three stations' sentences side by side, and the weight of this one shows. The stream-enterer: not falling to the lower destinies—what is sealed are the desire realm's *bad* destinations; he is still reborn in its good ones, gods and humans, at most seven times. The once-returner: one return—what is reduced is the *number* of rebirths within the desire realm; he still comes back, once. Both stations move structures *inside* the desire realm: the destination raised, the count reduced. The non-returner's "no return" moves the desire realm itself: not even its good destinations receive him again; rebirth has moved outside the boundary. "Not falling" and "not returning"—one word apart, and the difference is within-the-realm against across-it.

From "how many times remain" to "no longer here"—this is not the arithmetic line of seven, one, zero. It is a change of realm.

### Why the Ninth Grade

The question follows at once: severing a grade is severing a grade—why does the sixth (making a once-returner) merely tighten seven returns to one, while the ninth puts a person outside the realm? What gives the last grade such force?

The reasoning is the one Part Two laid down: rebirth needs an agent—a fetter still standing, strong enough to pull. The desire realm's chief pull on rebirth is sensual greed and ill will. Severed to the sixth grade, that force has three grades left—a weak residue, but real fetters still, strong enough to draw one return; so the once-returner must come back once. Severed to the ninth, the lower three are gone too: greed and ill will wholly severed, the five lower fetters gathered to completion—and the force binding one to the desire realm falls to zero. The force at zero, "rebirth in the desire realm" is not forbidden; it has lost its agent, and cannot occur. The treatise fixes the position in one sentence:

> If he severs the ninth, he becomes the fruit of non-returning, for he must not return to be reborn in the desire realm. This is also called "the five lower fetters severed" … because at this point they are severed in full assembly.

Severing the ninth grade makes the non-returner, who necessarily is not reborn in the desire realm; and the position is also named "the five lower fetters severed"—the first three fell back at stream-entry, greed and anger fell grade by grade, and at the ninth the five are gathered and done, so the collective name names the place. This passage is the treasury's fine ruling; the stations stand as usual: the sutta marks the *mark*—no return; the treatise does the *arithmetic*—the ninth grade.

And here one sees a temperament of subtraction: it is not linear. Not every severed grade buys an equal reduction of births. The first eight grades' severance is the desire realm's binding in *decrement*—rebirth tightening from seven returns to one. The ninth grade's severance is that binding at *zero*—rebirth leaping from "a bounded number within the realm" to "no return to this realm." The same single grade, landing in a different place, with force worlds apart: the last grade falls on a threshold.

The lamps of the last station can take one more step here. Nine lamps, put out to the sixth: the room is mostly dark, but the person is still in the room—one more trip back to make. Put out to the ninth: the desire realm's dark is wholly quenched—and the person is not standing in a darker room; the person has left the room. The last lamp's going out is not one shade less of dark. It is the way out.

### Crossed, and on the Far Shore

For this crossing, the sutta keeps an image of uncommon exactness. The Madhyama-āgama records: the Buddha, staying at Sāvatthī in Jeta's Grove, told the monks—I will teach you the seven persons in the water; listen closely, attend well. First He laid the seven out like a riddle: one stays sunk in the water; one surfaces and sinks again; one surfaces and stands; one stands and observes … The monks, having heard, asked Him to unfold it: "The World-Honored One is the root of the Dhamma, the World-Honored One is the master of the Dhamma; the Dhamma comes from the World-Honored One—may He alone teach it." And the World-Honored One untied them one by one: the one who stays sunk is the person covered over by unwholesome states; the one who surfaces and sinks again is the person who gains faith in good states and loses it; and so up the line, to the fourth—who knows suffering as it really is, and the three fetters fall: the stream-enterer too has his place in the water. And the sixth person figures the non-returner:

> This is the person who, having emerged, stands; having stood, observes; having observed, crosses; having crossed, reaches the far shore.

Emerging, standing firm, sighting the shore, crossing over, reaching the far bank. The earlier persons in the simile float or sink, surface and go under again—all still in the water, still in the desire realm's current. The sixth has crossed and landed, clear of the current itself. The image's precision lies here: it renders "no return to the desire realm" as a crossing in space—not swimming farther in the water (quantity, the business of the last two stations), but getting out of the water (kind, the business of this one). And the simile holds a seventh person—who, having reached the far shore, stands upon it: the one whose taints are exhausted, the view outside this book's last threshold. One picture of water, and the whole book's stations are drawn into it.

Part One's grammar runs here as usual: the approach of non-return is the moving phase—the seventh through ninth grades being severed, still mid-crossing; the fruit of non-return is the still phase—the ninth severed, the five lower fetters gathered and done: arrived on the far shore.

### Three Things to See Clearly Here

First, "not returning" is not "not falling" said twice. Not falling seals the bad destinations *within* the desire realm—the person is still on this bank. Not returning crosses the desire realm itself—the person has landed on the far one. One word apart: within the realm, across it.

Second, the anāgāmin is not "a sakadāgāmin with three more grades." By count it is the same ledger continued, but the ninth grade falls on a threshold—what it severs is not merely one grade, but the last residue of the desire realm's binding. Read this station as "a bit more severing," and the subtraction's non-linearity is lost: quantity, accumulated to the critical point, becomes kind.

Third, crossing the desire realm's wall is not crossing every wall. The non-returner's five higher fetters—craving for form, craving for the formless, conceit, restlessness, ignorance—stand unsevered; he has crossed the desire realm, and the bindings of the form and formless realms remain. "Reaching the far shore" is to have crossed the desire realm's current, not to have entered the remainderless. The one who has landed must still walk on. Do not take the anāgāmin for the end.

### After the Crossing

This chapter writes up to the threshold: the five lower fetters gathered and exhausted, the desire realm's binding at zero, the person not returning to this world. But just past the threshold rises a question none of the three earlier stations ever raised—

One who does not return to the desire realm: where does he go?

The sutta's answer is a destination that has not appeared before: the Pure Abodes. And more curious still, the canon divides "how one attains final nibbāna there" with the greatest care—five gaits. One severance, five arrivals. The next chapter looks at the five.
