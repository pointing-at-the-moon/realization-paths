# Entering the Stream

**Why the First Three Fetters Are the Severance of Orientation**

*中文主題：入流——前三結何以是定向之斷*

## Abstract

The first two papers established the master-thesis of subtraction (fruit measured by severance) and its jointing grammar (the approach-fruit hinge). This paper opens the fruit-by-fruit walk: the stream-enterer severs the first three fetters — identity-view, rite-and-ritual-grasping, and doubt. The question is not the *fact* that three fetters fall, but *why these three fall first*. The thesis: the first three are a cluster of *orientation-fetters* — identity-view misidentifies the subject of the journey, doubt blocks whether one sets out at all, and rite-grasping mistakes which road to travel; what they obstruct is not the sensual greed-and-aversion of the desire-realm (those are the latter two lower fetters) but the very capacity to *orient correctly toward liberation*. The *Abhidharmakośa* glosses exactly this: "deluded as to the path and doubting the path, they obstruct the trending toward liberation; hence only the severance of three is taught" — and adds that even one who momentarily rises above the desire-realm is "dragged back down by the first three, like jailers patrolling against escapees." To sever these three is to establish orientation; the stream-current image of "entering the stream" (the stream is the eightfold path; four factors of stream-entry; four unshakable confidences) depicts precisely the mark of, once orientation is set, "moving with the current, no longer reversible." The paper closes through the approach-fruit grammar: the approach of stream-entry is the first three fetters being severed (orientation in progress); the fruit is the first three severed and abiding (orientation established, flowing without reversal). Conclusion: the stream-enterer is not "how far one has practised" but "the direction is now right, never again to go astray" — the subtraction's first wall takes down *the possibility of taking the wrong road.*

**Keywords**: sotāpanna, stream-entry, first three fetters, identity-view, rite-and-ritual-grasping, doubt, eightfold path, four unshakable confidences, orientation, Abhidharmakośa, subtraction

## I. The Question

"Have I reached stream-entry?" — by the lights of the first two papers, the question must be rewritten. It does not ask "how much have I amassed" (experience is the illusion of accumulation), but "which wall has come down here" (severance is the real measure of subtraction). What the stream-enterer takes down is the first three fetters: identity-view, rite-and-ritual-grasping, and doubt. This paper's question runs one layer deeper than "three fetters severed": **why do these three fall first?**

The ten fetters have an order: five lower (identity-view, rite-grasping, doubt, sensual greed, ill-will) and five higher (greed for form, greed for the formless, conceit, restlessness, ignorance). Since subtraction takes down walls one at a time, the demolition has a sequence. What gives one pause is this: the three walls the first fruit takes down are not the three *heaviest*. Of the desire-realm defilements, sensual greed and ill-will are far grosser than identity-view, doubt, and rite-grasping — the suffering most palpable to a beginner is craving and aversion, not "I still have doubts about nibbāna." Were subtraction a matter of "take down the heaviest first," sensual greed and ill-will should fall first. Why does the canon instead have the seemingly more "speculative" first three fall first?

The thesis: the first three fall first not because they are light, but because what they obstruct is *setting out itself*. They are a cluster of *orientation-fetters* — obstructing not "how far one travels" but "whether the direction is right, whether one can depart at all." Identity-view misidentifies the subject of the walking (who practises?), doubt keeps one from daring or deciding to set out (does this road lead anywhere?), rite-grasping takes the wrong road (how should one walk?). Together they are precisely the three obstructions to "orienting correctly toward liberation"; while they stand, the fiercest exertion is only force applied in the wrong direction, or never setting out at all. To sever them is to calibrate the direction — and once direction is right, the stream-current image of "entering the stream" follows: one enters the proper current, moves with the flow, and though there be ups and downs, no longer reverses.

This is the ground of the stream-enterer's "not falling to lower destinies, bound for nibbāna": not that his reservoir of merit is full, but that his *bearing* has been permanently calibrated. The subtraction's first wall takes down not some gross affliction, but *the possibility of taking the wrong road itself*. The paper argues accordingly: Section II presents the scriptural basis (the listing of the first three, the stream-enterer's marks, the stream-current gloss); Section III, the core argument, establishes in three layers why the first three are the severance of orientation (the orientation-structure of the three; the *Kośa*'s "they obstruct the trending toward liberation, hence only three are taught" as direct proof; the correspondence of the stream-image with orientation); Section IV again omits convergence; Section V offers corrections; Section VI concludes.

## II. The Canonical Basis

### 2.1 The stream-enterer severs three fetters; his mark is non-falling and at most seven returns

The stream-enterer is defined by the severance of three fetters — the four-fruit definition of the *Saṃyukta-āgama*, fascicle 29, was cited in Paper 1. His *mark* the *Saṃyukta-āgama* fixes in fascicle 2 (sūtra 61):

> 比丘！是名須陀洹果，不墮惡道，必定正趣三菩提，七有天人往生，然後究竟苦邊。[^1]
>
> *"Monks! This is called the fruit of the stream-enterer: not falling to lower destinies, fixedly trending toward awakening, taking birth among gods and humans at most seven times, and thereafter making an end of suffering."*

Three fetters exhausted is the stream-enterer; his mark is threefold: not falling to lower destinies, fixedly trending toward awakening, making an end of suffering within at most seven returns. Note that all three are *directional* descriptions — "not falling," "fixedly trending," "making an end" speak not of the altitude of a state but of a *bearing now settled*.

Fascicle 15 (sūtra 396) develops it with the sunrise simile, and marks the crucial point: the stream-enterer may still feel grief and pain along the way, yet does not regress:

> 此三結盡，名須陀洹，不墮惡趣法，必定正覺……彼聖弟子中間雖起憂苦……不見彼聖弟子有一法不斷，能令還生此世者。[^2]
>
> *"With these three fetters exhausted, one is called a stream-enterer, of the nature not to fall to bad destinies, certain of awakening… Though that noble disciple may, in the meantime, give rise to grief and pain… one does not see in that noble disciple a single thing unsevered capable of making him take birth back in this world."*

The clause "may, in the meantime, give rise to grief and pain" is vital: the stream-enterer is not thereafter without sorrow or distress (that would be the experiential misreading); he still grieves, still suffers. But "no single thing unsevered remains capable of making him take birth back in this world" — no fetter lingers strong enough to pull him back upstream. Grief and pain are experience: they rise and fall. Orientation is severance: established, it does not regress. The canon keeps the two cleanly apart: the fruit lies not in the absence of grief but in the irreversibility of orientation.

### 2.2 The stream-current image: the stream *is* the eightfold path

The name "entering the stream" is no loose metaphor but has an exact gloss. *Saṃyukta-āgama*, fascicle 30 (sūtra 843), in dialogue between the Buddha and Sāriputta:

> 〔佛問：〕所謂流者，何等為流？舍利弗白佛言：世尊所說流者，謂八聖道。〔復問入流分，答有四種：〕謂親近善男子、聽正法、內正思惟、法次法向。〔四分成就入流者：〕謂於佛不壞淨、於法不壞淨、於僧不壞淨、聖戒成就。[^3]
>
> *"[The Buddha asked:] As to 'the stream' — what is the stream? Sāriputta said: The stream the Blessed One speaks of is the eightfold noble path. [Asked about the factors of stream-entry, he answered there are four:] associating with good persons, hearing the true Dharma, wise attention, and practice in accordance with the Dharma. [The four by which one is accomplished as a stream-enterer:] unshakable confidence in the Buddha, in the Dharma, in the Saṅgha, and accomplishment in noble virtue."*

Three things are anchored here. First, *the stream is the eightfold path* — the "stream" one enters is not some mystical state but the right path itself; to enter the stream is to "enter the current of the right path." Second, *there are four factors of stream-entry* (associating with the good, hearing the Dharma, wise attention, practice in accordance) — the four conditions for entering the current. Third, *the marks of one accomplished as a stream-enterer are four* (the four unshakable confidences: unshakable faith in Buddha, Dharma, Saṅgha, plus accomplishment in noble virtue). Taken together, "entering the stream" depicts exactly "having entered the right path, the bearing now settled" — fully consonant with the marks of 2.1.

### 2.3 The treatises gloss "stream" and "stream-enterer": the uncontaminated path as cause, trending to nibbāna

The treatises pin "stream" to the uncontaminated path. *Abhidharmakośa*, fascicle 23:

> 諸無漏道總名為流，由此為因趣涅槃故。預言為顯最初至得，彼預流故說名預流。[^4]
>
> *"The uncontaminated paths together are called 'the stream,' since by this as cause one trends toward nibbāna. 'Fore-' [in 'fore-stream-enterer'] indicates the first attainment; entering this stream first, one is therefore called a stream-enterer."*

"Stream = uncontaminated path, by which one trends to nibbāna" — treatise consonant with sūtra (2.2, "the stream is the eightfold path"): the stream is the uncontaminated portion of the right path. "Fore-" marks the first entry into this stream; the first to enter the uncontaminated current of the right path is therefore the "fore-stream-enterer." The treatises thereby fix the stream-enterer as one "certainly trending to nibbāna" — not for amount amassed, but for having entered the proper current.

> ⚠️ A note on register: the listing of the first three and the stream-enterer's marks (2.1), and the stream-current gloss (2.2), are stated outright in the Āgamas; the treatises' gloss of "stream = uncontaminated path, stream-enterer = first attainment" (2.3) is Abhidharma's elaboration, consonant with scripture but finer, drawn on here with its register as treatise marked.

## III. The Core Argument

### 3.1 The orientation-structure of the first three: what they obstruct is "setting out," not "travelling far"

Why do the first three form a cluster? Because what they obstruct is one and the same — all obstruct "setting out correctly." Taken one by one:

**Identity-view (sakkāya-diṭṭhi):** grasping a "self" within the five aggregates. As an obstruction to orientation, it *misidentifies the subject of the walking*. If practice takes "I want liberation" as its starting point, while that "I" is a deluded grasping, then the entire path is mis-aimed from the outset — like an arrow aimed at a mirage. Until identity-view is broken, all exertion serves a non-existent centre. It obstructs not some stretch of road but the road's *point of origin*.

**Doubt (vicikicchā):** wavering about the Buddha, Dharma, Saṅgha, the four truths, the path. As an obstruction to orientation, it *keeps one from daring or deciding to set out*. Doubt is not a blank of knowledge (not knowing one may train) but a gap of trust (uncertain whether it is worth walking, whether the road leads through). One who fundamentally doubts the path either never departs or turns back every three steps — he cannot "enter the stream," for he does not believe there is a stream to enter.

**Rite-and-ritual-grasping (sīlabbata-parāmāsa):** taking the non-path for the path, mistaking useless observances, austerities, and rituals for the cause of liberation. As an obstruction to orientation, it *takes the wrong road*. The rite-grasper is not unwilling to walk but walks *wrongly* — mistaking a road that does not lead to liberation for the road of liberation, deviating further the harder he strives. It obstructs the *discernment of the path as path*.

Together, the three form the complete triad of orientation-questions: *who walks* (identity-view — rightness of the subject), *whether to walk* (doubt — resolution to depart), *which road* (rite-grasping — discernment of the way). Until these three are resolved, "trending toward liberation" cannot even be spoken of — it is not a question of near or far, but of whether one is aimed, willing to move, set on the right road. This is why they are *one cluster*, and the cluster severed *first*: orientation is the precondition of all travel.

The contrast with the latter two lower fetters (sensual greed, ill-will) makes it clearer still: sensual greed and ill-will obstruct "travelling far within the desire-realm" (keeping one from rising above it) — they are *journey-fetters*; the first three obstruct "whether one can set out correctly" — they are *orientation-fetters*. Calibrate the direction first, then speak of travelling far — the sequence is natural.

### 3.2 The *Kośa*'s direct proof: "they obstruct the trending toward liberation, hence only three are taught"

This "orientation-structure" is no conjecture of mine; the *Abhidharmakośa*, fascicle 21, in glossing the name of the five lower fetters, gives outright the reason the first three are singled out to fall first:

> 又五順下分，由二不超欲，由三復還下……或不欲發趣，迷道及疑道，能障趣解脫，故唯說斷三……由後二種不能超欲界，設有能超由前三還下，如守獄卒防邏人故。[^5]
>
> *"Further, of the five lower fetters: by two one does not rise above the desire-[realm]; by three one falls back down again… Or: not wishing to set out, deluded as to the path, and doubting the path — these obstruct the trending toward liberation; hence only the severance of three is taught… By the latter two one cannot rise above the desire-realm; even if one could rise, by the first three one is dragged back down, like jailers patrolling against escapees."*

Two lines are the iron proof. First, "by two one does not rise above the desire, by three one falls back down": the latter two fetters (sensual greed, ill-will) keep beings *from rising above* the desire-realm — they are fetters that *hold one in place*; the first three, "even if one could rise, by the first three one falls back" — even having momentarily risen, with the first three unsevered one is *dragged back*. The commentator drives it home with a simile: "like jailers patrolling against escapees" — the first three are like jailers on patrol; though a prisoner scale the wall a moment, he is hauled back. Second, more directly: "not wishing to set out, deluded as to the path, and doubting the path — these obstruct the trending toward liberation; hence only the severance of three is taught" — the disease of the first three is precisely "not wishing to set out (unwilling to depart), being deluded as to the path (taking the wrong road — rite-grasping), doubting the path (doubt)," and because they *obstruct the trending toward liberation*, the severance of three is taken as the definition of stream-entry.

The four words "obstruct the trending toward liberation" are the scriptural-commentarial landing of this paper's orientation-thesis. The first three are singled out as what the stream-enterer severs not for their grossness but because they *specifically obstruct "trending" itself*. Sever them, and the road of "trending to liberation" truly opens; leave them, and however fierce the exertion, one is hauled back by the "jailers." Treatise and the paper's orientation-structure tally exactly.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the *Kośa*'s "deluded as to the path and doubting the path, they obstruct the trending toward liberation, hence only three are taught" and "even if one could rise, by the first three one falls back" positively establish that the first three are obstructions of orientation; their being severed first is owed to "obstructing the trending toward liberation" (orientation), not to grossness (journey). The orientation-thesis here has its express commentarial warrant.

### 3.3 The correspondence of the stream-image with orientation: direction set, the flow does not reverse

The severance of orientation corresponds tightly with the stream-current image of "entering the stream." The stream is the eightfold path (2.2) — to enter the stream is to "enter the current of the right path." And "entering the stream" is an apt image precisely because it captures the property unique to orientation once established: *flow-with-the-current*.

Once within the proper current, the flow itself carries one downstream (to nibbāna). Though the practitioner have ups and downs (as in 2.1, "may, in the meantime, give rise to grief and pain"), though he paddle now fast now slow, the overall bearing is fixed by the direction of flow — he may paddle a few strokes against it, but the flow does not reverse, and in the end he goes down with the current. This corresponds exactly to the severance of orientation: the first three severed, the subject is righted (no longer serving a deluded self), the resolve is set (no longer turning back in doubt), the road is discerned (no longer deviating onto the wrong one), so that "trending to liberation" becomes a *directed current* rather than a stagnant pool free to drift any way.

This is the stream-form ground of the stream-enterer's "not falling to lower destinies, bound for nibbāna, at most seven returns": not falling, because the possibility of reversal (falling) has been excluded by settled orientation; bound for nibbāna, because the current's flow at last reaches downstream; at most seven returns, because despite ups and downs the flow's force delivers one — finitely, certainly — to the destination (the fixed number of seven returns belongs in detail to the next paper, P04, "Irreversibility and the Seven Returns"). This paper takes only the "stream-entry" face: the stream-enterer is one whose orientation is established and who flows without reversal.

Closing through the approach-fruit grammar (continuing P02): the *approach of stream-entry* is the moving aspect of the first three fetters being severed (orientation under calibration, the jailers still patrolling but their force waning); the *fruit of stream-entry* is the resting aspect of the first three severed and abiding (orientation established, the jailers withdrawn, moving with the flow). The stream-enterer's fruit is the establishment and abiding of the cessation of "direction now right, flowing without reversal."

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

**❌ Misreading 1: "the stream-enterer = one who has reached a fairly high state."** The whole paper dismantles this. The stream-enterer is not "how far one has practised" but "direction now right." What the first three obstruct is orientation (setting out), not the journey (travelling far); so the key to stream-entry lies in "aim, resolve, discernment of the road," not the depth or breadth of experience. One dry in concentration but irreversibly done with identity-view, doubt, and rite-grasping is already a stream-enterer; one deep and luminous in absorption but still grasping a deluded self, still doubting the path, still taking the non-path is not yet in the stream. State and fruit part company here.

**⚠️ Correction 2: the first three's "falling first" is not because they are "light," nor does it mean sensual greed and ill-will are unimportant.** The first three fall first because they obstruct orientation (the precondition of trending to liberation), not because they are lighter than sensual greed and ill-will — the treatise calls the first three "jailers," of the heaviest weight. Sensual greed and ill-will are severed later (the latter two of the lower fetters), also not unimportant, but because they are journey-fetters (keeping one from rising above the desire-realm), their sequence falls after orientation is set. Earlier and later is structural sequence, not a ranking by weight. Do not misread the subtraction's order as "the earlier-severed are light, the later-severed heavy."

**⚠️ Correction 3: severing identity-view is not "attaining some grand state of non-self," but "no longer misidentifying the subject."** The severance of identity-view is easily read as "realizing some mystical experience of non-self" — which slides back into experience. Severing identity-view is the calibration of orientation: no longer taking a deluded "I" as the centre of practice, seeing the five aggregates as not-self and not-mine, as they are. It is the *cessation of a misidentification*, not the *acquisition of a new experience*. Likewise, severing doubt is not "giving rise to some experience of conviction" but "the cessation of wavering, the establishment of trust"; severing rite-grasping is not "attaining a peak experience of the right path" but "the cessation of taking the wrong road." The severance of all three is subtraction (the cessation of a kind of wrong orientation), not addition (the seizing of a new state).

## VI. Conclusion

**Essential restatement.** The stream-enterer severs the first three fetters — identity-view, rite-grasping, doubt — and the question is "why these three first." The answer: the three are a cluster of orientation-fetters, obstructing "setting out correctly" itself (who walks, whether to walk, which road), not the desire-realm journey. The *Kośa* establishes this outright with "deluded as to the path and doubting the path, they obstruct the trending toward liberation, hence only three are taught" and "even if one could rise, by the first three one falls back" (3.2). To sever them is to establish orientation, and the stream-current image (the stream is the eightfold path) depicts precisely the "flowing without reversal" once direction is right (3.3). In approach-fruit grammar: the approach of stream-entry is the first three being severed (orientation in progress), the fruit is the first three severed and abiding (orientation established). The subtraction's first wall takes down *the possibility of taking the wrong road.*

**Guidance for practice.** A practitioner taking stock of stream-entry should ask not "how deep my concentration, how bright my experience," but the three orientation-questions: do I still take a deluded "I" as the centre of practice (identity-view)? do I still waver, turning back every three steps, about this road (doubt)? is what I practise truly the road to liberation, or some useless grasping (rite-grasping)? If these three remain, then however my exertion blazes, it may be force in the wrong direction. If these three cease, the direction is right — and though experience be plain, you have entered the stream. To one long in practice who doubts "have I made any progress at all," this paper offers a different ruler: progress is not in the ledger of experience but in whether your *bearing* has been permanently calibrated.

**Open questions.** This paper cut Section IV because, though "orientation-calibration" formally resembles cybernetic navigation-correction, what it calibrates is the bearing toward an unconditioned cessation, alike in face but unlike in spirit with worldly feedback-systems; a forced convergence would misrepresent. A genuine question for later: must the severance of the first three be "simultaneous and sudden," or is there an internal sequence (identity-view first, then doubt, then rite-grasping)? This touches the dispute over the suddenness or gradualness of the path of seeing, beyond this paper's orientation-theme, left for later. Further, the "orientation / journey" division set here must be tested in later papers: P04 will verify how "irreversibility and the seven returns" unfold determinately from the irreversibility of orientation; P05 (the once-returner) must then handle a new difficulty — how does the *not-total* mark of "thinning greed-hatred-delusion" fit the subtraction (a wall only half taken down — does it count as taken down)? Once orientation is set, how the journey-fetters take "thinning" as their first unit of diminution is the task from the next paper on.
