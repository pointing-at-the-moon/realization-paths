# Irreversibility and the Seven Returns

**How Certainty Is a Doctrine of Severance, Not a Word of Reassurance**

*中文主題：不退與七返——確定性何以是斷結之義而非保證之辭*

## Abstract

The previous paper established that the stream-enterer's severance of the first three fetters is the establishment of orientation. This paper treats the two certainty-statements that follow upon stream-entry — "not falling to lower destinies" (irreversibility) and "at most seven returns" (a fixed upper bound on rebirths) — and argues that neither is a word of reassurance but a strict corollary of severance. Irreversibility is not "the Blessed One promises you will not fall," but "no single fetter remains capable of making one take birth back in this world" — the negative formulation of subtraction (the pull-back fetter being absent, there is nothing to regress by). The seven returns are not a lenient quota of "at most seven more times," but a fixed upper bound yielded by the way a severance settles a mind-continuum: the *Kośa* glosses it as "the continuum, by this point, must ripen" — the noble-path type once entered, the continuum must ripen within a finite, numbered span; so the "at most" of "at most seven returns" means *a maximum* (the greatest number of rebirths), not "all complete seven returns." The paper further clarifies: the Āgamas do not coin the literal term "non-regress" but carry its substance through "one does not see a single thing unsevered capable of making one take birth back in this world"; the treatises then fix the number through the twenty-eight existences (seven each among gods and humans) and the similes of the "seven-fold skill" and the "seven-leaf tree." Conclusion: the certainty of stream-entry is the two corollary-faces of the unconditioned property "the severed fetter cannot re-arise" — downward (no falling) and toward-the-end (the seven returns must make an end of suffering); certainty is not promised, but entailed by the subtraction.

**Keywords**: sotāpanna, stream-entry, irreversibility, not falling to lower destinies, at most seven returns, twenty-eight existences, certainty, the end of suffering, mind-continuum, Abhidharmakośa, severing fetters, subtraction

## I. The Question

The stream-enterer carries two widely-quoted certainty-statements: "not falling to lower destinies," and "at most seven returns among gods and humans, thereafter making an end of suffering." The first says he will not regress; the second, that he will take birth at most seven more times before suffering ends. The two sound like a pair of guarantees — as if, upon entering the stream, the Blessed One had issued you a "no-falling insurance policy" and a "graduate within seven" promise.

This paper argues: to read them as guarantees is a root misplacement. "Not falling" and "seven returns" are not graces *promised* but corollaries *entailed* by severance. The previous paper established that the stream-enterer's severance of the first three fetters is the establishment of orientation, the establishment of an unconditioned cessation. This paper will show that once that subtraction stands, "not falling" and "seven returns" arrive uninvited — they are the two necessary corollary-faces of the unconditioned property "the severed fetter cannot re-arise," one face turned downward (no falling), one turned toward the end (the seven returns must make an end of suffering).

Why does the distinction matter? Because the "guarantee" reading quietly drags the fruit back into accumulation and external force: as if certainty came from the warranty of an outside promiser rather than from a change in the practitioner's own structure. This is exactly the direction the first three papers rebut — imagining the fruit as something *given* (experience, grace, a quota) rather than as a property naturally disclosed by a wall taken down. If certainty is a doctrine of severance, it is internal, derivable, needing no external surety; if certainty is a word of reassurance, it hangs on the credit of the one who promises. This paper returns certainty to severance.

The argument: Section II presents the scriptural-commentarial basis (the Āgama carriage of non-regress; the treatises' fixing of the seven-return number); Section III, the core argument, establishes in three layers that certainty is the two corollary-faces of severance (irreversibility as the negative formulation of subtraction; the seven returns as the finite fixed number of a ripening continuum; both sourced in the non-re-arising of cessation); Section IV omits convergence; Section V offers corrections (especially the literal "non-regress" and the "all complete seven" errors); Section VI concludes.

## II. The Canonical Basis

### 2.1 The Āgama carriage of non-regress: not one thing unsevered capable of making one take birth back in this world

The Āgama's wording for the stream-enterer's non-regress is worth dwelling on — it does *not* use the three characters "non-regress" directly, but carries it through a negative clause. *Saṃyukta-āgama*, fascicle 15 (sūtra 396):

> 此三結盡，名須陀洹，不墮惡趣法，必定正覺……不見彼聖弟子有一法不斷，能令還生此世者。[^1]
>
> *"With these three fetters exhausted, one is called a stream-enterer, of the nature not to fall to bad destinies, certain of awakening… one does not see in that noble disciple a single thing unsevered, capable of making him take birth back in this world."*

The crux is the final clause: "one does not see… a single thing unsevered, capable of making him take birth back in this world" — searching this noble disciple through, one cannot find any factor "still unsevered, and strong enough to make him take birth back in this world (fall back into the upstream of saṃsāra)." The substance of non-regress is here expressed as an *absence*: the hook that could pull one back is missing. This is precisely the grammar of subtraction — non-regress is not some protection added, but the regress-causing fetter removed.

Fascicle 2 (sūtra 61) lists the three marks positively: "not falling to lower destinies, fixedly trending toward awakening, taking birth among gods and humans seven times, thereafter making an end of suffering."[^2] All three are directional: not falling (the downward road sealed), fixedly trending (the road to the end fixed), seven existences making an end (the journey finite and certain to arrive).

### 2.2 The treatises' fixing of the seven-return number: the continuum, by this point, must ripen

What the "at most" of "at most seven returns" means, and where the number comes from, the treatises gloss precisely. *Abhidharmakośa*, fascicle 23, first marks it in verse:

> 未斷修斷失，住果極七返。[^3]
>
> *"With the to-be-abandoned-by-cultivation not yet abandoned, abiding in the fruit, at most seven returns."*

Then develops it in prose:

> 諸住果者於一切地修所斷失都未斷時名為預流，生極七返。七返言顯七往返生，是人天中各七生義。極言為顯受生最多，非諸預流皆受七返故。[^3]
>
> *"One abiding in the fruit, while the cultivation-abandonables of all stages remain wholly unabandoned, is called a stream-enterer, born at most seven returns. 'Seven returns' indicates seven to-and-fro births — the sense of seven births each among gods and humans. 'At most' indicates the greatest number of rebirths — for not all stream-enterers undergo seven returns."*

Two senses must be held tight: first, "seven returns" = seven births each among gods and humans (a maximum of twenty-eight existences in all); second, "at most" = the *greatest* number of rebirths, "not all stream-enterers undergo seven returns" — not every stream-enterer must complete seven; seven is the ceiling, not a fixed allotment. The commentator fixes the number further at the close of fascicle 23 with similes, and cites a cross-school reading in corroboration:

> 彼從此後別於人中，極多結七中有生有，天中亦然，總二十八，皆七等故說極七生，如七處善及七葉樹……飲光部經分明別說於人天處各受七生。[^4]
>
> *"Thereafter, among humans, at most seven intermediate-and-birth existences, and likewise among gods, twenty-eight in all — all being sevens, hence 'at most seven births,' like the seven-fold skill and the seven-leaf tree… the Kāśyapīya scripture distinctly states seven births taken each in the human and divine stations."*

"Twenty-eight in all" (seven human + seven divine, each comprising intermediate and birth existence), the similes "like the seven-fold skill and the seven-leaf tree," and even the citation of the Kāśyapīya (飲光部) scripture as corroboration — the treatises place the number "seven" within the structure of a *ripening continuum*, not an arbitrary leniency.

### 2.3 Why the seven returns are finite: the continuum, by this point, must ripen

Why must suffering end *within* seven returns? The *Kośa*, same fascicle, gives the mechanism:

> 何緣彼無受第八有？相續齊此必成熟故，聖道種類法應如是。[^4]
>
> *"Why does he not undergo an eighth existence? Because the continuum, by this point, must ripen — such is the nature of the noble-path type."*

"The continuum, by this point, must ripen" — the stream-enterer's continuum (the stream of body-and-mind), having entered the noble-path type, is like ripe fruit certain to fall: at this node, the continuum *must necessarily* ripen (make an end of suffering), hence no possibility of an eighth existence. The treatise also glosses "making an end of suffering":

> 經說預流作苦邊際……依齊此生後更無苦，是令後苦不相續義。或苦邊際所謂涅槃。[^5]
>
> *"The scripture says the stream-enterer makes an end of suffering… based on there being, from this point on, no further suffering — the sense of not letting later suffering continue. Or 'the end of suffering' means nibbāna."*

"Making an end of suffering" is "from this point on, later suffering does not continue" — the continuity of suffering is here sealed off. The finitude of the seven returns is rooted in this property of "the continuum must ripen, later suffering does not continue."

> ⚠️ A note on register: the carriage of non-regress (2.1) and the three marks of the seven returns (2.2, sūtras 61/396) are stated outright in the Āgamas; the fine glosses "at most = maximum, not a fixed allotment," "twenty-eight existences," "the continuum by this point must ripen," and "the end of suffering" (2.2–2.3) are the *Kośa*'s treatise-teaching, consonant with scripture but finer; the citation of the "Kāśyapīya scripture" is the commentator's adducing of a cross-school reading in corroboration, marked here as treatise.

## III. The Core Argument

### 3.1 Irreversibility is the negative formulation of subtraction: the regress-causing fetter being absent, there is nothing to regress by

"Non-regress" is most easily read as a *positive protection* — as if, upon stream-entry, a layer of fall-proof armour were installed. The Āgama's wording precisely refuses this reading: it says "one does not see a single thing unsevered capable of making one take birth back in this world" (2.1) — the substance of non-regress is a *negative* fact: the regress-causing fetter no longer exists.

This fits subtraction exactly. The previous paper established: the stream-enterer's severance of the first three fetters is the establishment of an unconditioned cessation; and the unconditioned cannot re-arise (P01 §3.3). Now "regress" requires, as its *cause*, some still-existing fetter able to pull one back into the upstream; and that cause (the orientation-obstructing ones among the first three) has precisely been permanently dismantled. Without a cause, "regress" is not "forbidden" but "unable to occur" — just as a wall taken down does not "rebuild itself," not because someone guards it against rebuilding, but because "rebuilding" has lost its agent.

So non-regress is not externally-added armour but the *negative formulation* of subtraction: the fruit's being a cessation itself excludes the cause of regress. This also explains why the Āgama does not use the positive term "non-regress" directly, but the negative clause "one does not see a thing unsevered capable of making one take birth back" — because the substance of non-regress is, by its nature, an *absence*; a positive term more easily misleads one into supposing something was installed. The Āgama's wording fits the bone of subtraction more closely than the later three-character "non-regress."

### 3.2 The seven returns are the finite fixed number of a ripening continuum, not a lenient quota

If "seven returns" is read as "you are granted at most seven more chances," it again falls back into the accumulative imagination of a "quota" — as if there were a ledger recording how many chances you have left. The treatises' gloss is categorically different: the seven returns are a *finite fixed number* yielded by the *ripening of a continuum*.

The mechanism is "the continuum, by this point, must ripen" (2.3). The stream-enterer's continuum, having entered the noble-path type, possesses the momentum of "necessary ripening" — like ripe fruit certain to fall, like an arrow loosed certain to complete its flight. Ripening requires the continuum to unfold over some number of births to finish its work, and this unfolding has a *structural finitude*: seven each among gods and humans, twenty-eight existences in all (2.2). "Seven" is not a bestowed number of chances but the structural quantity "the upper bound of the unfolding required for the continuum to ripen." Hence "at most" is glossed as *maximum* not *fixed allotment* — "not all stream-enterers undergo seven returns" (2.2): one of sharp faculties may finish in three or two; seven is the ceiling, not a span everyone must walk in full.

This corresponds tightly with subtraction: with orientation set (the first three severed), the continuum is like water entered into the proper stream, and "the distance required to flow to the lower reaches (the end of suffering)" has an upper bound; the seven returns are the number of that bound. It is not a debt-quota of "how many owed," but the journey-ceiling of "certain to arrive with the current, and at most this many." Certainty here shows as twofold: certain to arrive (ripening is necessary) + finite (twenty-eight existences as the bound).

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the *Kośa*'s three lines — "the continuum, by this point, must ripen," "twenty-eight in all," and "'at most' indicates the greatest number of rebirths, not all undergo seven returns" — establish that the seven returns are the finite fixed number of a ripening continuum, not a lenient quota. "At most" as a ceiling and not a fixed allotment is the treatise's express teaching.

### 3.3 Irreversibility and the seven returns share one source: the two corollary-faces of cessation's non-re-arising

Non-regress (downward) and the seven returns (toward the end) seem two matters, but in fact share one source: *the severed fetter cannot re-arise* (the unconditioned nature of cessation).

Looking downward: the fetter that could make one fall back into the upstream is severed and cannot re-arise, hence "not falling" — this is the corollary of cessation's non-re-arising in the *reverse* direction. Looking toward the end: the continuum, orientation now set, must ripen and ripen *finitely*, hence "the seven returns must make an end of suffering" — this is the corollary of the same non-re-arising in the *forward* direction. One nature, two faces: what is severed cannot re-arise, which both seals the road back (non-regress) and ensures the road ahead is certain-to-arrive and finite (seven returns).

It is thus apparent that the "certainty" of stream-entry is not two independent guarantees at all, but *two logical consequences of one unconditioned property*. To read them as promises sunders the two and misplaces their source (assigning it to external surety); to read them as corollaries of severance unifies the two at once and locates their source internally (in cessation). Certainty is not given, but entailed.

Closing through the approach-fruit grammar (continuing P02–P03): once the fruit of stream-entry (the first three severed and abiding) stands, "non-regress" and "the seven returns" are what the *resting aspect* of that fruit necessarily *spreads out* — the fruit being the abiding of a cessation, its abiding carries of itself the two spreadings of "incapable of regress" (downward) and "necessarily finite ripening" (toward the end). Certainty is the temporal projection of the fruit's resting aspect: sealed toward the past (non-regress), fixed toward the future (the seven returns).

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

**❌ Misreading 1: "non-regress = the Blessed One promises you will not fall."** The whole paper dismantles this. Non-regress is not external surety but the negative formulation of subtraction: the regress-causing fetter no longer exists (2.1, 3.1). To assign non-regress to a promiser's warranty re-externalizes and re-accumulates the fruit — certainty then hangs on the credit of an outside force rather than a change in internal structure. The Āgama's negative wording "one does not see a thing unsevered capable of making one take birth back" guards precisely against this error.

**⚠️ Correction 2: "at most seven returns" is not "all complete seven returns," and "non-regress" is not Āgama-literal.** First, "at most" is glossed as a maximum, not a fixed allotment; "not all stream-enterers undergo seven returns" (2.2) — one of sharp faculties may end suffering in fewer than seven, seven being the ceiling. To read it as "every stream-enterer must return seven more times" is an error. Second, the three characters "non-regress" are a later term; the Āgama carries non-regress through the negative clause "one does not see a thing unsevered capable of making one take birth back in this world" (2.1). This paper uses "non-regress" for predicative convenience, but it must not be read back into the Āgama as if that positive term were original — in fact the Āgama's negative wording fits the bone of subtraction more closely.

**⚠️ Correction 3: the "must" of the seven returns is the necessity of a ripening continuum, not the predetermination of fatalism.** The "must" of "the continuum, by this point, must ripen" is the structural momentum of a continuum once severance is accomplished (like ripe fruit certain to fall), not an externally predetermined destiny. The stream-enterer must still practise (the path of cultivation, severing the remaining fetters); the seven returns are the upper bound of "if the continuum is left to unfold, at most this many," not a fate of "having to walk seven full lives no matter what." One of sharp faculties practising diligently may be swift; the "must" refers to the certainty of *direction and ceiling*, not the pre-writing of the *process*. Do not mistake certainty for fatalism.

## VI. Conclusion

**Essential restatement.** The stream-enterer's "not falling" and "seven returns" are not two guarantees but the two corollary-faces entailed by severance. Non-regress is the negative formulation of subtraction — the regress-causing fetter severed and unable to re-arise, hence nothing to regress by (3.1); the seven returns are the finite fixed number of a ripening continuum — "the continuum by this point must ripen," "twenty-eight in all," "at most" a ceiling not a fixed allotment (3.2); the two share one source in the unconditioned non-re-arising of cessation, one face sealing the past (non-regress), one fixing the future (seven returns) (3.3). Certainty is not promised, but entailed by the subtraction.

**Guidance for practice.** Toward "non-regress" and "seven returns," the practitioner should give rise not to the ease of "I have a guarantee now," nor to the anxiety of "I have only a few left," but to a faithful understanding of the internal certainty of severance: your non-regress hangs on no external surety, but on whether that wall is truly taken down, whether the regress-causing fetter truly no longer exists — if it truly is severed, certainty is a property of your structure, which none can give and none can take away. The fitting response to this certainty is not slackening ("I won't regress anyway") but more diligent verification and advancement of the remaining severances — for "seven returns" is a ceiling, not a quota, and one of sharp faculties practising diligently may finish the journey sooner.

**Open questions.** This paper cut Section IV because, though "the finite fixed number of a ripening continuum" formally resembles the certain upper bound of decay, decay is a probabilistic statistic and what decays is conditioned, alike in face but unlike in spirit with cessation's non-re-arising; a forced convergence would misrepresent. A genuine question for later: do the temporal structures of "continuum" and "ripening" in "the continuum by this point must ripen" require importing the *Kośa*'s theory of momentary continuity to be fully grounded? This touches the theory of time, beyond this paper's certainty-theme. Further, this Part (the stream-enterer) is here complete. The next Part (the once-returner, from P05) will face subtraction's first real difficulty: "thinning greed-hatred-delusion" — a mark of *not-total* severance. The subtraction established in the first three papers measured only walls of "wholly severed / severed." But "thinning" is a wall only partly taken down. How can a wall taken down only halfway still constitute a "fruit"? How is the doctrinal standing of "thinning," as an independent unit of diminution, to be established? This is exactly what the next paper must confront head-on.
