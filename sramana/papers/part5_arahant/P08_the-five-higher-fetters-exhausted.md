# The Five Higher Fetters Exhausted

**Why the Last Five Walls Are the Subtlest and Hardest**

*中文主題：五上分結盡——最後五牆何以最細最難*

## Abstract

After the non-returner crosses the desire-realm, what remains is only the five higher fetters — greed for form, greed for the formless, conceit, restlessness, and ignorance. This paper asks: why are these last five walls the subtlest and hardest to sever among the ten fetters? The thesis: the difficulty of the five higher lies not in their being "gross" but in their being "subtle" — they are not the fevers of the desire-realm (gross and easily felt) but the fine fetters binding to the heights (the form- and formless-realms, down to the faintest residue of self); the nearer the endpoint, the more what is severed is not "an obvious fault" but "an extremely hidden grasping." The *Kośa* glosses their name as "augmenting the higher realms, making beings unable to rise beyond the higher": greed for form and the formless bind to the meditative bliss of the upper two realms, conceit is the last shadow of "self," restlessness the mind's last unrest, ignorance the root of all fetters. The paper further clarifies a methodological point: "the five higher fetters" as a *term* does not appear in the Āgamas; the Āgamas list the contents substantively (as the *Saṃyukta-āgama*'s impermanence-perception sūtra lists "greed for form, greed for the formless, restlessness, conceit, ignorance," together with sensual greed), and the terminological full-set is the treatises' (the *Kośa*'s) accomplishment. So this paper marks plainly the boundary between "term" and "content." Conclusion: the severance of the five higher is the closing of the subtraction — what is severed is ever subtler precisely because the gross is already exhausted; the last five walls are hard to take down not because the walls are thick but because they have retreated to the most hidden place, requiring the subtlest wisdom (such as the perception of impermanence) to be uprooted. This is the object of the arahant-*approach*; the mastery of its full severance (the exhaustion of the taints) belongs to the Attainment axis.

**Keywords**: arahant, five higher fetters, greed for form, greed for the formless, conceit, restlessness, ignorance, perception of impermanence, augmenting the higher, the subtlest, Abhidharmakośa, subtraction

## I. The Question

The subtraction has come this far: only five walls remain — greed for form, greed for the formless, conceit, restlessness, ignorance — the five higher fetters. The non-returner has crossed the desire-realm, the five lower exhausted; only these five remain. By the straight extrapolation of "fruit measured by severance," the arahant seems merely to "sever five more fetters" — another five walls, no different from the fruit-by-fruit severance hitherto.

But scripture and commentator treat these five with a wording wholly unlike the earlier ones. The earlier fetters mostly bore the marks of "gross," "fevered," "afflicting" — sensual greed and ill-will, scorching and easily felt, recognizable by all as faults. The five higher have no such gross mark: what greed for form and the formless covets is the subtle bliss of meditative absorption (the attainments of the form- and formless-realms), not gross desire; what conceit grasps is the last thread of "self"'s shadow, not gross arrogance; what restlessness stirs is the mind's last trace of unrest, not gross agitation; what ignorance deludes is the most fundamental and least self-evident delusion. They are acknowledged as the hardest five among the ten to sever — and their difficulty lies precisely not in grossness but in *subtlety*.

The thesis: the difficulty of the five higher is the "difficulty near the endpoint" — what is severed is ever subtler precisely because the gross is already exhausted. Practice is like peeling an onion: the coarse outer layers peel easily (the desire-realm's greed and hatred, removed in sequence by stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner), but the deeper one goes the thinner the layer, and the thinner, the harder to grip. The five higher are the innermost few layers: what is coveted is no longer gross desire but meditative bliss, what is grasped no longer the gross self but the self's shadow, what is deluded no longer gross confusion but the root of ignorance. The last five walls are hard to take down not because the walls are thick but because they have retreated to the most hidden place — the gross gone, what remains is only "extremely hidden grasping," which no wisdom but the subtlest can uproot.

The paper must also clarify a methodological point: "the five higher fetters" as an *enumerated term* does not appear in the Āgamas; the Āgamas list it substantively (stating the fetter-names directly), the clean enumerated terminology being the treatises' accomplishment. This boundary bears on the honesty of the axis — this paper marks plainly that "the term belongs to the treatise, the content to scripture," not reading a later term back into the Āgamas. The argument: Section II presents the basis (the treatise's definition, the Āgama's substantive listing, the perception of impermanence as severing them); Section III, the core argument, establishes in three layers why the last five walls are subtlest and hardest (the higher binding lies aloft, hence subtle; the five are each their category's last residue; the subtlest wisdom is needed to uproot); Section IV omits convergence; Section V offers corrections (especially the term/content boundary); Section VI concludes (and opens P09's threshold of the exhaustion of taints).

## II. The Canonical Basis

### 2.1 The treatise's definition: augmenting the higher realms, making one unable to rise beyond

The name and enumeration of "the five higher fetters" are the treatises' establishment. *Abhidharmakośa*, fascicle 21:

> 順上分亦五，色無色二貪，掉舉慢無明，令不超上故。論曰：如是五種若未斷時，能令有情不超上界。順益上界故，名順上分結。[^1]
>
> *"The higher-augmenting are also five: the two greeds for form and the formless, restlessness, conceit, ignorance — because they make one unable to rise beyond the higher. Treatise: while these five are unsevered, they make beings unable to rise beyond the higher realms. Because they augment the higher realms, they are named the higher-augmenting fetters."*

The five higher fetters = greed for form, greed for the formless, restlessness, conceit, ignorance; the sense of their name lies in "augmenting the higher realms, making beings unable to rise beyond the higher" — just as the five lower fetters "make one unable to rise above the desire-realm" (cited in P06), the five higher make beings unable to rise beyond the *upper two realms* (form and formless). Note the symmetry: the five lower bind to the desire-realm (unable to rise above desire), the five higher bind to the upper realms (unable to rise above the higher). The realm bound being higher (the absorptive states of the form- and formless-realms), the fetter is naturally *subtler* than the desire-realm's gross affliction — this is the structural root of the five higher's difficulty (3.1).

### 2.2 The Āgama's substantive listing: the perception of impermanence severs the higher-realm fetters

The *term* "five higher fetters" does not appear in the Āgamas; yet their *content* the Āgamas list by stating the fetter-names directly. *Saṃyukta-āgama*, fascicle 10 (the impermanence-perception sūtra):

> 無常想修習多修習，能斷一切欲愛、色愛、無色愛、掉、慢、無明。[^2]
>
> *"The perception of impermanence, cultivated and much cultivated, can sever all sensual greed, greed for form, greed for the formless, restlessness, conceit, ignorance."*

This sentence takes "the cultivation of the perception of impermanence" as what severs, and lists what is severed: sensual greed, greed for form, greed for the formless, restlessness, conceit, ignorance. Of these, greed for form, greed for the formless, restlessness, conceit, ignorance are the five content-items of the five higher fetters; only the sūtra lists "sensual greed" together, making six, without the enumerated terminological cut of "the five higher fetters." This shows precisely: the Āgamas have the *content* (the names of the higher-realm fetters), while "the five higher fetters" as an *enumerated term* is the treatises' matter.

Worth special note is the means of severance: "the perception of impermanence." To sever the subtlest five higher fetters, what is used is no gross antidote but "the perception of impermanence" — contemplating the impermanence of all (including upper-realm meditative bliss, including "self"). Scripture depicts it with three similes: like a farmer deep-ploughing to root out the grass (to the root), like shaking cut grass so the withered all fall, like a fierce wind shaking the branch so the mango fruits all drop. All three depict "falling from the root" — not trimming the branches and leaves (gross antidote) but stirring the very root (the subtlest wisdom), so that the most hidden graspings fall of themselves. This tallies closely with "the last five walls need the subtlest wisdom" (3.3).

> ⚠️ A note on register (key to this paper): the *enumerated term* "five higher fetters" and the gloss "augmenting the higher realms" (2.1) are the *Kośa*'s establishment; the Āgamas do not present this term. What the Āgamas have is a substantive listing of fetter-names (2.2, and that joined with sensual greed as six, not a clean five-set). This paper uses the term "five higher fetters" following the treatises, but marks plainly: the term belongs to the treatise, the content to scripture; the boundary must not be blurred.

## III. The Core Argument

### 3.1 The higher binding lies aloft, hence subtle: the difficulty is in hiddenness, not grossness

Why are the five higher hardest? The first-layer answer lies in the *realm* they bind. The five lower bind to the desire-realm — what the desire-realm's fetters (sensual greed, ill-will) confront is gross, evident desire-pleasure and aversion-distress, scorching and obvious, easily recognized as faults, so hard to sever yet "easy to see." The five higher bind to the upper two realms (form, formless) — what they confront is the subtle bliss of meditative absorption (greed for form coveting the form-realm's absorptive bliss, greed for the formless coveting the formless-realm's), a subtle grasping within deep stillness, neither scorching nor burning, resembling rather "the good" and "the pleasant," so extremely *hard to recognize as a fetter*.

The crux of the difficulty lies in this "non-self-evidence." The desire-realm's greed and hatred have their fault written on the face (distress present); the upper realm's absorption-greed has its fault hidden within bliss (the subtler the grasping the deeper the absorption, mistaken instead for attainment). So too with conceit, restlessness, ignorance: conceit's shadow hides in the subtle thought "I have attained," restlessness's unrest hides in the subtle stirring within deep stillness, ignorance's root hides at the very bottom of all cognition and never shows on the surface. The higher the binding (the stiller, the more blissful, the subtler), the less the fetter shows itself; the less it shows, the harder it is to make its object and sever it. So the five higher's difficulty lies not in their force being great (gross) but in their form being hidden (subtle) — like dust clinging to a mirror, the coarse grime easily wiped, the last thin film hardest to notice, hardest to clean.

This is the structure of "difficulty near the endpoint": subtraction proceeds fruit by fruit, the gross exhausted first (the desire-realm's fevers, removed in sequence by the first four fruits), and the nearer the endpoint the subtler what remains. The five higher are the innermost layer — not because they are "stronger than sensual greed," but because the gross is gone and what remains is only subtle fetters retreated to the most hidden place. The increase of difficulty is not an increase of force but an increase of hiddenness.

### 3.2 The five fetters are each their category's last residue

The "subtlety" of the five higher can be seen in each being "the last residue of some category of grasping." One by one:

**Greed for form, greed for the formless** — the last residue of greed. Gross desire's greed (the desire-realm's sensual greed) was exhausted at the non-return position; the greed that remains is no longer gross desire but a subtle greed for the *meditative bliss* of the form- and formless-realms. This is greed's residue retreated to the highest, purest place: what is coveted is now the stillest bliss, so it least resembles "greed." **Conceit** — the last shadow of "self." Identity-view (gross self-view) was severed at the stream-enterer position; yet the grasping of "self" does not necessarily wholly perish with the gross view, and a thread of subtle conceit of "self" remains (such as the fine thought "I have attained"). Conceit is the self's shadow retreated to the subtlest place. **Restlessness** — the mind's last unrest. Gross distraction was long since subdued; the restlessness that remains is the last trace of subtle stirring even a deeply still mind retains, the last residue of stillness not yet perfected. **Ignorance** — the root of all fetters. Ignorance is not one fetter ranked alongside the other four but the root on which all fetters depend; it is the last to be severed because it is the deepest, most fundamental — the prior nine fetters are all its branches, and while the root stands, the branches can return.

Thus the "last-ness" of the five higher is clear: they are, respectively, the last residues of greed (greed for form and the formless), self-grasping (conceit), mental stirring (restlessness), and root-delusion (ignorance), each retreated to its subtlest place. To sever them is the final move that uproots each category of affliction *from the root*. "Subtlest" means precisely that they are each category's root-end, shadow, last thread — not a gross mark but an ember.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the *Kośa*'s gloss "augmenting the higher realms, making one unable to rise beyond" (2.1), symmetric with the five lower's "unable to rise above desire" (P06), shows that the realm bound being higher, the fetter is subtler. The five fetters divide into the last residues of greed (greed for form and the formless), self (conceit), stirring (restlessness), and root-delusion (ignorance); their difficulty lies in hiddenness (subtlety), not in force (grossness), tallying with subtraction's closing property that "what is severed near the endpoint is ever subtler."

### 3.3 The subtlest wisdom needed to uproot: the perception of impermanence's "severing the root"

The subtlest fetters need the subtlest antidote. The five higher having retreated to the most hidden place (no longer graspable as gross marks), gross antidotes (such as the perception of the foul to counter sensual greed) have no purchase; what is needed is a wisdom able to reach the most fundamental and most universal — the perception of impermanence.

Why can the perception of impermanence sever the five higher? Because the common root-disease of the five higher is a subtle grasping at "permanence": one who covets the form- and formless-realms' meditative bliss subtly grasps that absorptive state as a lasting, possessable permanent-bliss; the conceited subtly grasps that there is a "permanent self" to take pride in; the ignorant fundamentally does not see impermanence and grasps permanence. The perception of impermanence directly illumines "all (including the subtlest meditative bliss, including 'self') is impermanent," thereby dissolving this subtle grasping *from the root* — hence scripture's similes of "deep-ploughing to root out the grass" (to the root), "shaking so the withered all fall" (stir the root and the branches and leaves fall of themselves), "a fierce wind shaking the branch so the fruits all drop" (shake the trunk and the tips fall of themselves) (2.2). All three depict "treating the root, not the branch": not countering greed for form, greed for the formless, conceit, restlessness, ignorance one branch-end at a time, but stirring their common root (the grasping at permanence) with the perception of impermanence, so the five fetters fall together.

This tallies exactly with 3.1–3.2: the five higher being each category's last residue, retreated to the most hidden place, cannot be exhausted by gross one-by-one antidotes but must be uprooted at a single stroke by the most universal and most fundamental wisdom (the perception of impermanence, reaching the common root of "permanence" that underlies all these subtle graspings). The subtlest walls need the subtlest and most thoroughgoing wisdom — subtle, to make their hiddenness its object; thoroughgoing, to reach their root.

Closing through the approach-fruit grammar (continuing P02): the moving aspect of the five higher being severed is the *arahant-approach* — this is the last stretch of "severing-in-progress" the Realization axis governs. But a boundary must be solemnly marked: this paper halts at the arahant-*approach* (the moving aspect of the five higher being severed, the taints about to be exhausted), and does not enter the *mastery* of the arahant-*fruit*. What the resting aspect of the arahant-fruit discloses — the mastery of liberation-of-mind and liberation-by-wisdom, the three knowledges and six higher knowledges, the eight liberations and the attainment of cessation — belongs to the Attainment axis, *arhat* (the twin-axis seam fixed by the PLAN). The Realization axis, *sramana*, walks to "the five higher being severed" and there halts; the next paper, P09 (the approach to the exhaustion of taints), walks the last step to the threshold, shoulder to shoulder with the Attainment axis's P01 (the exhaustion of taints is mastery) at the same instant of the taints' exhaustion. This paper is the arahant-approach's "object of severance" (the five higher); P09 is the arahant-approach's "arrival" (the threshold of the exhaustion of taints).

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

**❌ Misreading 1: "the five higher are grosser than the five lower, hence hard to sever."** The whole paper dismantles this. The five higher's difficulty lies not in grossness but precisely in *hiddenness (subtlety)* — the realm bound being high (the form- and formless-realms' meditative bliss), what is confronted is not gross affliction but subtle grasping, resembling rather the good and the pleasant, so hardest to recognize, hardest to make one's object. To misread "hardest to sever" as "grossest" misplaces the source of difficulty — it lies in hidden form, not great force. The gross (sensual greed, ill-will) was exhausted earlier at the prior fruits.

**⚠️ Correction 2: "the five higher fetters" is the treatises' enumerated term; the Āgamas have the content but not this enumerated term, and it must not be read back.** This is the key honesty of this paper. The clean five-list of "the five higher fetters" and the gloss "augmenting the higher" come from the *Kośa* (2.1); what the Āgamas have is a substantive listing of fetter-names (as the impermanence-perception sūtra lists greed for form, greed for the formless, restlessness, conceit, ignorance, and joined with sensual greed as six, not a clean five-division, 2.2). This paper uses the term following the treatises for convenience, but it must not be read back into the Āgamas as the Buddha's express enumerated statement of "the five higher fetters." Content in scripture, term in treatise — the boundary must be clear.

**⚠️ Correction 3: this paper halts at the arahant-*approach* and does not treat the *mastery* of the arahant-*fruit*.** The severance of the five higher is the arahant-*approach* (the moving aspect of severing-in-progress); the arahant-*fruit*'s *mastery* it accomplishes (the exhausted-taints' liberation of mind and wisdom, the three knowledges and six higher knowledges, and so on) belongs to the Attainment axis, *arhat*, not to this paper nor to this axis. The Realization axis walks only to the moving aspect of "severing the five higher, trending to the exhaustion of taints." Do not take this paper as having said all there is of the arahant — this paper says "how to take down the last five walls," not (and does not say) "what the mastery tastes like once the walls are gone." The latter is the Attainment axis's matter.

## VI. Conclusion

**Essential restatement.** The five higher fetters the arahant-approach severs (greed for form, greed for the formless, conceit, restlessness, ignorance) are the subtlest and hardest five among the ten. Their difficulty lies not in grossness but in subtlety: the realm bound is high (the form- and formless-realms' meditative bliss), what is confronted is hidden grasping rather than gross affliction, resembling rather the good and the pleasant, so hardest to recognize (3.1); the five divide into the last residues, retreated to the most hidden place, of greed, self, mental stirring, and root-delusion (3.2); so the subtlest and most thoroughgoing wisdom is needed (the perception of impermanence, reaching the common root of "permanence-grasping") to uproot them, treating the root not the branch (3.3). "The five higher fetters" is the treatises' enumerated term; the Āgamas have the content but not this enumeration, and this paper marks the boundary plainly (2.2, §V Correction 2). The subtraction here reaches its close — what is severed is ever subtler precisely because the gross is already exhausted.

**Guidance for practice.** The understanding a practitioner should form of the five higher is not "five gross fetters remain for me to assault fiercely," but the recognition that "the nearer the endpoint, the more the work must be subtle rather than forceful." The earlier fruits' severance could rely on clearly seeing the fault and countering it (the faults of sensual greed and ill-will are evident); the severance of the five higher requires being able to see the subtle grasping within "the seeming-good, the seeming-pleasant" — to see, within the deepest meditative bliss, its impermanent covetability; within the subtle thought "I have attained," its conceit; within deep stillness, its restlessness not yet exhausted. This is not gained by adding force but requires the subtlest contemplation (the perception of impermanence) to illumine the hiddenness and stir the common root of permanence-grasping. To be especially admonished: do not take upper-realm meditative bliss as ultimate and covet it — the deeper the meditative bliss the more it resembles attainment, and that is exactly where greed for form and the formless reside; not coveting at the most blissful is the true work of severing the five higher.

**Open questions (and the opening of P09).** This paper cut Section IV because, though "what is severed near the endpoint is ever subtler" formally resembles asymptotic convergence or the tail of purification, those are conditioned asymptotics, alike in face but unlike in spirit with the root-severance of the cessation of the five higher. A genuine question for later: ignorance being "the root of all fetters," is its severance "simultaneous" with the severance of the prior nine or "separately last"? Is the severance of ignorance the same matter as the exhaustion of taints (all taints forever ended) or two matters? This leads precisely to the next paper. With this, the first paper of Part 5 is complete: this paper sets out the arahant-approach's "object of severance" (the five higher, the subtlest five walls). The next paper, P09, "The Approach to the Exhaustion of Taints," will walk to this approach's "arrival" — the last instant of the five higher being severed, which is the threshold of the exhaustion of taints. And P09 is the axis's *seam paper*: it stands shoulder to shoulder with the Attainment axis's *arhat* P01, "The Exhaustion of Taints Is Mastery," at the same instant of the taints' exhaustion — the Realization axis writing "the approach of the last wall's taking-down" (the threshold, the moving aspect), the Attainment axis writing "the opening that the exhaustion of taints is mastery" (the threshold, the resting aspect). This paper's "severing the five higher" walks, in P09, to the threshold; the mastery beyond the threshold is handed to the Attainment axis. The subtraction's last step and mastery's first step are the two faces of one and the same step.
