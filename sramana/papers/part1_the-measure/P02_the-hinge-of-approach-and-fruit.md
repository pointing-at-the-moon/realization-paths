# The Hinge of Approach and Fruit

**Why the Four Pairs and Eight Persons Are Two Phases of One Subtraction**

*中文主題：向果相扣——四雙八輩何以是減法的兩個相位*

## Abstract

The preceding paper established that the four śrāmaṇya-phalas are measured by severance — a subtraction. This paper asks after that subtraction's inner grammar: why are the four fruits not four isolated marks, but unfold as the *four pairs and eight persons* (cattāri purisayugāni, aṭṭha purisapuggalā) — four approaches, four fruits, eight kinds of noble one? The thesis: *approach* and *fruit* are two phases of one and the same severing. The approach is the dynamic *cutting* — the wall coming down; the fruit is the static *establishment* — the wall standing down. Hinged one before the other, they form the minimal unit of the subtraction. Scripturally, the *Saṃyukta-* and *Madhyama-āgamas* list the eight as paired approach/fruit in so many words. The fine mechanics of the turn — "upon the sixteenth mind, the knowledge-of-the-type-of-the-path, one is called 'abiding in the fruit,' no longer 'approaching'" — is by contrast chiefly elaborated in the *Abhidharmakośa*; this paper marks that difference of register honestly. It further argues that the *Kośa*'s "the names are eight but the substances five" discloses the hinge's deep sense: the latter three fruits' approaches are not separate from the prior fruit, so eight names reduce to five substances — proof that approach and fruit are not two things joined, but the moving and resting faces of a single severance. Conclusion: the eight persons are the *joint* of the subtraction, and the approach-fruit hinge is the grammar of the fruit-by-fruit walk to come.

**Keywords**: four pairs and eight persons, four approaches and four fruits, approach, fruit, eight noble persons, sixteenth path-moment, abiding-in-fruit, Abhidharmakośa, subtraction, severing fetters, unconditioned cessation

## I. The Question

The preceding paper moved the wager from *experience* back to *severance*: the four śrāmaṇya-phalas are a subtraction, measuring what is gone. But if the subtraction were merely four isolated marks — three fetters, five lower, five higher, all exhausted — it would resemble four snapshots more than a road one walks. Yet the canon does not stop at four fruits; it tells the same road as *four pairs and eight persons*: stream-enterer-approach and stream-enterer-fruit, once-returner-approach and once-returner-fruit, and so on — one approach, one fruit, four pairs, eight kinds. Why eight, not four? This doubling — where, exactly, does the extra half lie?

A common reading takes the *approach* as a "preparatory class" for the fruit — a student not yet passed, still working, not yet holding the certificate. The reading is not wrong, but it is too weak: it casts the approach-fruit distinction as a *quantitative* gap between "not yet enough" and "now enough," as if the approach were a diluted fruit. If so, the distinction would be of little consequence — why would the canon solemnly establish eight?

This paper presses a stronger reading: **approach and fruit are two phases of one and the same severing.** The approach is not "a fruit not yet reached," but the *progressive aspect* of the act of severing — the wall in the instant of coming down; the fruit is the *completed aspect* of that same act — the wall now standing down. The two differ not in degree but in mode, moving versus resting, hinged one before the other; only together do they make one complete *joint* of the subtraction. To take down a wall is necessarily to have both a "coming-down" and a "down"; four principal walls yield four sets of "coming-down / down" — and that is eight. The extra half is not four more marks, but the *temporal aspect* each mark carries: the subtraction is not an instantaneous switch but an *event*, with an "in-the-midst" and an "after."

Establishing this bears directly on how the remaining seven papers of this axis proceed. From Paper 3 onward, each fruit-by-fruit treatment is in fact the treatment of an *approach-fruit joint*: the approach of entering the stream (the first three fetters being severed) and the fruit of entering the stream (the first three fetters severed and abiding). Without first making plain the grammar of the hinge, the fruit-by-fruit walk would degenerate into a row of four snapshots. This paper is the second foundation stone of Part 1 — the first established *that it is a subtraction*; this one establishes *how the subtraction is jointed*.

The argument: Section II presents the scriptural basis, showing how the Āgamas list the eight as paired approach/fruit in so many words; Section III, the core argument, establishes in three layers why approach and fruit are moving-and-resting aspects rather than two things (the temporal structure of the hinge; the *Kośa*'s "eight names, five substances" as internal proof of the hinge; and the grammatical significance for the fruit-by-fruit walk from Part 2 on); Section IV again omits interdisciplinary convergence, for the reason in Section VI; Section V offers middle-way corrections; Section VI concludes.

## II. The Canonical Basis

### 2.1 The Āgamas, in so many words: four pairs and eight persons, approach paired with fruit

The name "four pairs and eight persons" and its paired approach/fruit structure are stated outright in the Āgamas, not a later treatise-construction. *Saṃyukta-āgama*, fascicle 20:

> 謂向須陀洹、得須陀洹果，向斯陀含、得斯陀含，向阿那含、得阿那含，向阿羅漢、得阿羅漢。如是四雙八士，是名世尊弟子僧。[^1]
>
> *"That is: approaching-the-stream-enterer and gaining-the-stream-enterer's-fruit; approaching-the-once-returner and gaining-the-once-returner; approaching-the-non-returner and gaining-the-non-returner; approaching-the-arahant and gaining-the-arahant. Such are the four pairs, eight persons — this is called the Blessed One's community of disciples."*

The sentence-form is itself a fossil of the thesis: "approach X, gain X-fruit," paired rank by rank, four pairs complete, named "four pairs, eight persons." The canon does not rest with "four fruits"; it insists on the double label "approach / gain" — a doubling that is no rhetorical surplus but the rendering of each fruit as a pair of *trending-toward* and *having-gained*.

The same structure appears in the *Madhyama-āgama*, with two recensions mutually confirming it. Fascicle 30: 「彼眾實有阿羅訶、趣阿羅訶，有阿那含、趣阿那含，有斯陀含、趣斯陀含，有須陀洹、趣須陀洹，是謂四雙八輩」 — *"…there are the arahant and the one trending-to-arahantship, the non-returner and the one trending-to-non-return… these are called the four pairs and eight persons."*[^2] Fascicle 55 marks it still more sharply with "trending / fruit-realized": 「實有阿羅訶真人趣、阿羅訶果證、阿那含趣、阿那含果證、斯陀含趣、斯陀含果證、須陀洹趣、須陀洹果證，是為四雙人八輩聖士」 — where 趣 ("trending") is the approach and 果證 ("fruit-realized") is the fruit.[^3] Three recensions, one structure of approach paired with fruit.

Worth noting: all three passages stand in the *recollection-of-the-Saṅgha* (saṅghānussati) context — the eight persons are enumerated as the *content* of "the Blessed One's community of disciples." Even in the earliest scriptural testimony, the Saṅgha-jewel is a field of merit precisely because its members are these sets of "trending-toward / having-gained" noble ones. Approach paired with fruit is the *structure of the Saṅgha as Saṅgha*, not a merely scholastic taxonomy.

### 2.2 The treatises' elaboration: the fine mechanics of the approach-fruit turn

The Āgamas establish the *structure* of approach-paired-with-fruit outright; but the fine mechanics of *how an approach turns into a fruit* — in which very instant that turn falls — are chiefly elaborated by Abhidharma. *Abhidharmakośa*, fascicle 23 (Chapter on the Noble Ones):

> 即前隨信隨法行者，至第十六道類智心名為住果，不復名向。隨前三向今住三果，謂前預流向今住預流果，前一來向今住一來果，前不還向今住不還果。[^4]
>
> *"The former follower-by-faith and follower-by-Dharma, upon reaching the sixteenth mind — the knowledge-of-the-type-of-the-path — is called 'abiding in the fruit,' no longer 'approaching.' The prior three approaches now abide as three fruits: the prior stream-approach now abides as the stream-fruit; the prior once-return-approach as the once-return-fruit; the prior non-return-approach as the non-return-fruit."*

The commentator anchors that turn at the "sixteenth mind, the knowledge-of-the-type-of-the-path" within the path of seeing: before it, "approach"; at this one instant, "abiding in the fruit," no longer approach; the prior three approaches, in this single mind-moment, turn into the three fruits. This pins the phase-transition of "coming-down / down" to the level of a single moment of consciousness.

> ⚠️ A note on register: the pairing of approach and fruit (2.1) is stated outright in the Āgamas; the *fine localization* of the turn at the "sixteenth mind" (2.2) is Abhidharma's reasoned elaboration upon the Āgama frame, not stated by the Āgamas themselves. This paper draws on the treatises' analysis here, but marks honestly: it is the commentators' legitimate development of the scriptural frame, weighted as treatise, not sūtra. The moving/resting analysis of the core argument below abides by this same boundary.

## III. The Core Argument

### 3.1 The temporal structure of the hinge: two aspects, moving and resting — not two grades of degree

To read the *approach* as a "diluted fruit" errs by understanding through *quantity of degree* a distinction that is really one of *aspect*. To sever a fetter is not a continuous slider from 0% to 100%, counting as "fruit" only past some mark; it is an *event* with an "in-the-midst" and an "after." The approach is the event's progressive aspect — the path arising, the fetter in the instant of being cut; the fruit is the same event's completed aspect — the fetter severed, its unconditioned cessation-through-discrimination now established and abiding (this cessation being the "substance of the fruit" of the previous paper, §3.1).

The crux: the aspect of the fruit is "the cessation having-been-established," and cessation is *unconditioned*. The unconditioned admits no "such-and-such percent" — a wall is not "70% taken down" while half of it still stands; the cessation, as cessation, is the *establishment* of the fact that this fetter is severed — either established or not. So the approach-fruit distinction is, at root, not the magnitude of "how much severed" but the moving-and-resting *aspects* of "the event of severing." The approach is the moving aspect (severing-in-progress); the fruit the resting aspect (severed-and-abiding). This is also why the canon must list the eight under a double label: the demolition of each wall inherently contains the two indispensable aspects of "coming-down" and "down"; lacking either, the event "demolishing the wall" is incomplete.

An ordinary analogy: erasing a line of writing from a blackboard. "Erasing" and "erased clean" are not "50% erased" and "100% erased" as degrees — "erased clean" is the establishment of a state (that line is no longer there); "erasing" is the act trending toward that establishment. You would not say "I have reached the 80%-fruit of that line"; you would say "I am erasing it (approach); I have finished erasing it (fruit)." The hinge of approach and fruit is the hinge of an act and its completed state.

### 3.2 "Eight names, five substances": the internal proof of the hinge

If approach and fruit are truly "moving and resting aspects" rather than "two things joined," there should be a trace within the canon to follow. The *Abhidharmakośa*, fascicle 24, supplies exactly that trace:

> 〔有學及無學者〕總成八聖補特伽羅，行向住果各有四故……名雖有八事唯有五，謂住四果及初果向，以後三果向不離前果故。[^5]
>
> *"[The trainees and the one-beyond-training] total eight noble persons, since 'practising-the-approach' and 'abiding-in-the-fruit' each number four… Though the names are eight, the substances are only five — namely, abiding in the four fruits and the first fruit's approach — because the latter three fruits' approaches are not separate from the prior fruit."*

"Though the names are eight, the substances are only five" — eight designations, but in fact only five items. Why do eight collapse into five? Because "the latter three fruits' approaches are not separate from the prior fruit": the once-returner-approach is not separate from the stream-fruit, the non-returner-approach not separate from the once-return-fruit, the arahant-approach not separate from the non-return-fruit — the "trending-toward" of the later fruit rests precisely *upon* the "having-gained" of the earlier. So eight names (four approaches + four fruits) hinge down into five substances (four fruits + only the first approach standing alone).

This "eight collapsing into five" is the hard proof of the hinge. Were approach and fruit two mutually independent things, eight would be eight, with no ground to collapse to five. They can collapse precisely because each adjacent "prior fruit" and "later approach" are two faces of one place: for one standing on the stream-fruit, his "having-severed the first three fetters and abiding" (the resting aspect of the fruit) is at the same time the foothold of his "trending toward the thinning of greed-hatred-delusion" (the moving aspect of the next approach). The rest of the prior fruit *is* the support of the next approach; moving and resting interlock here. "Eight names, five substances" — the number itself is saying: approach and fruit are not a two that was assembled, but joint after interlocking joint of one subtraction.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: "eight names, five substances" is no mere counting coincidence but the internal corroboration of the consubstantiality of approach and fruit. That the latter three fruits' approaches are not separate from the prior fruit shows that "the fruit established" and "the next approach arising" are two interlocking faces of one severing-process — the subtraction is not four isolated points but a chain of interlocking links.

### 3.3 The grammatical significance for the fruit-by-fruit walk

With approach and fruit established as moving-and-resting aspects, the remaining seven papers of this axis acquire a unified grammar. From Paper 3 on, each fruit is no longer a snapshot but an *approach-fruit joint*: first its approach (the moving aspect of a fetter being severed, why this fetter falls first), then its fruit (the establishment of that fetter's cessation, and its mark of non-regress). So with the stream (the severing and the severed of the first three fetters), so with the once-returner (the thinning-in-progress and the thinned of greed-hatred-delusion), so with the non-returner (the exhausting and the exhausted of the five lower fetters), through to the arahant-approach (the severing of the five higher fetters, Paper 8) and the approach-to-exhaustion-of-taints (reaching the threshold, Paper 9).

One point must be drawn out: why Paper 9, "the approach to the exhaustion of taints," is named *approach* and not *fruit*. Precisely because the arahant-fruit's "mastery" (the liberation of mind and wisdom, the three knowledges and six higher knowledges, disclosed by the fruit's resting aspect) belongs to the Attainment axis, *arhat*. The Realization axis, *sramana*, walks only to the moving aspect of "the five higher fetters being severed, the taints about to be exhausted," and there halts — at the threshold. The very pair of categories, approach and fruit, is thus the grammatical ground of the seam between the two axes: the Realization axis writes to the *approach* of the final fruit (the moving aspect, the severing), and the Attainment axis takes up the *fruit* of the final fruit (the resting aspect, the mastery). The hinge established in this paper therefore serves not only the fruit-by-fruit walk of Parts 2–5, but also pre-lays that seam between Paper 9 and the Attainment axis.

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

**❌ Misreading 1: "Approach = a fruit at the passing margin."** The whole argument of this paper is the dismantling of this misreading. The approach is not "a fruit short of degree" but the moving aspect of the severing-event; the fruit is not "degree now sufficient" but the resting aspect of cessation established. To understand approach and fruit through quantitative gap (how many tenths severed) misconstrues the unconditioned cessation as a conditioned process divisible into percentages — a root misplacement, the very variant of "measuring fruit by experience" the previous paper rebutted (imagining the fruit as something accumulable and gradable).

**⚠️ Correction 2: The fine localization at the "sixteenth path-moment" is treatise, not sūtra, and must not be read back as scriptural.** Approach paired with fruit is stated outright in the Āgamas (2.1), but its anchoring at the sixteenth of the path-of-seeing's sixteen minds is Abhidharma's fine construction (2.2). This paper draws on that analysis to specify the "point" of the moving-to-resting turn, but the exposition must not read it back into the Āgamas as the Buddha's express statement. The commentators' elaboration has its own value and consistency; only let each keep its place — scripture establishes the structure, the treatise elaborates the mechanism.

**⚠️ Correction 3: The hinge of approach and fruit does not entail "the fruit can regress to the approach."** The two-aspects account might raise the doubt: since they are two faces of one event, could the fruit "regress" to the approach? It cannot. The previous paper, §3.3, established it: cessation is unconditioned, the acquisition-of-severance does not regress; "abiding in the fruit, no longer approaching" (cited 2.2) is itself the express statement of directional irreversibility. The moving-to-resting turn is one-way — once a wall is taken down for good, it does not "regress to being-taken-down." The hinge of approach and fruit is a hinge of "moving first, then resting," not a ring in which moving and resting interconvert. Establishing the hinge does not loosen the non-regress established by the previous paper.

## VI. Conclusion

**Essential restatement.** The four pairs and eight persons are not an ornamental doubling of the four fruits, but the *jointing grammar* of the subtraction: approach and fruit are the moving and resting aspects of one and the same severing — the approach the moving aspect of severing-in-progress, the fruit the resting aspect of cessation established — hinged one before the other, forming the minimal joint of the subtraction. The Āgamas list the eight as paired approach/fruit outright (2.1); the *Kośa*'s collapse of "eight names, five substances" internally proves the consubstantiality of approach and fruit, the later approach not separate from the prior fruit (3.2); while the localization of the turn at the "sixteenth path-moment" is the treatises' fine elaboration of this structure, whose register as treatise this paper marks honestly (2.2, §V-Correction 2).

**Guidance for practice.** In taking stock of himself, beyond asking "which wall has come down" (the previous paper), the practitioner may further divide two questions: "which wall am I now taking down" (approach) and "which wall stands down for good, never to be rebuilt" (fruit). The former discerns where present effort lies (the moving aspect); the latter confirms the non-regress of what is accomplished (the resting aspect). For one long dwelling within an "approach," not yet seeing the mark of a "fruit" and so growing faint-hearted, this paper offers a settling: you stand in the moving aspect of a given fetter's "being severed" — this is not "not yet passing," but the progressive aspect inherent to the subtraction-event; the wall is coming down, and the resting aspect of its standing-down will follow of itself.

**Open questions.** This paper cut Section IV because, though the "moving / resting" of approach and fruit formally resembles the linguistic aspect-opposition of "progressive / perfective," what aspect qualifies is the temporal phase of a worldly event, whereas approach and fruit mark the establishment-or-not of an unconditioned cessation — alike in face, unlike in spirit; a forced convergence would misrepresent, so better omitted. A genuine question for later: does the moving/resting division of approach and fruit require importing a temporal theory of "moment" (kṣaṇa) and "continuum" (saṃtāna) to be fully grounded? This touches the *Kośa*'s theory of time, beyond the present paper's measuring-theme, and is left to a later paper of this axis or elsewhere. Further, the "approach-fruit hinge" grammar set here must be tested in the fruit-by-fruit treatment from Part 2 on: can the approach and fruit of stream-entry really be jointed cleanly as "the severing / the severed of the first three fetters"? And how does the "thinning of greed-hatred-delusion" (once-returner) — a mark of *not-total* severance — fit the moving/resting frame? This is exactly what the papers from Paper 3 on must verify one by one.
