# Not a Continuation of Arhatship

**Why the Bodhisattva Path Is Entered by Bodhicitta, Not by Completed Arhatship**

*中文主題：菩薩道不從阿羅漢果接續——發菩提心為大乘唯一入門*

## Abstract

A widely held picture imagines the road to buddhahood as a single track: first complete the four śrāmaṇya-phalas and become an arahant, then "turn from the lesser to the greater" (回小向大) and join the bodhisattva path on toward buddhahood. This paper argues that the picture runs head-on against the canon. In the *Yogācārabhūmi* (chapters on the Arising of the Mind and on Lineage), the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, and the *Avataṃsaka-sūtra*, the sole entry to the Mahāyāna bodhisattva path is the arising of bodhicitta: the bodhisattva's first arising of the mind has the "first right vow" for its very nature, and once it arises he is already said to have *entered* unsurpassed awakening and to be *counted among* the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle; the bodhisattva "passes beyond the grounds of the śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha and enters the bodhisattva position," surpassing all of the two vehicles *from the first arising of the mind*, and though he practises the real-limit he does not realize it (不作證). The three witnesses agree: the bodhisattva path and the four fruits are two *parallel* tracks, not a sequence; one need not first attain arhatship to enter. The paper presses further: the two tracks diverge *from the first moment of aspiration* — the śrāvaka first aspires only to escape the suffering of aging-sickness-death, whereas the bodhisattva first aspires with great vows and great compassion, "so that the lineage of the Tathāgata not be cut off." Their measures differ accordingly: not the *subtraction* of "which fetters are severed," but the *accumulation* of bodhicitta, the two provisions and six perfections, and the non-abiding of "long due for nirvāṇa yet not taking its realization." The conclusion: the independence of the bodhisattva path is not an upgrade upon arhatship, but a distinct vehicle forked from the very first arising of the mind.

**Keywords**: bodhisattva path, bodhicitta, bodhisattva-gotra, four śrāmaṇya-phalas, turning from lesser to greater, two parallel tracks, not-taking-realization, Yogācārabhūmi, Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra, Avataṃsaka, merit of the first arising of the mind, non-abiding nirvāṇa

## I. The Question

Picture a practitioner who has climbed the subtractive ladder of the four śrāmaṇya-phalas: three fetters severed for stream-entry, greed and hatred thinned for once-return, the five lower fetters exhausted for non-return, the taints ended for arahantship. Someone now tells him: "You have reached the end of the śrāvaka road; from here you 'turn from the lesser to the greater' (回小向大) and join the bodhisattva path." As if the bodhisattva path were a second storey built atop arahantship — as if one had first to finish the four fruits on the ground floor before there could be any talk of ascending.

The picture is so common that "first arahant, then bodhisattva" has become an almost unexamined default. Yet the canon presents another kind of person: one who may never have severed a single fetter, but who, in some moment, seeing beings in suffering, cannot rest, and so gives rise to the vow *to seek unsurpassed awakening for the sake of liberating all beings*. In that very instant, the texts say, he "is already said to have entered unsurpassed awakening, and is counted among the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle." He has not climbed a single rung of the four fruits, and yet he already stands on the bodhisattva path. So where is the entrance to that path — at the top of the arahant's storey, or at this single arising of the mind?

The thesis of this paper is one sentence, and it collides with the default head-on: **the bodhisattva path is not a continuation of arhatship; its sole entry is the arising of bodhicitta. From the very first arising of the mind it surpasses the two vehicles and runs as a track parallel to the four fruits, not as a second leg appended to them.** The condition for entering the bodhisattva path is not "having completed the four fruits" but "having given rise to bodhicitta" — that, and nothing more. The one who has severed no fetter but has aroused the great mind, the canon admits as a bodhisattva; the one whose four fruits are complete but who has not aroused the great mind, the canon does not, by the mere height of his fruit, automatically admit as a bodhisattva. The criterion lies in the mind, not in the fruit.

This is not a sectarian quarrel. It bears on how the entire Mahāyāna road is understood. If the bodhisattva path were the sequel to arahantship, the Great Vehicle would be an extension of the Lesser — the practitioner would first walk a subtractive road of dismantling, then turn back to the starting line; and the independence of the Mahāyāna, indeed everything by which it is said to be "not shared with the two vehicles," would have no place to stand. If, instead, the bodhisattva path forks off independently from the arising of the mind, then from the outset it is another measure, another goal, a vehicle held by another lineage — it may share certain grammar of severing defilement with the four fruits (the ground-attained bodhisattva, too, severs defilement), yet it does not take the four fruits as its prior stage. The whole of Division 2 of this axis, *The Bodhi Path*, all ten papers, rests on this independence of "forking from the arising of the mind." The present paper is the foundation stone: its task is to make plain *why it is not a continuation, and why the arising of the mind is itself the entry.*

The argument proceeds in three moves. Section II lays out the canonical basis — how the *Yogācārabhūmi*, the *Śāstra*, and the *Avataṃsaka* *positively* make the arising of the mind the entry, and establish the two parallel tracks by "passing beyond the two-vehicle grounds and entering the bodhisattva position." Section III is the core argument, establishing the independence in three layers: the difference of the entry condition (aspiration, not severance), the difference of goal and measure (the accumulation of not-taking-realization, not the subtraction of taking-realization), and the difference of lineage (superior from the first, not later overtaking). Section IV would ordinarily turn to interdisciplinary convergence; here I deliberately omit it, for reasons given in Section VI. Section V offers middle-way corrections. Section VI concludes and marks the seam shared with the Attainment-axis paper BODHI-P01.

## II. The Canonical Basis

### 2.1 The arising of the mind is the entry: once it arises, one has entered unsurpassed awakening and is counted among the bodhisattvas

The most direct passage defining the *entry condition* stands in the *Yogācārabhūmi*, in the Chapter on the Arising of the Mind (Bodhisattva-bhūmi). The treatise speaks directly of the nature and function of "arousing the mind":

> 復次，菩薩最初發心，於諸菩薩所有正願是初正願，普能攝受其餘正願，是故發心以初正願為其自性。……又諸菩薩初發心已，即名趣入無上菩提，預在大乘諸菩薩數。此據世俗言說道理，是故發心趣入所攝。又諸菩薩要發心已，方能漸次速證無上正等菩提，非未發心，是故發心能為無上菩提根本。[^1]
>
> *"The bodhisattva's first arising of the mind is, among all the bodhisattva's right vows, the first right vow, and it embraces all the rest; therefore the arising of the mind has the first right vow for its nature. … And once the bodhisattva has first aroused the mind, he is at once said to have entered unsurpassed awakening and to be counted among the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle. … And only after arousing the mind can he gradually and swiftly realize unsurpassed perfect awakening, not without arousing it; therefore the arising of the mind can be the root of unsurpassed awakening."*

Note the three verbs: *enter*, *be counted among*, *root*. Once the mind is aroused, one *has entered* unsurpassed awakening — the threshold of entry lies at this single move. Once the mind is aroused, one *is counted among* the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle — belonging, too, is fixed at this single move. And the arising of the mind *is the root* of unsurpassed awakening — without it there is no gradual realization. Taken together, the treatise ties "entering the Great Vehicle" cleanly to the arising of the mind, and not to any prior fruit.

What such arising looks like, the *Avataṃsaka* (Chapter on the Ten Abodes) states concretely, at the very first abode, the Abode of Arousing the Mind:

> 佛子！云何為菩薩發心住？此菩薩見佛世尊形貌端嚴，色相圓滿，人所樂見，難可值遇，有大威力；或見神足；或聞記別；或聽教誡；或見眾生受諸劇苦；或聞如來廣大佛法，發菩提心，求一切智。[^2]
>
> *"O sons of the Buddha, what is the bodhisattva's Abode of Arousing the Mind? This bodhisattva sees the World-Honoured One's form upright and adorned … of great majestic power; or sees His spiritual feats; or hears prophecies; or listens to His instructions; or sees beings undergoing acute suffering; or hears the Tathāgata's vast Dharma — and arouses the mind of awakening, seeking omniscience."*

The condition of arousing is seeing the Buddha's majesty, hearing the vast Dharma, or seeing beings in acute suffering; and the *content* of the arousing is "seeking omniscience" (一切智, *sarvajñatā*) — to seek the Buddha's knowledge of all, not one's own escape. Once this thought arises, the bodhisattva enters the Abode of Arousing the Mind, the *first* of the ten abodes. The first rung of the bodhisattva's stations, in other words, has the arising of bodhicitta itself for its entry-mark — not "the four fruits complete."

The root motive of the arousing, the *Śāstra* assigns to compassion:

> 慈悲是佛道之根本。所以者何？菩薩見眾生老病死苦、身苦心苦、今世後世苦等諸苦所惱，生大慈、悲，救如是苦，然後發心求阿耨多羅三藐三菩提。亦以大慈悲力故，於無量阿僧祇世生死中，心不厭沒；以大慈悲力故，久應得涅槃而不取證。[^3]
>
> *"Compassion is the root of the Buddha-path. Why? The bodhisattva, seeing beings tormented by the sufferings of aging, sickness, death, bodily and mental pain, the pains of this life and the next, gives rise to great loving-kindness and compassion, and to rescue such suffering then arouses the mind seeking anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. And by the power of great compassion, through immeasurable asaṃkhyeya lifetimes of birth-and-death his mind does not weary or sink; by the power of great compassion, though long due for nirvāṇa, he does not take its realization."*

The decisive clause is the last: the bodhisattva, "though long due for nirvāṇa, does not take its realization" (久應得涅槃而不取證). One whose root is compassion, even arrived at the point where nirvāṇa is his to take, does not take it. This is the pivot of Section III's "difference of goal"; for now, register only this: the *cause* of arousing is great compassion, and its *direction* is not-taking-realization — both heterogeneous to the śrāvaka's "seek escape from one's own suffering, take the realization of nirvāṇa."

### 2.2 Two parallel tracks: passing beyond the two-vehicle grounds into the bodhisattva position, not falling to the śrāvaka ground

If "arising as entry" sets the bodhisattva path's *starting point*, "two parallel tracks" sets its *relation* to the śrāvaka road — parallel, not sequential. The *Śāstra*, fascicle 23, poses the sharp question: since the bodhisattva fully possesses the thirty-seven factors (which the śrāvaka cultivates), why does he not fall into the śrāvaka's fruit?

> 問曰：若菩薩具足三十七品諸法者，云何不入聲聞法位？答曰：「具足」者，具足觀知而不取證，了了觀知，故名「具足」。……復次，我先說：「不捨眾生，以不可得空智和合故，不墮聲聞地。」[^4]
>
> *"Question: If the bodhisattva fully possesses the thirty-seven factors, how is it he does not enter the śrāvaka's stage? Answer: 'Fully possesses' means he fully contemplates and knows yet does not take realization; he contemplates and knows with full clarity — hence 'fully possesses.' … Again, as I said before: 'Not abandoning beings, conjoined with the wisdom of unattainable emptiness, he does not fall to the śrāvaka ground.'"*

The answer is exact: the bodhisattva's "full possession" of the factors is "full contemplation and knowing *without taking realization*." The very same contemplative wisdom — by which the śrāvaka takes realization and enters the śrāvaka stage — the bodhisattva fully knows yet does not take to realization, "conjoined with not abandoning beings and the wisdom of unattainable emptiness," and so does not fall to the śrāvaka ground. The two vehicles overlap (the same path-factors, the same contemplation of emptiness), yet their entry and exit fork from the start: one takes realization and enters the śrāvaka position; the other contemplates without realizing, holding the vow and not falling. This is not "first enter the śrāvaka, then convert to bodhisattva," but two tracks diverging, by what is taken and what is declined, on one and the same field of contemplative wisdom.

Why do they diverge? The *Śāstra*, fascicle 18, points the root to the difference *at the first arising of the mind*:

> 菩薩摩訶薩知是諸法已，能令諸法入自性空，而於諸法無所著，過聲聞、辟支佛地，入菩薩位中……諸阿羅漢、辟支佛初發心時，無大願，無大慈大悲，不求一切諸功德……但欲求脫老、病、死苦故。諸菩薩從初發心，弘大誓願，有大慈悲，求一切諸功德，供養一切三世十方諸佛，有大利智，求諸法實相。[^5]
>
> *"The bodhisattva-mahāsattva, having known these dharmas, can bring them into self-nature emptiness, and clinging to nothing, passes beyond the grounds of the śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha and enters the bodhisattva position. … The arhats and pratyekabuddhas, at their first arising of the mind, had no great vow, no great loving-kindness and compassion, sought not all the merits … but only to escape the suffering of aging, sickness, death. The bodhisattvas, from the first arising of the mind, take vast vows, possess great compassion, seek all merits, make offerings to all the buddhas of the three times and ten directions, possess great keen wisdom, and seek the true character of dharmas."*

"Passes beyond the grounds of the śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha and enters the bodhisattva position" — this is the language of *route*: the bodhisattva *passes beyond* the two-vehicle grounds and *enters* the bodhisattva position; he does not *summit* the two-vehicle grounds and continue. And what lets him pass and enter, the treatise ties to the difference *at the first arising*: the arhat and pratyekabuddha, *at their first arising*, sought only to escape the suffering of aging-sickness-death, without great vow or compassion; the bodhisattva, *from his first arising*, takes vast vows, possesses great compassion, seeks all merits and the true character of dharmas. The cut that forks the tracks falls at the first arising of the mind, not at the height of a fruit.

The *Avataṃsaka* (Chapter on the Ten Grounds, the Seventh Ground, Far-Reaching) renders this "superior from the start" most vividly, by the simile of the prince:

> 譬如王子，生在王家，王后所生，具足王相，生已即勝一切臣眾，但以王力，非是自力……菩薩摩訶薩亦復如是，初發心時，以志求大法故，超過一切聲聞、獨覺；今住此地，以自所行智慧力故，出過一切二乘之上。……是故菩薩雖行實際而不作證。[^6]
>
> *"Just as a prince, born into the royal house, borne by the queen, fully bearing the royal marks, from birth surpasses all the ministers — but by the king's power, not his own power … so too the bodhisattva-mahāsattva: at the first arising of the mind, because he aspires to the great Dharma, he surpasses all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas; now abiding on this ground, by the power of his own practised wisdom, he goes beyond all the two vehicles. … Therefore the bodhisattva, though he practises the real-limit, does not realize it."*

The prince surpasses the ministers from birth by "the king's power" (lineage, blood), not by "his own power" after growing into competence. So the bodhisattva: *at the first arising of the mind*, "because he aspires to the great Dharma, he surpasses all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas" — a surpassing of *status*, present from the start, not a later overtaking once some fruit is completed; only at the seventh ground does he further go beyond by "the power of his own practised wisdom." The closing line — "though he practises the real-limit, he does not realize it" (雖行實際而不作證) — sounds the same note as 2.1's "long due for nirvāṇa yet not taking its realization" and 2.2's "fully knows yet does not take realization." Section III will show how these three converge on the single core of independence.

### 2.3 Lineage and the merit of first arising: a superior vehicle in its own right, exceeding the fruits' realization

The independence of the bodhisattva path has a deeper root still — lineage (種姓, *gotra*). The *Yogācārabhūmi*, Chapter on Lineage:

> 云何種姓？謂略有二種。一、本性住種姓，二、習所成種姓。……若諸菩薩成就種姓，尚過一切聲聞、獨覺，何況其餘一切有情。當知種姓無上最勝。……一切聲聞、獨覺種姓，唯能當證煩惱障淨，不能當證所知障淨；菩薩種姓，亦能當證煩惱障淨，亦能當證所知障淨。[^7]
>
> *"What is lineage? In brief, two kinds: the lineage abiding by nature, and the lineage produced by cultivation. … A bodhisattva who has accomplished the lineage surpasses even all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas — how much more all other beings. Know that the lineage is unsurpassed and supreme. … The lineage of all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas can realize only the purity from the affliction-hindrance, not the purity from the cognitive-hindrance; the bodhisattva lineage can realize both the purity from the affliction-hindrance and the purity from the cognitive-hindrance."*

Lineage is the support of the first arising, the capacity-nature fit for buddhahood. The treatise calls the bodhisattva lineage "unsurpassed and supreme," surpassing "all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas" — sinking the independence down to the level of *vehicle-nature*: the bodhisattva is bodhisattva not by later cultivation but by the twofold lineage, abiding-by-nature and produced-by-cultivation. More decisive is the closing discrimination: the two-vehicle lineage realizes only purity from the affliction-hindrance (severing defilement), the bodhisattva lineage both that and purity from the cognitive-hindrance (severing defilement *and* the cognitive obstruction). The fruit sought differs (one purity, the other both), the lineage relied upon differs; so the two are distinct vehicles at the level of lineage itself — let alone a continuation.

The merit of the arising, the *Yogācārabhūmi* (Arising of the Mind) ranks supreme:

> 又諸菩薩最初發心，能攝一切菩提分法殊勝善根為上首故，是善、極善……又諸菩薩最初發心所起正願，於餘一切希求世間、出世間義妙善正願，最為第一，最為無上。……又諸菩薩悲愍一切有苦眾生，為欲濟拔，發菩提心，是故發心是悲等流。[^8]
>
> *"The bodhisattva's first arising of the mind, because it gathers all the excellent roots-of-good of the factors of awakening as its head, is good, supremely good … The right vow set up by the bodhisattva's first arising of the mind is, among all other wholesome right vows that seek worldly and *world-transcending* aims, the very first, the unsurpassed. … And the bodhisattva, pitying all suffering beings and wishing to rescue them, arouses the mind of awakening; therefore the arising of the mind is an out-flow of compassion."*

The first arising is, "among all … *world-transcending* wholesome right vows, the very first, the unsurpassed" — note "world-transcending": even the śrāvaka's vow to transcend the world stands beneath bodhicitta's vow. The arising is an "out-flow of compassion" and the head that gathers all roots-of-good of the awakening-factors. Again: the *starting point* of the bodhisattva path is itself supreme, requiring no four fruits to lay its foundation.

Most forceful by contrast is a passage from the *Avataṃsaka* (Chapter on the Merit of First Arising), which weighs "the merit of the first arising" directly against "the merit of teaching beings to attain the four śrāmaṇya-phalas and even pratyekabuddhahood":

> 此人功德比菩薩初發心功德，百分不及一，千分不及一，百千分不及一，乃至優波尼沙陀分亦不及一。何以故？佛子！一切諸佛初發心時，不但為……教得須陀洹果、斯陀含果、阿那含果、阿羅漢果、辟支佛道故，發菩提心；為令如來種性不斷故……發於無上菩提之心。[^9]
>
> *"This person's merit, compared to the merit of the bodhisattva's first arising of the mind, does not reach a hundredth part, a thousandth part, a hundred-thousandth part, nor even an upaniṣad-fraction of it. Why? O son of the Buddha, all buddhas, at their first arising of the mind, aroused the mind of awakening not merely to … teach [beings] to attain the fruit of stream-entry, once-return, non-return, arahantship, and pratyekabuddhahood; but so that the lineage of the Tathāgata not be cut off … they aroused the mind of unsurpassed awakening."*

The sutra sets, at one pole, the merit of giving every comfort to immeasurable beings *and* teaching them to attain in sequence stream-entry up to arahantship and pratyekabuddhahood; at the other pole, the bodhisattva's *first arising*; and the former "does not reach a hundredth … nor even an upaniṣad-fraction" of the latter. Why should the merit of arising so vastly exceed the merit of "teaching others to attain the four fruits"? The sutra answers: the buddhas' first arising was "not merely" to teach beings to attain the four fruits and pratyekabuddhahood (that much the śrāvaka road can accomplish), but "so that the lineage of the Tathāgata not be cut off." That clause — "so that the lineage of the Tathāgata not be cut off" — is the mark of bodhicitta's independent goal: it does not stop at delivering beings from suffering to the four fruits (the work of the śrāvaka road); it wants the *unbroken lineage of the Tathāgata*, the continuity of buddhas. The goal being this, the merit of arising naturally lies outside the magnitude of the fruits.

## III. The Core Argument

Section II has established that "arising as entry, two parallel tracks, lineage superior in itself" is the plain word of the canon. This section answers the deeper question: in which three things does the independence of the bodhisattva path consist, such that it cannot be a sequel to arahantship? In three layers.

### 3.1 Difference of entry condition: arising is not severance — one thought enters, no prior stage required

The four śrāmaṇya-phalas are a subtraction; their entry condition is *severance*: no stream-enterer without three fetters severed. The bodhisattva path's entry condition is not severance but arising: one who has severed no fetter yet has aroused bodhicitta is already counted among the bodhisattvas (2.1). These are two gates of unlike kind — one keyed to "what has been dismantled," the other to "what has been aroused."

Because the entry conditions are of unlike kind, the "first arahant, then bodhisattva" picture cannot stand. Continuation presupposes that the bodhisattva path's gate sits at the *end* of the four fruits, openable only by swiping a "severance complete" card; but the canon sets that gate at the *arising of the mind*, openable by swiping a "bodhicitta aroused" card. One who has never swiped a severance card opens the gate the moment he swipes the arising card. The gates being unlike, there is no "finish the former road before entering the latter."

A homely contrast. A driving licence is a subtractive qualification: you must pass the modules, get your demerits to zero, before you earn the right to drive — no shortcut, the later stage waiting on the earlier. But "resolving to become a doctor" is another matter entirely: it is not "first finish every driving licence, then you may resolve" — the resolve is constituted in the moment of the vow, wholly unrelated to whether you hold a licence. To read the bodhisattva path as "a continuation exam after arahantship" is like saying "you must pass every driving licence before you are entitled to resolve to be a doctor" — mistaking a matter of vow for a matter of accumulated qualification. Arising is a resolve, not a continuation exam; one thought enters it, no prior stage required.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: "Once the mind is aroused, one is said to have entered unsurpassed awakening and is counted among the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle" (*Yogācārabhūmi*, Arising of the Mind) is the treatise's *positive definition* of the entry condition, and its predicate is "arising," not "severance" or "fruit attained." So at the level of definition, entry to the bodhisattva path does not hang on completing the four fruits.

### 3.2 Difference of goal and measure: the accumulation of not-taking-realization, not the subtraction of taking-realization

The second layer is deeper. Granted the entry conditions differ — might the bodhisattva path simply "change the doorway and walk the same subtractive road of severance"? It does not. The canon thrice seals off this reading with one and the same construction: the bodhisattva, "long due for nirvāṇa, does not take its realization" (*Śāstra*, 2.1); "fully knows yet does not take realization" (*Śāstra*, 2.2); "though he practises the real-limit, does not realize it" (*Avataṃsaka*, 2.2). The shared structure of all three: *arrived at the very point of taking realization, he does not take it.*

This "not taking realization" is the deepest cut between the two tracks. The śrāvaka road's goal is *to take realization* — to realize nirvāṇa, exhaust the taints, come no more to being; its measure is subtraction, counting "which fetters are severed." The bodhisattva path's goal is *non-abiding* — even fully contemplating, even practising the real-limit, even long due for nirvāṇa, he still, by great compassion and great vow, does not take its realization, so that "through immeasurable asaṃkhyeya lifetimes of birth-and-death his mind does not weary or sink," continuing to deliver beings, continuing to accumulate the two provisions and six perfections. Its measure is therefore not subtraction but *accumulation*: counting "what mind was aroused, what provisions were amassed, for whose sake the available realization was declined."

So even where the two tracks share some grammar of severing defilement, they remain distinct tracks. The ground-attained bodhisattva, too, severs defilement — a point the later papers of this division (the Ten Grounds) will treat in detail — but though both "sever," their frames are opposite: the śrāvaka takes severance as *the accomplishment of the goal* (severed in full, he takes realization and exits), the bodhisattva takes severance as *an instrument for delivering beings* (severs yet does not take realization, does not exit; the mechanism of "retaining a trace of defilement to moisten rebirth" is founded just here). The same act reads, on the subtractive ledger, as "accounts settled, leave the field"; on the accumulative ledger, as "obstruction cleared, continue the work." Different ledgers, different roads.

Again a contrast. Two people each pay off a debt in full (both "sever"). One pays it off in order to "be debt-free and retire from the field" — that is taking-realization-and-exiting. The other pays it off in order to "restore his credit and go on borrowing, expand the enterprise, support more people"; he could plainly settle up and rest (long due for nirvāṇa), yet does not (and does not take realization), because his goal was never "debtlessness" but "running the enterprise." Their act of repayment is identical; the direction of their lives is opposite. So with the bodhisattva's severing of defilement and the śrāvaka's severing of fetters: the act may be the same, the goal and the measure opposite from the start.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the three clauses of "not taking / not realizing" (twice in the *Śāstra*, once in the *Avataṃsaka*'s Ten Grounds) jointly show that the bodhisattva path's target is not "the realization of nirvāṇa felt" but "the accumulation of delivering beings in non-abiding." The target differing from the śrāvaka road's, the two are not two legs of one road but two tracks of two goals.

### 3.3 Difference of lineage: superior from the start, not later overtaking

The third layer returns to the deepest point — vehicle-nature. The "first arahant, then bodhisattva" picture assumes, at bottom, that the bodhisattva is "an arahant who studied further," as if one of the same lineage, having cultivated high enough, were promoted from śrāvaka to bodhisattva. The canon denies it: the bodhisattva has a lineage of his own, "unsurpassed and supreme, surpassing even all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas" (*Yogācārabhūmi*, Lineage, 2.3).

The prince simile (2.2) makes this plainest: the prince "from birth surpasses all the ministers, but by the king's power, not his own power." No minister, however able, is "promoted" to prince by dint of merit — princehood lies in blood and lineage, not in accumulated achievement. So the bodhisattva: "at the first arising of the mind, because he aspires to the great Dharma, he surpasses all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas" — a surpassing of *status*, possessed from the start, not a continuation-ascent after the four fruits are filled. Add the Lineage chapter's discrimination: the two-vehicle lineage realizes only purity from the affliction-hindrance, the bodhisattva lineage both that and purity from the cognitive-hindrance — what each can sever differs, the fruit each tends toward differs (one liberation, the other omniscience); so they are two vehicles at the very level of lineage. Whence, then, a "continuation by which one lineage rises from low to high"?

This is why the Chapter on the Merit of First Arising dares to say: the merit of teaching beings to attain, throughout, the four śrāmaṇya-phalas and even pratyekabuddhahood does not reach a hundred-thousandth part of the merit of the bodhisattva's first arising (2.3). If the bodhisattva path were truly the sequel to arahantship, the first arising would be at most "the prologue to the sequel" — how could it instead exceed the consummate merit of the whole "main text"? The only coherent reading is: what the first arising belongs to is, from the outset, another magnitude, a vehicle held by another lineage — it exceeds the four fruits not because it travels farther than they, but because it was never on their axis to begin with.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the twofold lineage (abiding-by-nature / produced-by-cultivation) and the discrimination "the bodhisattva lineage also realizes purity from the cognitive-hindrance" (*Yogācārabhūmi*, Lineage) establish, at the level of vehicle-nature, the independence of the bodhisattva path — not an upgrade of śrāvaka cultivation, but a distinct vehicle held by a distinct lineage.

Taken together, the three layers — entry by arising not severance (one thought enters, no prior stage); goal as the accumulation of not-taking-realization, not the subtraction of taking-realization (two tracks of two goals, not two legs of one road); lineage superior to the two vehicles from the start (a distinct vehicle, not an upgrade) — jointly establish the thesis: **the bodhisattva path is not a continuation of arhatship; the arising of bodhicitta is its sole entry; from the arising it forks off independently.**

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

This section clears away three misreadings that crowd close upon the thesis.

**❌ Misreading 1: "Path-independence means the Great Vehicle disparages the two vehicles, and the arahant fruit is worthless."** The paper holds that the bodhisattva path does not take arahantship as its prior stage; it in no way holds that the arahant fruit is false or inferior. "Not taking realization" is a *choice* under great compassion and great vow, not a denial of "the realization not taken" — precisely because that nirvāṇa is real and available (long due for nirvāṇa), declining it is what makes it an act of vow-power. The defilement the bodhisattva severs in common with the śrāvaka is severed for real (the ground-attained bodhisattva severs the discriminated and the innate defilements — the Ten Grounds papers will treat this); its severance, too, is genuine. The difference lies in goal and measure, not in "one true, one false." To read independence as disparagement is to mishear "I walk another road" as "your road is wrong." The canon's establishing of the bodhisattva lineage's supremacy displays the difference of vehicle-nature and the breadth of the goal sought — not that the śrāvaka road cannot end birth-and-death. The śrāvaka road ends birth-and-death faithfully; the four-fruits division (`sramana`) has positively established as much, and this paper does not contend with it.

**⚠️ Correction 2: "Not a continuation of arhatship" is not a verbatim canonical proposition, but a reading the author has ratified, jointly displayed by functionally equivalent passages.** It must be stated honestly: across the sources this paper relies on, no single literal proposition "the bodhisattva path does not continue from arahantship" is to be found. The canon expresses the sense through a set of functionally equivalent sentences — "passes beyond the grounds of the śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha and enters the bodhisattva position," "does not enter the śrāvaka's stage," "does not fall to the śrāvaka ground," "fully knows yet does not take realization," "though practising the real-limit, does not realize it," "at the first arising surpasses all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas." The paper reads this set as "path independent, not continuation" — a synthetic reading with ample doctrinal warrant and ratified by the author, *not* a word-for-word citation. Should a reader meet "does not continue from arhatship" in quotation marks elsewhere, let it be understood as an interpretive title, not the original line of any one sutra or treatise. This boundary is marked plainly, in keeping with the recorded source-gap, so that interpretation is not passed off as source-text.

**⚠️ Correction 3: "Arising as entry" is not "one surge of emotion enters forever without regress"; nor should the "vow-bodhicitta / engaged-bodhicitta" pairing be treated as fixed by this paper's sources.** First: though arising enters in one thought (3.1), the first station it enters — the Ten Faiths — is an *outer-ordinary, regressible* position; the faith-stages can still regress, and the next paper of this division, BODHI-P02, treats this very "instability." So "arising is entry" and "entered yet able to regress" do not conflict: what is entered is the gate; the first stretch within (the Ten Faiths) has not yet reached non-regress. To inflate "arising as entry" into "arising means non-regress, means a secure foothold on the stations" is another extreme, which the paper does not take. Second, a terminological boundary: the pairing "vow-bodhicitta / engaged-bodhicitta," though a common later formulation, has no fixed locus in this paper's three sources (*Yogācārabhūmi*, *Śāstra*, *Avataṃsaka*); the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s "right vow (as nature)" and "basis of training" are functional cognates, not that pairing itself. The paper therefore does not erect a heading on that pairing, nor settle for the canon what it has not stated; any future doctrinal treatment of "vow / engaged bodhicitta" should gather the *Bodhicaryāvatāra* tradition or Avataṃsaka commentaries for its basis.

## VI. Conclusion

**Essential restatement.** The bodhisattva path does not continue from arahantship; its sole entry is the arising of bodhicitta, and from the first arising it forks off independently. On the textual side, the *Yogācārabhūmi* positively defines the entry by "once the mind is aroused, one has entered unsurpassed awakening and is counted among the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle"; the *Śāstra* and the *Avataṃsaka* establish the two parallel tracks by "passing beyond the śrāvaka-pratyekabuddha grounds into the bodhisattva position" and "at the first arising surpassing all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas"; the Lineage chapter establishes the difference of vehicle-nature by the bodhisattva lineage's being "unsurpassed and supreme, realizing also purity from the cognitive-hindrance"; and the Chapter on the Merit of First Arising marks bodhicitta's independent goal (so that the lineage of the Tathāgata not be cut off) by the arising's merit exceeding that of "teaching others to attain the four fruits." On the doctrinal side, three layers support the independence: entry by arising, not severance (one thought enters, no prior stage, 3.1); goal as the accumulation of not-taking-realization, not the subtraction of taking-realization (two tracks of two goals, not two legs of one road, 3.2); lineage superior to the two vehicles from the start (a distinct vehicle, not an upgrade, 3.3).

**Guidance for practice.** A practitioner who would take stock of himself by this measure should exchange the question "must I first complete arahantship, then turn from lesser to greater?" for "this thought of mine — is it to escape my own suffering and seek liberation, or to deliver beings and seek unsurpassed awakening?" The former question traps one in the ladder-anxiety of a "qualification theory," as if the Great Vehicle were a ticket redeemable only after collecting a full set of Lesser-Vehicle stamps; the latter brings one back to the moment of arising — once bodhicitta is aroused, you are already on the road, with no four fruits required as warrant. Nor, equally, should one make light of this: the first station entered is the regressible outer-ordinary faith-position; the work after arising is to carry this mind from "able to regress" toward "non-regress" (position-non-regress begins at the Abode of Arousing the Mind), not to suppose that having once aroused the mind settles everything for good.

**Open questions.** First, this paper deliberately cut the Section IV interdisciplinary convergence: the core of the bodhisattva path's independence is the two concepts of "vehicle / lineage" and "the vow of not-taking-realization," and while the contemporary psychology of "vocation / calling" approximates the sense of "arising is not an examination" (used as a homely figure in 3.1), it has no corresponding structure for "non-abiding nirvāṇa" or "a lineage that also severs the cognitive-hindrance"; a three-layered convergence (likeness / fundamental difference / irreducibility) cannot be sustained without lapsing into forced analogy, so it is better omitted. A genuine question worth later treatment: is there some worldly structure of "reorienting by commitment rather than ranking up by accumulated skill" robust enough to bear a careful convergence with "forking by arising"? Left to a later paper. Second, the master-thesis "independent from the arising" set out here must be tested in the station-by-station unfolding to come: the immediately following BODHI-P02 will treat — given that arising is entry, why is the very first station entered (the Ten Faiths) the least stable, an outer-ordinary position that both regresses and advances? How do "arising as entry" and "the faith-position can regress" coexist on one road? This is the first test of the master-thesis brought down to the concrete.

**The seam (SEAM).** This paper shares one and the same independence thesis with the Attainment-axis paper BODHI-P01, "Bodhisattva Mastery Does Not Continue from Arhatship" (`attainment-signatures/bodhisattva`): the Realization axis (this paper) speaks of the independence of the *path* — how the bodhisattva path runs without continuing from arahantship; the Attainment axis speaks of the independence of the *fruit-virtue* — how bodhisattva mastery stands without continuing from arhat mastery. The two axes share a source at the arising of the mind, and diverge in their account of the walking and the arriving. The doctrine of the seam is the author's to ratify; this paper does not speak for the Attainment axis.
