# Dedication

**The Threefold Dedication · Practice-Non-Regress and the Last of the Three Worthy Ranks; the Accumulated Good Has Its Direction Turned**

*中文主題：迴向——三處迴向與大乘樞紐*

## Abstract

The preceding paper, BODHI-P05, left the bodhisattva amid dense accumulation — the Ten Practices, by the altruistic six perfections, laying up measureless roots of good. But "accumulation" at once presses a question: with so much good accumulated, where does it go? Were it to settle into one's own blessing-fruit, would that not fall back into the "taking realization, taking fruit" that BODHI-P01 refuted? This paper argues: the Ten Dedications (the last of the three worthy ranks) are precisely the position of "turning the accumulated good" — the six-perfection roots of good the Ten Practices accumulated do not, here, stagnate into self-fruit, but are dedicated to three places: dedicated to beings, to bodhi, to the real (suchness). This "dedication" is the Mahāyāna's pivot: it keeps the accumulated good from settling into private fruit, ever turning it toward benefiting others, toward buddhahood, toward suchness, and thereby makes the bodhisattva's "practice" stand firm and non-regressing (practice-non-regress), completing the three worthy ranks, poised to enter the first ground's path of seeing. In the *Avataṃsaka*'s Chapter on the Ten Dedications, the ten names are listed (the Dedication of Rescuing All Beings while Departing from the Mark of Beings … the Dedication of Entering the Measureless Dharma-Realm, taught in common by the buddhas of the three times); within the per-dedication expositions the three directions are shown dispersed — dedicating the roots of good of giving "to beings," dedicating the roots of good planted "to the buddhas" (bodhi), dedicating all roots of good "in accord with the mark of suchness" (the real); and it repeatedly says the bodhisattva "accomplishes indestructible deeds, heads for omniscience, and in the end does not regress," "arouses the non-regressing mind, gathering the mind-jewel." From this the paper argues three things: dedication as "turning" (cutting off the stagnation into self-fruit); the unity of the threefold dedication (downward to beings, upward to bodhi, deep into suchness); and dedication and practice-non-regress (with no self-fruit to regress into, the practice does not regress). An honest mark: "threefold dedication" is the doctrinal synthesis of the *Dasheng yizhang*, the *Huayan Tanxuan ji*, and the like, not a verbatim phrase of the *Avataṃsaka*; "practice-non-regress · the last of the three worthy ranks" is the stational classification of the *Keyūra*, the *Renwang*, the *Cheng weishi lun*, and the like (and the treatises differ in how they parse the stations), not a sentence of this chapter; this paper argues from these two classificatory frameworks (also those the axis's PLAN adopts) while marking their nature, the treatise-texts lying beyond this round's source (T0279), to be ratified by the author (see Section V). The conclusion: dedication is not loss but turning; it is the crucial turn that keeps accumulated good from falling into self-fruit and makes the bodhisattva's practice non-regressing, the three worthy ranks here brought to their close.

**Keywords**: Ten Dedications, dedication, threefold dedication, dedicating to beings, dedicating to bodhi, dedicating to the real, dedication in accord with suchness, practice-non-regress, the last of the three worthy ranks, the Mahāyāna pivot, Avataṃsaka Ten Dedications chapter, doctrinal synthesis

## I. The Question

The last paper left the bodhisattva amid dense accumulation. The practice-position, by the altruistic six perfections, acted and accumulated for beings, each act one performed for them and laid up for them — giving without regret or grudging, pure precepts unattached to the five sense-fields, patience that bears insult, vigour that never troubles a being. In this position the bodhisattva laid up measureless roots of good (BODHI-P05).

But "accumulation" itself at once presses a sharp question: with so much good accumulated, where does it go? If the roots of good merely thicken and settle — becoming a private ledger of "how much merit I have cultivated," a blessing-reward held in reserve to be drawn down — is that not precisely a falling-back into the "taking realization, taking fruit" that BODHI-P01 refuted? The bodhisattva path's whole thesis is "not taking realization, not abiding in nirvāṇa, accumulating the delivering of beings for the beings' sake"; were the good the practice-position lays up to become one's own blessing-fruit, then the more accumulated, the further from the path's root intent. Accumulation needs an outlet — a mechanism that keeps it from settling into self-fruit.

The outlet the canon gives is "dedication." To dedicate (迴向, *pariṇāmanā*): 迴 is to turn, 向 is the direction faced; dedication is "to turn and face toward" — to take the accumulated good, from its natural place of settling (one's own blessing-fruit), and actively turn it, redirecting it elsewhere. Toward what? In the per-dedication expositions, the Chapter on the Ten Dedications shows three directions dispersed: dedication to beings (that the good go to all beings), dedication to bodhi (that the good turn up toward buddhahood), dedication to the real (that the good accord with suchness). The doctrinal masters synthesize these three directions as the "threefold dedication." And this "turning" is the Mahāyāna's pivot: it is the structural fulfilment of "not hoping for fruit" (the Practice of Joy, P05) — the accumulated good, ever dedicated to the three places and never kept for oneself, leaves the bodhisattva's "practice" no self-fruit to regress into, and so it stands firm and non-regressing.

The thesis of this paper: **the Ten Dedications (the last of the three worthy ranks) are the position of "turning the accumulated good" — the six-perfection roots of good the Ten Practices accumulated do not, here, stagnate into self-fruit, but are dedicated to three places (beings · bodhi · the real); this dedication is the Mahāyāna pivot, keeping the good from settling into private fruit and ever turning it toward benefiting others, toward buddhahood, toward suchness, thereby making the bodhisattva's practice stand firm and non-regressing (practice-non-regress), completing the three worthy ranks, poised to enter the first ground's path of seeing.** This takes the baton from BODHI-P05: P05 said the practice-position densely accumulates the good of benefiting others through the six perfections; this paper says that accumulated good, at the dedication-position, has its direction turned and does not fall into self-fruit.

By placement, the three worthy ranks here come to their close: abodes (first of the worthy, making good the son-of-the-Buddha standing, position-non-regress), practices (middle of the worthy, going out to benefit others, the six perfections enacted), dedications (last of the worthy, turning the accumulated, practice-non-regress). The Ten Dedications are the last stretch of the three worthy ranks; their fullness is the completion of the worthy ranks and the approach of the noble ranks (the first ground's path of seeing).

Two boundaries of sources must be stated honestly up front, detailed in Section V. First, "threefold dedication" (dedicating to beings · bodhi · the real) as a tripartite scheme is the synthetic classification of doctrinal masters such as the *Dasheng yizhang* and the *Huayan Tanxuan ji* upon the "direction" of the Ten Dedications, not a phrase set word-for-word in the chapter; the *Avataṃsaka* shows the reality of the three directions dispersed and individually in the per-dedication expositions, and this paper's §2.2 takes one passage positively showing each direction. Second, the term "practice-non-regress," and the stational classification "the Ten Dedications as the last of the three worthy ranks, fulfilling practice-non-regress," are likewise not sentences of this chapter, but issue from the stational systems of the *Keyūra*, the *Renwang*, the *Cheng weishi lun*, and the like (and the treatises differ in how they parse the stations); this chapter has "in the end does not regress," "the non-regressing mind," and this paper argues from them, on the classificatory architecture the axis adopts (PLAN: practice-non-regress tied to the Ten Dedications / first ground), while marking the classificatory nature. The treatise-texts lie beyond this round's designated source (T0279), unsupplied, to be ratified by the author.

The argument proceeds in three moves. Section II lays out the canonical basis: the full list of the ten dedications; the canonical showing of the three directions; the non-regress passages of the dedication-position. Section III is the core argument, in three layers: dedication as "turning" (3.1); the unity of the threefold dedication (3.2); dedication and practice-non-regress (3.3). Section IV deliberately omits interdisciplinary convergence, for the reason in Section VI. Section V offers middle-way corrections, marking honestly the synthetic classification of threefold dedication, the stational classification of practice-non-regress, the variant name, and the misreading "dedication is loss." Section VI concludes, with the cross-reference index, and looks ahead to P07 (the first ground's path of seeing) and the Attainment-axis seam.

## II. The Canonical Basis

### 2.1 The full list of the ten dedications

In the *Avataṃsaka*'s Chapter on the Ten Dedications, the bodhisattva Vajra-Banner (Vajradhvaja), by the Buddha's spiritual power, lists the names and number of the bodhisattva's ten dedications:

> 佛子！菩薩摩訶薩迴向有十種，三世諸佛咸共演說。何等為十？一者救護一切眾生離眾生相迴向，二者不壞迴向，三者等一切諸佛迴向，四者至一切處迴向，五者無盡功德藏迴向，六者入一切平等善根迴向，七者等隨順一切眾生迴向，八者真如相迴向，九者無縛無著解脫迴向，十者入法界無量迴向。[^1]
>
> *"O son of the Buddha, the great bodhisattva's dedications are of ten kinds, taught in common by the buddhas of the three times. What are the ten? First, the Dedication of Rescuing All Beings while Departing from the Mark of Beings; second, the Indestructible Dedication; third, the Dedication Equal to All Buddhas; fourth, the Dedication Reaching All Places; fifth, the Dedication of the Inexhaustible Treasury of Merit; sixth, the Dedication of Entering All Equal Roots of Good; seventh, the Dedication of Equally According with All Beings; eighth, the Dedication of the Mark of Suchness; ninth, the Dedication of Bondless, Attachless Liberation; tenth, the Dedication of Entering the Measureless Dharma-Realm."*

The ten in order: Rescuing All Beings while Departing from the Mark of Beings, the Indestructible, Equal to All Buddhas, Reaching All Places, the Inexhaustible Treasury of Merit, Entering All Equal Roots of Good, Equally According with All Beings, the Mark of Suchness, Bondless-Attachless Liberation, Entering the Measureless Dharma-Realm. Of the same rank as the Ten Abodes and Ten Practices, these ten dedications too are "taught in common by the buddhas of the three times" — a fixed stational set the buddhas teach in common. (On variant names: the sixth dedication reads, in the chapter's general listing, "the Dedication of Entering All Equal Roots of Good," while its per-dedication heading reads "the Dedication of According-with and Firming All Roots of Good" — a wording-difference between the general listing and the per-dedication exposition within the 80-fascicle text itself, not a difference of recension; this paper sets them side by side, leaving the choice of name to the author, detailed in Section V.)

Note first the directions these ten names disclose. The first dedication is "Rescuing All Beings while Departing from the Mark of Beings" — rescuing beings (toward beings), yet "departing from the mark of beings" (not grasping the marks of rescuer and rescued); the third is "the Dedication Equal to All Buddhas" — toward the buddhas, equal to buddhahood; the eighth is "the Dedication of the Mark of Suchness" — according with suchness, toward the real. Beings, the buddhas (bodhi), suchness (the real) — the three directions already lie dispersed among these ten names. The "threefold dedication" the doctrinal masters synthesize is just the gathering of these dispersed directions into three rubrics.

### 2.2 The threefold dedication: beings · bodhi · the real, each positively shown in canon

Though the term "threefold dedication" belongs to doctrinal synthesis (detailed in Section V), the reality of its three directions the *Avataṃsaka* positively shows in the per-dedication expositions. One passage for each.

First, **dedicating to beings.** The sixth-dedication passage shows the roots of good cultivated dedicated to beings:

> 菩薩摩訶薩以此布施所有善根迴向眾生：「願一切眾生清淨調伏；願一切眾生滅除煩惱，嚴淨一切諸佛剎土；……願一切眾生於念念中常作三世一切佛事，教化眾生向一切智。」[^2]
>
> *"The great bodhisattva dedicates all the roots of good of this giving to beings: 'May all beings be purified and tamed; may all beings extinguish their afflictions and adorn-purify all the buddha-lands; … may all beings, in thought after thought, ever perform the buddha-work of all the three times, teaching beings toward omniscience.'"*

The bodhisattva takes the roots of good gained from giving, keeps them not for himself, but "dedicates them to beings" — that beings be purified and tamed, extinguish afflictions, and even "head toward omniscience." The direction of the good points wholly to beings.

Second, **dedicating to bodhi.** The third-dedication passage (Equal to All Buddhas) shows the roots of good turned up toward the buddhas (buddhahood · bodhi):

> 佛子！菩薩摩訶薩獲得如是安樂之時，復更發心迴向諸佛，作如是念：「願以我今所種善根，令諸佛樂轉更增勝……」[^3]
>
> *"O son of the Buddha, when the great bodhisattva gains such joy, he further arouses the mind to dedicate to the buddhas, thinking thus: 'May the roots of good I now plant cause the buddhas' joy to turn ever more surpassing …'"*

When the bodhisattva gains joy, he does not take it for his own enjoyment but "arouses the mind to dedicate to the buddhas," turning the roots of good he plants up as an offering toward buddhahood — the direction of the good points up toward bodhi.

Third, **dedicating to the real (suchness).** The eighth-dedication passage (the Mark of Suchness) shows all roots of good accorded with suchness, dedicated to the ultimate:

> 其中所有一切善根，悉是趣向十力乘心之所建立，皆悉迴向一切種智，唯一無二。[^4]
>
> *"All the roots of good therein are established by the mind that heads for the ten-powers vehicle; all are dedicated to omniscience, one alone, without a second."*

All roots of good are "dedicated to omniscience, one alone without a second," "dedicated in accord with the mark of suchness" — the direction of the good is deeply accorded with suchness-the-real, the duality of grasper and grasped dissolved.

Beings, bodhi, the real — downward, upward, deep, each direction positively shown in canon. From these three passages this paper sets up the account of "threefold dedication" (that it is a doctrinal synthesis, not a verbatim sentence of the *Avataṃsaka*, is detailed in Section V).

### 2.3 The non-regress of the dedication-position: accomplishing indestructible deeds, in the end not regressing

The "non-regress" of the dedication-position the chapter states repeatedly. First, at the opening of the eighth, the Dedication of the Mark of Suchness, it summarizes this position's bodhisattva:

> 此菩薩摩訶薩正念明了，其心堅住，遠離迷惑；專意修行，深心不動；成不壞業，趣一切智，終不退轉；志求大乘，勇猛無畏。[^5]
>
> *"This great bodhisattva's right mindfulness is clear, his mind firmly settled, far from delusion; single-minded in cultivation, deep-minded and unmoved; he accomplishes indestructible deeds, heads for omniscience, and in the end does not regress; resolved on the Great Vehicle, brave and fearless."*

"Accomplishes indestructible deeds, heads for omniscience, and in the end does not regress" — his mind firmly settled, deep-minded and unmoved, the deeds he accomplishes indestructible, heading for omniscience without in the end regressing. Second, already at the first dedication (Rescuing All Beings while Departing from the Mark of Beings), the bodhisattva is shown arousing "the non-regressing mind":

> ……心、不退轉心，無量劫中修集希有難得心寶……[^6]
>
> *"… the mind, the non-regressing mind, gathering through measureless eons the rare and hard-to-attain mind-jewel …"*

The bodhisattva arouses "the non-regressing mind," gathering through measureless eons the hard-to-attain mind-jewel. The dedication-position, through and through, has "non-regress" for its mark.

(It must be noted in passing: what the chapter states is the textual mark of "in the end does not regress," "the non-regressing mind"; whereas naming this position "practice-non-regress" and classifying it as "the last of the three worthy ranks, fulfilling practice-non-regress" is the stational classification of the *Keyūra*, the *Renwang*, the *Cheng weishi lun*, and the like, not a sentence of this chapter. This boundary is detailed in Section V.)

## III. The Core Argument

Section II presented three things: the full list of the ten dedications (the ten names already disclosing the three directions of beings, buddhas, suchness); the three directions each positively shown in canon (dedicating to beings · bodhi · the real); the non-regress passages of the dedication-position (accomplishing indestructible deeds · in the end not regressing · the non-regressing mind). This section, in three layers: dedication as "turning" (3.1); the unity of the threefold dedication (3.2); dedication and practice-non-regress (3.3).

### 3.1 Dedication as "turning": cutting off the road by which roots of good settle into self-fruit

Roots of good have a kind of natural "gravity" — they settle. When a person does good, cultivates merit, the most natural destination is to enter it under his own name: "how much blessing I have laid up," "how much reward I shall one day receive." If roots of good settle along this gravity, they become a privately held, in-reserve blessing-fruit. For one seeking the blessings of gods and humans this is unobjectionable; but for the bodhisattva, this is just the danger — BODHI-P01 established that the bodhisattva path's aim is not "taking realization, taking fruit" but the accumulation of delivering beings in non-abiding nirvāṇa; were the good the practice-position (P05) densely lays up to settle along gravity into self-fruit, the thicker the accumulation, the further from the root intent.

"Dedication" is the turn against this gravity. To dedicate is to turn, the direction faced: dedication takes the roots of good, from their natural destination of "settling into self-fruit," and actively turns and redirects them elsewhere (beings · bodhi · suchness). It is not a new dharma cultivated apart from the roots of good, but a "change of direction" given to good already cultivated — the same good, bound to fall into the private ledger, once dedicated, is turned outward. Herein lies dedication as the Mahāyāna pivot: it is the structural redemption of "not hoping for fruit" (the Practice of Joy's "not hoping for fruit, not seeking fame, not coveting gain," P05) — where the Practice of Joy is the mind in the very act of giving, the dedication-position erects "of all good accumulated, none kept as self-fruit" as a dedicated, pervasive position; the former is the mind at the moment of giving, the latter the total turning of the good accumulated.

A contrast: the "earmark" in personal finance. When income enters the account, the most natural is to leave it in one's own current account, available at any time for one's own spending (settling into self-fruit). But you can also, the moment it arrives, earmark it — transfer the whole into some dedicated fund, say a "children's education fund" or a "charitable trust." Once earmarked, that money, though it passed through your hands, is no longer a "balance available at any time for your own withdrawal"; its direction has changed. Dedication is the "earmarking" of roots of good: once the good cultivated is dedicated to beings, to bodhi, to suchness, it is no longer "my blessing in reserve to be drawn"; its direction has been turned outward.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: dedication as turning is the active redirection of roots of good, from their natural destination of "settling into self-fruit," to the three places; it is the structural redemption, upon the good accumulated, of BODHI-P01's "not taking realization" and BODHI-P05's "not hoping for fruit" — not a new dharma apart from good already cultivated, but the turning of its direction, so that accumulation does not fall into self-fruit.

### 3.2 The unity of the threefold dedication: downward to beings, upward to bodhi, deep into suchness

The three directions of dedication — beings, bodhi, the real — are not three separate things one might pick among, but three dimensions of one and the same dedication. The downward: dedicating to beings (that the good go to all beings, §2.2 first) — the dimension of compassion. The upward: dedicating to bodhi (turning the good up toward buddhahood, §2.2 second) — the dimension of vow and wisdom. The deep: dedicating to the real (according the good with suchness, to omniscience, one without a second, §2.2 third) — the dimension of emptiness-wisdom. One set of roots of good, at once moistening beings downward, heading for buddhahood upward, according with suchness deep; the three directions gather the whole of where the bodhisattva's good goes, lacking only the one direction of "inward, back to oneself."

And the three directions inter-pervade, not sealed off from one another. Dedicating to beings, yet wishing beings to "head toward omniscience" (§2.2 first, the sutra's words), the dedication to beings already contains the direction to bodhi; dedicating to bodhi, yet the buddhas' joy turning more surpassing for the delivering of beings, the dedication to bodhi too is not apart from beings; dedicating to the real (according with suchness, one without a second) dissolves the duality of dedicator and dedicated, of director and directed, so that the prior two directions do not fall into the grasping of grasper-and-grasped — the first dedication's "rescuing all beings" that is at once "departing from the mark of beings" (§2.1, the name) is just the union of dedicating to beings (compassion) and dedicating to the real (departing from marks). The three directions are like three streams of one source: downward, upward, deep, yet their water of one taste.

A contrast: a lamp lit in a room — its light at once illumines the person underfoot (downward · beings), reveals the direction on high (upward · bodhi), and, being without own-nature, pervades unobstructed (deep · suchness). This is not three separate lamps, nor a demand that you choose which side to light; it is the three reaches of one and the same lamp's light. So with dedication's three places: not three different dedications, of which one is chosen to practise, but one and the same turned-over good, necessarily reaching beings downward, bodhi upward, suchness deep.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the threefold dedication (beings · bodhi · the real) is three dimensions of one dedication — downward compassion, upward vow-and-wisdom, deep emptiness-wisdom — and the three inter-pervade (dedicating to beings wishes their heading for omniscience; dedicating to the real frees the prior two of the grasper-grasped mark). The three gather the whole of where the bodhisattva's good goes, lacking only the direction "back to oneself." (Note: the tripartite rubric "threefold dedication" is a doctrinal synthesis, not a verbatim sentence of the *Avataṃsaka*; detailed in Section V.)

### 3.3 Dedication and practice-non-regress: with no self-fruit to regress into, the practice does not regress

A final question: how does "dedication" make the "practice" non-regressing? This is the crux of why the dedication-position is classified as "practice-non-regress" (its classificatory nature detailed in Section V). The answer lies in the "turning" of 3.1: the bodhisattva's good, ever dedicated to the three places and kept not at all as self-fruit, leaves his "practice" no foothold to regress into — self-fruit.

Consider: one who does good yet still enters it on his own blessing-ledger has, in his "practice," a road of retreat — once the doing grows hard, the benefiting of others goes ill, he can at any time retreat into the self-fruit of "I have laid up no small blessing; enough," and the practice regresses. But a bodhisattva who dedicates the whole of his accumulated good to beings, bodhi, suchness, keeping not the least as self-fruit, has no such private fruit of "enough, time to rest" to retreat into — the good being not on his own ledger, there is no "balance on the ledger" for him to fall back upon. With no self-fruit to regress into, the practice does not regress. Dedication is not another matter apart from practice; dedication is precisely the mechanism that makes practice non-regressing: cutting off every road of retreat (self-fruit), the practice thereby stands firm. The sutra says this position's bodhisattva "accomplishes indestructible deeds, heads for omniscience, and in the end does not regress" (§2.3) — the deeds accomplished are "indestructible," "in the end do not regress," precisely because they are wholly dedicated, with no self-fruit to retreat into.

This "practice-non-regress" is to be seen as layered apart from the prior position's "position-non-regress." BODHI-P03 established "position-non-regress" — its texture the view's not turning before dilemmas, what does not regress being the "bodhisattva position" (not falling to the two vehicles or the ordinary), locking positional standing. Here "practice-non-regress" — what does not regress being the "altruistic six-perfection practice" (not regressing in what is practised), locking the practice. Position-non-regress (Ten Abodes) held "who one is" (the son-of-the-Buddha standing not regressing); practice-non-regress (Ten Dedications) holds "what one does" (the altruistic practice not regressing). The threefold non-regress firms layer by layer, from position to practice; the Ten Dedications full, the three worthy ranks come to their close, the bodhisattva poised at the first ground's path of seeing (BODHI-P07), entering the noble from the worthy.

A contrast: burning the boats and smashing the cauldrons. After an army crosses the river, it deliberately sinks the boats and breaks the cooking-pots — cutting off every road of retreat. Why can it then fight without regress? Precisely because there is no road of retreat to take. Keep the boats and pots (self-fruit), and the moment the battle grows hard there is the thought and the road of "withdraw"; the boats and pots destroyed (the good wholly dedicated, no self-fruit), there is, besides going forward, no ground to retreat to, and so the action stands firm and non-regressing. The bodhisattva's dedication is just this "burning the boats" upon the roots of good: the accumulated good wholly dedicated to the three places, kept not as self-fruit, leaves the altruistic practice no private fruit to retreat into, and so the practice does not regress.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: dedication makes practice non-regressing, its mechanism being "no self-fruit to regress into" — the good wholly dedicated to the three places, kept not for oneself, leaves the practice no self-fruit foothold to retreat into, and so it stands firm and non-regressing (the sutra's "accomplishing indestructible deeds · in the end not regressing," §2.3). This "practice-non-regress" (locking the altruistic practice) is layered apart from BODHI-P03's "position-non-regress" (locking the bodhisattva's positional standing); the threefold non-regress firms layer by layer, position to practice, and the Ten Dedications full, the three worthy ranks close, at the threshold of the first ground's path of seeing. (Note: the term "practice-non-regress · the last of the three worthy ranks" and the stational classification are erected by the treatises and doctrinal masters, not a verbatim sentence of the *Avataṃsaka*; detailed in Section V.)

Taken together, the three layers — dedication is the one turn of roots of good from settling into self-fruit to the three places (3.1); the threefold dedication (beings · bodhi · the real) is the downward, upward, deep dimensions of one dedication, lacking only the direction back to self (3.2); and the good wholly dedicated, with no self-fruit to regress into, the bodhisattva's practice stands firm and non-regressing (3.3) — jointly establish the thesis: **the Ten Dedications are the position of turning the accumulated good, their dedication to the three places being the Mahāyāna pivot — keeping the good from falling into self-fruit and ever turning it toward beings · bodhi · suchness, thereby making the practice non-regressing and the three worthy ranks complete.**

## IV. Interdisciplinary Convergence

(This paper deliberately omits the interdisciplinary-convergence section; the rationale and explicit skip-declaration appear in Section VI. ## V. Middle-Way Corrections

This section clears away four misreadings; the first two bear on the documentary nature of this paper's two major themes, and require special honesty.

**⚠️ Correction 1: "threefold dedication" is a doctrinal synthesis, not a verbatim phrase of the *Avataṃsaka*'s Chapter on the Ten Dedications.** This is the boundary this paper must mark first. The tripartite rubric "dedicating to beings · to bodhi · to the real (suchness)" is the synthetic classification of doctrinal masters such as the *Dasheng yizhang* and the *Huayan Tanxuan ji* upon the "direction" of the Ten Dedications; there is no sentence of the chapter that says word-for-word "dedication has three places." The *Avataṃsaka* shows the reality of the three directions dispersed and individually within the per-dedication expositions — the three passages cited in §2.2 (dedicating to beings at the sixth-dedication passage, to bodhi at the third, to the real at the eighth Mark-of-Suchness passage) each positively show "one direction," while the "gathering of these three directions into 'three places'" as a rubric is a later doctrinal classification. This paper argues from that synthesis (also the architecture of the axis's PLAN's "threefold dedication"), for it does indeed gather the reality of the Ten Dedications' direction; but it marks that it is a "synthetic classification," not verbatim canon. Let the reader know: the reality of the three directions has canonical evidence (§2.2); the rubric "three places" belongs to doctrinal classification.

**⚠️ Correction 2: the term "practice-non-regress," and the stational classification "the Ten Dedications as the last of the three worthy ranks, fulfilling practice-non-regress," are not verbatim sentences of this chapter.** This chapter does have the passages "in the end does not regress," "accomplishes indestructible deeds," "the non-regressing mind" (§2.3); but to name the dedication-position "practice-non-regress," and to classify it as "the last of the three worthy ranks (abodes · practices · dedications), fulfilling practice-non-regress here," issues from the stational systems of the *Keyūra*, the *Renwang*, the *Cheng weishi lun*, and the like, not from the chapter's own statement. And note: the treatises differ among themselves on the placement of the threefold non-regress (position · practice · mindfulness) and on the parsing of the three worthy and ten noble ranks (e.g., "practice-non-regress" tied by some to the Ten Dedications, by others to the first ground; the axis's PLAN takes "practice-non-regress tied to the Ten Dedications / first ground"). This paper argues from the chapter's "non-regress" passages, on the classificatory architecture the axis adopts, while marking "practice-non-regress · the last of the three worthy ranks" as the treatises' and doctrinal masters' stational classification, not the *Avataṃsaka*'s express word. Such treatise-texts lie beyond this round's designated source (T0279), their anchors not yet assembled, to be supplied in a later round of treatise source-gathering (*Keyūra* · *Renwang* · *Cheng weishi lun*, and the like); the final ratification of the station-sequence is the author's, on the basis of school and treatise.

**⚠️ Correction 3: the sixth dedication's name differs between general listing and per-dedication exposition; set side by side, no side taken.** The sixth dedication reads, in the chapter's general listing (0124c), "the Dedication of Entering All Equal Roots of Good," while its per-dedication heading (0155c) reads "the Dedication of According-with and Firming All Roots of Good" — a wording-difference between general listing and per-dedication exposition within the 80-fascicle text itself, not a difference of translation-recension. This paper, at the listing of the names, follows the general listing as "the Dedication of Entering All Equal Roots of Good," and marks here that the per-dedication exposition reads "the Dedication of According-with and Firming All Roots of Good," setting the two side by side without selecting a uniform name for the author. Should a reader meet this dedication under another name elsewhere, let it be known to be the same sutra's general/per-dedication variant, or that source's selection.

**❌ Misreading 4: dedication = "sending merit out so it is gone," a loss.** This is the commonest misunderstanding, and must be cleared. Dedication is not diminution but turning. To dedicate roots of good to the three places is not to destroy roots of good, to throw merit away, but to change their direction and their place of ripening — dedicated to beings, the good is not lost but wishes beings benefit; dedicated to bodhi, the good is not lost but becomes the provision heading for buddhahood; dedicated to the real, the good is not lost but accords with suchness, with non-attainment. The second of the ten dedications is named, precisely, "the Indestructible Dedication" (§2.1, the name) — the two words "indestructible" show just this: the good of dedication is indestructible, not perishing once dedicated out. In truth dedication not only does not diminish roots of good but makes them great: a good that, settling into self-fruit, would halt at the small reward of gods and humans, once dedicated to beings · bodhi · suchness, turns into the great good heading for omniscience and accomplishing buddhahood. So dedication is not "doing merit and then throwing it away," but the turn that "makes small good great, makes the defiled into undefiled provision." Let the reader not, by the worldly measure "given out, so there is less," mis-measure dedication.

## VI. Conclusion

**Essential restatement.** The Ten Dedications (the last of the three worthy ranks) are the position of "turning the accumulated good": the six-perfection roots of good the Ten Practices accumulated do not, here, stagnate into self-fruit, but are dedicated to three places — beings, bodhi, the real; this dedication is the Mahāyāna pivot, keeping the good from settling into private fruit and ever turning it toward benefiting others · buddhahood · suchness, thereby making the bodhisattva's practice stand firm and non-regressing (practice-non-regress), completing the three worthy ranks. On the textual side, the *Avataṃsaka*'s Chapter on the Ten Dedications lists the ten names (taught in common by the buddhas of the three times), shows the reality of the three directions dispersed in the per-dedication expositions (dedicating to beings · to the buddhas · in accord with suchness), and repeatedly says "accomplishes indestructible deeds · heads for omniscience · in the end does not regress," "arouses the non-regressing mind · gathers the mind-jewel." On the doctrinal side, three layers support it: dedication is the one turn of roots of good from settling into self-fruit to the three places (3.1); the threefold dedication is the downward · upward · deep dimensions of one dedication, lacking only the direction back to self (3.2); the good wholly dedicated, with no self-fruit to regress into, the practice stands firm and non-regressing (3.3). Honest marks: "threefold dedication" is a doctrinal synthesis; "practice-non-regress · the last of the three worthy ranks" is the treatises' stational classification; the sixth dedication's names are set side by side; dedication is not loss but turning (Section V).

**Guidance for practice.** Dedication is a turn performable in daily use, and a turn most crucial to the bodhisattva path. Let the practitioner know two things. First, do not let the good settle into a private ledger. Of every measure of good you do — an act of giving, a keeping of precepts, a sitting in stillness — at the moment of completing it, learn to dedicate then and there: may this good go to all beings, head for unsurpassed bodhi, accord with suchness-the-real. This dedication is not a courteous coda after the deed but the crucial turn that cuts off "the grasping of self-fruit" and makes the practice non-regressing; undedicated, the good settles back along gravity into one's own blessing-ledger, and the practice gains a private fruit to retreat into. Second, do not take dedication for loss. The good is not lost by dedication but made great — what is turned out is the small reward "back to me," what is gained is the great good heading for omniscience (as the name "Indestructible Dedication" shows). One who can, in daily use, dedicate in thought after thought, keeping no self-fruit, will find his altruistic practice naturally standing firm — this is "practice-non-regress" begun on level ground.

**Open questions.** First, this paper deliberately cut the Section IV interdisciplinary convergence, for the reason above. Second, the treatise-texts of this paper's two major themes — the synthesis of "threefold dedication" (the *Dasheng yizhang*, the *Huayan Tanxuan ji*, and the like) and the stational classification of "practice-non-regress · the last of the three worthy ranks" (the *Keyūra*, the *Renwang*, the *Cheng weishi lun*, and the like, with the treatises differing on the parsing of stations) — lie beyond this round's designated source (T0279), their anchors unsupplied; once a later round of treatise source-gathering is assembled, this paper's synthetic and stational arguments should be reinforced, the final parsing of the station-sequence to be ratified by the author on the basis of school and treatise. Third, the three worthy ranks here come to their close at the Ten Dedications, practice-non-regress established, the bodhisattva poised at the threshold of the first of the noble ranks. BODHI-P07 will treat the first ground (the Ground of Joy) and the path of seeing: the pass from worthy into noble — the Ten Dedications full, one enters the first ground, severs the discriminated afflictions and sees the path, turning from the three worthy ranks' "suppression" to the ten noble grounds' "severance," a great turning-point of the whole bodhisattva path.

**The seam (SEAM).** This paper meets the Attainment-axis `attainment-signatures/bodhisattva` at the two faces of "dedication": the Realization axis (this paper) tells the "practice" of dedication — how the accumulated good is turned to the three places, making the practice non-regressing; the Attainment axis reads the fruit-virtue dedication accomplishes — the bodhisattva mastery (a mastery of not abiding in self-fruit) the wholly-dedicated good and practice-non-regress sign. It must be strictly distinguished from the Attainment-axis `tathagata`'s fruit-ground: the Ten Dedications are causal-stage, the last of the three worthy ranks, their dedication and non-regress alike matters of the causal-stage bodhisattva-portion, not the ultimate mastery of the Tathāgata's fruit-ground. The doctrine of the seam is the author's to ratify; this paper does not speak for the Attainment axis.
