# The Practice-Position

**The Ten Practices · the Six Perfections Become Conduct, the Completion of Self-Benefit Turning into the Conduct of Benefiting Others**

*中文主題：行位——利他與六度之踐行*

## Abstract

The preceding paper, BODHI-P04, carried the bodhisattva to the Abode of Consecration — matured within the Buddha-family, invested and "fit to do the Dharma-King's work," the door now opening outward. The abode-position was "maturing within the house" (the completion of self-benefit); what, then, does "going out to benefit others" concretely look like? This paper argues: the Ten Practices (the middle of the three worthy ranks) are precisely this position of "going out to benefit others," their substance being the concrete enactment of the six perfections, their direction expressly the benefiting of all beings. In the *Avataṃsaka*'s Chapter on the Ten Practices, the ten run in order — Joy, Benefiting, Non-Opposition, Indomitability, Freedom-from-Confusion, Excellent Manifestation, Non-Attachment, Hard-to-Attain, Excellent Dharma, Truth — taught by the buddhas of the three times; and the first six practices each have one perfection for their substance: the Practice of Joy is giving (a great donor, without regret or grudging, not hoping for fruit, "only to benefit all beings"), the Practice of Benefiting is discipline, the Practice of Non-Opposition is patience, the Practice of Indomitability is vigour, the Practice of Freedom-from-Confusion is meditation (right mindfulness), the Practice of Excellent Manifestation is assigned to wisdom (abiding in non-attainment — an exegetical assignment). The treatises set the backbone: the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* lists the six perfections (*dāna · śīla · kṣānti · vīrya · dhyāna · prajñā*) and, by the figure of the blind and the guide, has the five without prajñā like the blind, and only with prajñā as guide "having eyes," becoming "perfections (gone to the far shore)"; the *Yogācārabhūmi* sorts the six into merit-portion and wisdom-portion; the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* assigns the six one by one to benefiting beings (giving benefits, discipline does not harm, patience bears insult, vigour grows good and destroys evil) so that beings are tamed. From this the paper argues three things: the difference between abode and practice (the completion of self-benefit turning into the conduct of benefiting others); the "enactment" of the six perfections (from abstract virtue-list to recognizable concrete conduct); and that the six are not a flat list but have prajñā as guide. An honest mark: the assignment of the Practice of Excellent Manifestation to wisdom is an exegetical reading, and the assignment of the latter four practices (Non-Attachment, Hard-to-Attain, Excellent Dharma, Truth) to the latter four perfections (means, vow, power, knowledge) is the classification of Chengguan's commentary, not a per-practice assignment in the sutra itself; this paper does not force the latter (see Section V). The conclusion: benefiting others is not deferred until self-cultivation is complete; the work of the practice-position is precisely to "enact the six perfections within the conduct of benefiting others," with the prajñā of non-attainment for its eye.

**Keywords**: Ten Practices, practice-position, six perfections, enactment, the turn to benefiting others, Practice of Joy, prajñā as guide, merit-and-wisdom portions, cool dharma-pool, three worthy ranks, Avataṃsaka Ten Practices chapter, exegetical assignment

## I. The Question

The last paper carried the bodhisattva to the doorway of the Buddha-family — or rather, to the inmost end within the door. The Ten Abodes close with the Abode of Consecration: within the family of the buddhas of the three times the bodhisattva enters the house at arousing the mind, matures by degrees, and at consecration is invested, "accomplishing ten kinds of knowledge," fit to do the Dharma-King's work (BODHI-P04). But that paper's close already noted: investiture is not the finale but the beginning; the crowned sovereign is precisely now to go out and take up his work. The work of the abode-position is "maturing within the house," the ripening of the self-benefit side; and the door having opened outward, the next step is naturally: **go out, and benefit others.**

The difficulty is: "going out to benefit others" rings grand, but brought down to the concrete, what does it look like? Is it the empty striking of a great-compassion pose, or is there recognizable, performable concrete conduct? The canon's answer is utterly concrete — it is the "Ten Practices." The Chapter on the Ten Practices lists the bodhisattva's ten kinds of practice, and the first six of these ten correspond one to one with the six perfections: the Practice of Joy *is* giving in action, the Practice of Benefiting is discipline in action, the Practice of Non-Opposition is patience in action, the Practice of Indomitability is vigour in action, the Practice of Freedom-from-Confusion is meditation in action, and the Practice of Excellent Manifestation points to wisdom. That is: the "six perfections," recited by many as an abstract list of virtues, are in the practice-position a string of grounded, presently-performed actions. And the Chapter's opening Practice of Joy marks the direction of these practices unmistakably: the bodhisattva gives "not hoping for fruit, not seeking fame, not coveting gain; only to rescue all beings, to embrace all beings, to benefit all beings" — the arrow of giving turned, from "self," wholly toward "other."

The thesis of this paper: **the Ten Practices (the middle of the three worthy ranks) are the bodhisattva's position of "going out to benefit others"; their substance is the concrete enactment of the six perfections (the first six practices each being one perfection in action), their direction expressly the benefiting of beings; and the six, with prajñā as guide, become the "going to the far shore."** This takes the baton from BODHI-P04: P04 said the abode-position, at consecration, is "fit to do the Dharma-King's work"; this paper says that "Dharma-King's work" is concretely the altruistic enactment of the six perfections.

By placement, recall the sequence of the three worthy ranks: abodes (first of the worthy), practices (middle of the worthy), dedications (last of the worthy). The Ten Practices stand in the middle — the abode-position made good the "position" (the son-of-the-Buddha standing, position-non-regress), and the practice-position unfolds that standing into "conduct" (the altruistic conduct of the six perfections), while the dedication-position (next paper) turns the direction of the good that conduct accrues. This paper treats the middle stretch: how, the standing once fixed, the bodhisattva *acts*.

Two boundaries of sources must be stated honestly up front, detailed in Section V. First, in the assignment of the first six practices to the six perfections, the first five (giving, discipline, patience, vigour, meditation) are such that the sutra's wording *is* the matter of that perfection (the Practice of Joy throughout speaks of giving), the assignment direct; whereas the Practice of Excellent Manifestation assigned to prajñā has for its literal wording "the three deeds pure · abiding in non-attainment," and the assignment to prajñā is an exegetical reading (non-attainment being the mark of prajñā's emptiness-wisdom), not direct as in the first five. Second, the assignment of the latter four practices (Non-Attachment, Hard-to-Attain, Excellent Dharma, Truth) to the latter four perfections (means, vow, power, knowledge) is the classification of Chengguan's *Avataṃsaka Commentary*; the Chapter on the Ten Practices itself does not assign a perfection to each of these four per practice. This paper takes the "six perfections enacted" angle (the six as principal), and does not force the latter four practices onto the latter four perfections; their assignment is the author's to ratify.

The argument proceeds in three moves. Section II lays out the canonical basis: the full list of the ten; the evidence of the turn to benefiting others; the six perfections as the substance of the practices. Section III is the core argument, in three layers: the difference between abode and practice (3.1); the "enactment" of the six perfections (3.2); prajñā as guide (3.3). Section IV deliberately omits interdisciplinary convergence, for the reason in Section VI. Section V offers middle-way corrections, marking honestly the boundary of exegetical and classificatory assignments, the variant names, and that "the six run through the whole course, not confined to the Ten Practices." Section VI concludes, with the cross-reference index, and looks ahead to P06 (the Ten Dedications) and the Attainment-axis seam.

## II. The Canonical Basis

### 2.1 The full list of the ten practices

In the *Avataṃsaka*'s Chapter on the Ten Practices, the bodhisattva Forest-of-Merit (Guṇavana), by the Buddha's spiritual power, lists the names and number of the bodhisattva's ten practices:

> 佛子！何等是菩薩摩訶薩行？佛子！菩薩摩訶薩有十種行，三世諸佛之所宣說。何等為十？一者歡喜行，二者饒益行，三者無違逆行，四者無屈橈行，五者無癡亂行，六者善現行，七者無著行，八者難得行，九者善法行，十者真實行；是為十。[^1]
>
> *"O son of the Buddha, what is the great bodhisattva's practice? O son of the Buddha, the great bodhisattva has ten kinds of practice, taught by the buddhas of the three times. What are the ten? First, the Practice of Joy; second, the Practice of Benefiting; third, the Practice of Non-Opposition; fourth, the Practice of Indomitability; fifth, the Practice of Freedom-from-Confusion; sixth, the Practice of Excellent Manifestation; seventh, the Practice of Non-Attachment; eighth, the Practice of the Hard-to-Attain; ninth, the Practice of Excellent Dharma; tenth, the Practice of Truth. These are the ten."*

The ten in order: Joy, Benefiting, Non-Opposition, Indomitability, (Freedom-from-)Confusion, Excellent Manifestation, Non-Attachment, Hard-to-Attain, Excellent Dharma, Truth. These ten are "taught by the buddhas of the three times" — of the same rank as the Ten Abodes' "taught by the buddhas of the past, future, and present" (BODHI-P04): a fixed stational set the buddhas teach in common, not the private statement of one time or one land. (On variant names: the 80-fascicle text's general listing reads "Practice of Indomitability, Practice of (Freedom-from-)Confusion," while the per-practice headings read "Practice of Indomitability" and "Practice of Freedom-from-Confusion" (橈 a variant of 撓; "free-from-confusion" and "leaving-confusion" being one referent); and other recensions, such as the 60-fascicle *Avataṃsaka* and separate translations, have names like "Practice of Non-Anger / Practice of Reverence" not used in the 80-fascicle here. This paper throughout follows the 80-fascicle wording, sets them side by side, and does not substitute other recensions' names; see Section V.)

### 2.2 The turn to benefiting others: giving not for one's own fruit, but to benefit beings

Toward what does the conduct of the ten practices aim? The opening Practice of Joy fixes the aim. It says of the bodhisattva's giving:

> 佛子！此菩薩為大施主，凡所有物悉能惠施；其心平等，無有悔吝，不望果報，不求名稱，不貪利養；但為救護一切眾生，攝受一切眾生，饒益一切眾生。[^2]
>
> *"O son of the Buddha, this bodhisattva is a great donor, able to give away whatever he has; his mind even, without regret or grudging, not hoping for fruit, not seeking fame, not coveting gain — only to rescue all beings, to embrace all beings, to benefit all beings."*

The crux lies in the latter contrast: the bodhisattva's giving is "not hoping for fruit, not seeking fame, not coveting gain" — not for his own blessings, his own renown, his own offerings; its one aim is "only to rescue all beings, to embrace all beings, to benefit all beings." That single "only to … beings" turns the arrow of giving wholly from self to other. This is the eye of the "practice-position" as a position of benefiting others: not that giving is not practised, but that what the giving is *for* has become purely the beings.

This altruistic aim shows its dharma-giving face still more at the ninth, the "Practice of Excellent Dharma":

> 此菩薩為一切世間天、人、魔、梵、沙門、婆羅門、乾闥婆等作清涼法池，攝持正法，不斷佛種。[^3]
>
> *"This bodhisattva makes himself a cool dharma-pool for all the worlds' gods, humans, māras, brahmās, ascetics, brahmins, gandharvas, and the rest, upholding the true Dharma, not letting the Buddha-seed be cut off."*

In the Practice of Excellent Dharma the bodhisattva "makes himself a cool dharma-pool for all beings," upholds the true Dharma so the Buddha-seed is not cut off — benefiting beings by eloquence in expounding the Dharma, the dharma-giving face of benefiting others made present. Material giving (Practice of Joy) and dharma-giving (Practice of Excellent Dharma) mirror each other head and tail; the conduct of the ten practices, through and through, faces toward beings.

The treatises set a per-item correspondence for this "six perfections as benefiting others." The *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha*, in its Chapter on the Superior Mark of Entering Cause-and-Fruit, assigns the six to the mark of benefiting beings:

> 由施波羅蜜利益眾生，由戒波羅蜜不損惱眾生，由忍波羅蜜能安受彼毀辱不起報怨心，由精進波羅蜜生彼善根滅彼惡根，由此利益因一切眾生皆得調伏。[^4]
>
> *"By the perfection of giving, one benefits beings; by the perfection of discipline, one does not harm beings; by the perfection of patience, one can bear their insult without raising a vengeful mind; by the perfection of vigour, one grows their roots of good and destroys their roots of evil; and by these causes of benefit, all beings are tamed."*

Giving as benefit, discipline as not-harming, patience as bearing insult, vigour as growing good and destroying evil — the *Saṃgraha* points out one by one: each of the six perfections is itself a form of benefiting and taming beings. The six perfections and benefiting others are not two things (first cultivate the six, then go benefit), but two faces of one: the enactment of the six perfections *is* the enactment of benefiting others. (The *Yogācārabhūmi*'s Chapter on Self-and-Other Benefit likewise opens "self-benefit and other-benefit" through many gates, showing the bodhisattva's practices — the six perfections included — to embrace both self and other; complementary to this.)

### 2.3 The six perfections as the substance of the practices: the first six practices each one perfection in action

The altruistic conduct having its aim, what is its substance? The per-practice expositions of the first six practices in the Chapter on the Ten Practices are, in their very wording, the six perfections. First the treatises' fixed list of the six; the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* lists them by Sanskrit name:

> 六波羅蜜滿。何等六？檀波羅蜜、尸羅波羅蜜、羼提波羅蜜、毘梨耶波羅蜜、禪波羅蜜、般若波羅蜜。[^5]
>
> *"The six perfections fulfilled. What six? The perfection of dāna (giving), of śīla (discipline), of kṣānti (patience), of vīrya (vigour), of dhyāna (meditation), of prajñā (wisdom)."*

Dāna (giving) · śīla (discipline) · kṣānti (patience) · vīrya (vigour) · dhyāna (meditation) · prajñā (wisdom) — this is the backbone of the six perfections' nomenclature. And the first six practices of the Chapter have, each, just these six perfections for their substance:

The Practice of Joy is giving (*dāna*), its text already seen at 2.2 (a great donor, without regret or grudging, not hoping for fruit). The Practice of Benefiting is discipline (*śīla*):

> 何等為菩薩摩訶薩饒益行？此菩薩護持淨戒，於色、聲、香、味、觸，心無所著……但堅持淨戒。[^6]
>
> *"What is the great bodhisattva's Practice of Benefiting? This bodhisattva guards the pure precepts, his mind unattached to form, sound, scent, taste, touch … but firmly holding the pure precepts."*

The Practice of Non-Opposition is patience (*kṣānti*):

> 何等為菩薩摩訶薩無違逆行？此菩薩常修忍法，謙下恭敬；不自害，不他害，不兩害……令恒安住忍辱柔和。[^7]
>
> *"What is the great bodhisattva's Practice of Non-Opposition? This bodhisattva constantly cultivates the dharma of patience, humble and reverent; harming neither self, nor other, nor both … ever abiding in patient gentleness."*

The Practice of Indomitability is vigour (*vīrya*):

> 何等為菩薩摩訶薩無屈橈行？此菩薩修諸精進，所謂：第一精進、大精進、勝精進……終不為惱一眾生故而行精進。[^8]
>
> *"What is the great bodhisattva's Practice of Indomitability? This bodhisattva cultivates all kinds of vigour — foremost vigour, great vigour, surpassing vigour … and never practises vigour for the sake of troubling a single being."*

The Practice of Freedom-from-Confusion is meditation (*dhyāna*, right mindfulness):

> 何等為菩薩摩訶薩離癡亂行？此菩薩成就正念，心無散亂，堅固不動，最上清淨，廣大無量，無有迷惑。[^9]
>
> *"What is the great bodhisattva's Practice of Freedom-from-Confusion? This bodhisattva accomplishes right mindfulness, his mind without distraction, firm and unmoved, supremely pure, vast and measureless, without delusion."*

The Practice of Excellent Manifestation, then, points to prajñā (wisdom) — and the nature of this assignment must be noted: the literal wording speaks of "the three deeds pure, abiding in non-attainment":

> 何等為菩薩摩訶薩善現行？此菩薩身業清淨、語業清淨、意業清淨，住無所得、示無所得身語意業，能知三業皆無所有、無虛妄故，無有繫縛。[^10]
>
> *"What is the great bodhisattva's Practice of Excellent Manifestation? This bodhisattva's bodily deed is pure, verbal deed pure, mental deed pure; abiding in non-attainment, manifesting bodily-verbal-mental deeds of non-attainment, able to know the three deeds as wholly without own-being and without falsity, and so without bondage."*

"Abiding in non-attainment, able to know the three deeds as wholly without own-being" — this "non-attainment" is the very mark of prajñā's emptiness-wisdom, and so exegesis mostly assigns the Practice of Excellent Manifestation to prajñā. But it must be marked: this is an "assignment by mark" (matching prajñā by the mark of non-attainment), not, as in the first five practices, a wording that is word-for-word the name-and-matter of the perfection (giving, pure precepts, the dharma of patience, vigour, right mindfulness). This boundary is detailed in Section V.

As for the last of the ten, the "Practice of Truth," its text has the accord of word and deed for its mark:

> 何等為菩薩摩訶薩真實行？此菩薩成就第一誠諦之語，如說能行，如行能說……隨如來學智慧成就。[^11]
>
> *"What is the great bodhisattva's Practice of Truth? This bodhisattva accomplishes speech of the foremost sincerity and truth: as he speaks, so he can act; as he acts, so he can speak … following the Tathāgata in learning, his wisdom accomplished."*

"As he speaks, so he can act; as he acts, so he can speak" — word and deed according without deceit is the close of the ten practices through and through: arrived at truth, all that the prior nine practices do accords with what is said, word and deed as one.

The six perfections are not a flat, scattered list. The *Yogācārabhūmi* judges them to have the warp and weft of two portions, merit and wisdom:

> 謂略說福，即是三種波羅蜜多。一、施波羅蜜多，二、戒波羅蜜多，三、忍波羅蜜多。智唯一種波羅蜜多。謂慧波羅蜜多。精進、靜慮波羅蜜多，應知通二分。一者、福分，二者、智分。[^12]
>
> *"In brief, merit is the three perfections: first, the perfection of giving; second, of discipline; third, of patience. Wisdom is the single perfection of wisdom (prajñā). The perfections of vigour and of meditation, know, run through both portions — the portion of merit and the portion of wisdom."*

Giving, discipline, patience as the merit-portion; wisdom (prajñā) as the wisdom-portion; vigour and meditation running through both — the six are no structureless juxtaposition but have the two warps of "merit" and "wisdom" running through them. This structure leads precisely to what Section III, 3.3 will say: prajñā as guide.

## III. The Core Argument

Section II presented three things: the full list of the ten practices; the turn to benefiting others (giving not for one's own fruit but for beings, the Practice of Excellent Dharma as a cool dharma-pool, the *Saṃgraha*'s per-item benefiting); the six perfections as the substance of the first six practices (with the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s merit-and-wisdom portions showing the six's structure). This section, in three layers: the difference between abode and practice (3.1); the "enactment" of the six perfections (3.2); prajñā as guide (3.3).

### 3.1 The difference between abode and practice: the completion of self-benefit turning into the conduct of benefiting others

The difference between abode-position and practice-position can be put in a phrase: the abode is "completing," the practice is "doing"; what the abode completes is oneself (maturing into a son of the Buddha within the family), what the practice does is benefiting others (going out to benefit beings). BODHI-P04 made clear that the Ten Abodes are an arc of "entering the Buddha-family as a son of the Buddha," nurturing, reaching at the Abode of Consecration "the accomplishment of ten knowledges, fit to do the Dharma-King's work" — the weight of this stretch lies in "nurturing," making good and ripening the bodhisattva standing within the family. The standing once made good (son of the Buddha), the position once non-regressing (BODHI-P03), what follows is naturally not to keep "nurturing" but to "use" — to put what was nurtured to use in benefiting others. The Ten Practices as "practice" are just this "putting to use."

This too dovetails with the axis's master thesis. BODHI-P01 established that the bodhisattva path's aim is not "taking realization" but "the accumulation of delivering beings in non-abiding nirvāṇa" — its measure being accumulation, measuring "for whom what was accumulated." The practice-position is just the first stretch of dense enactment in which this "accumulation of delivering beings" comes down to the concrete: the conduct of the six perfections, each one a provision accumulated for beings. The abode-position made good the one who can accumulate (the son of the Buddha); the practice-position is where that one begins, densely, to accumulate and to deliver.

A contrast: the medical student "completing training" versus the physician "in practice." The Ten Abodes are like a medical student receiving the full nurture within the academy — finishing the coursework, passing the examinations, receiving the licence (investiture, fit to do the Dharma-King's work). But getting the licence is not the aim of studying medicine; practising it is. Licence in hand, the physician walks out of the academy, enters the consulting room, and puts the learning to use on patients (going out to benefit others). The work of the practising stage is no longer "to learn" but "to treat" — surgery by surgery, patient by patient. The practice-position is to the bodhisattva what practice is to the physician: the standing (son of the Buddha / physician) fixed, it is now the time to go out, take up the work, and densely benefit others. The abode is completing training; the practice is being in practice.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the Ten Abodes are the self-benefit completion of "entering and maturing in the Buddha-family, fit at consecration to do the Dharma-King's work" (BODHI-P04); the Ten Practices above them are the conduct of "going out to benefit others." This turn dovetails with the axis's master thesis "the bodhisattva path aims at the accumulation of delivering beings in non-abiding nirvāṇa" (BODHI-P01) — the practice-position being the dense enactment of accumulating and delivering. The abode completes the one; the practice uses the one.

### 3.2 The "enactment" of the six perfections: from a list of virtues to actions presently performed

The most crucial disclosure of the practice-position is this: here the six perfections are "enacted." People are used to reciting the six perfections as a list of virtues — giving, discipline, patience, vigour, meditation, wisdom, six names. But the first six practices of the Chapter on the Ten Practices tell us: these six names are not abstract entries but six recognizable actions being presently performed. The Practice of Joy is not "a theory about giving" but "the very giving the bodhisattva is doing — without regret or grudging, not hoping for fruit, only for beings"; the Practice of Benefiting is not "the definition of discipline" but "the very pure precepts the bodhisattva is guarding, unattached to the five sense-fields" (2.3). The six perfections turn from "what they are" (virtues) into "what is being done" (conduct) — this is the sense of "enactment."

And with the enacting, the arrow turns to benefiting others. The *Saṃgraha* has pointed out one by one: giving is benefiting beings, discipline is not harming beings, patience is bearing beings' insult, vigour is growing beings' good and destroying beings' evil (2.2). That is: once the six perfections are enacted, each perfection is itself a form of benefiting others — not first cultivating the six and then taking them to benefit others, but the enactment of the six perfections being, in the very act, the enactment of benefiting others. This explains why the Chapter's Practice of Joy says at the outset "only to benefit all beings": because this word "practice" itself carries "acting for others."

A contrast: the dish-name on the menu, versus the dish presently rising from the pan in the kitchen. "Kung Pao chicken," four words printed on a menu, is a name, a description — you read it, you know "what it is," yet there is not a bite to eat. Only when the cook makes it on the stove, brings it to the table, fills the guest, does it pass from "dish-name" to "a dish presently filling someone." The six perfections as a list of virtues are six dish-names on a menu; the six perfections of the practice-position are six dishes the kitchen is presently making and presently bringing out to fill people (benefiting others). "Enactment" is just this step from dish-name to served — and the dish was, all along, for someone to eat (practised for others' benefit).

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the wording of the first six practices in the Chapter is the concrete action of the six perfections (giving · pure precepts · the dharma of patience · vigour · right mindfulness, 2.3), turning the six from abstract virtues into recognizable conduct; while the *Saṃgraha*'s per-item assignment of the six to benefiting beings (2.2) shows this enactment to be, in the very act, the form of benefiting others. The "enactment" of the six perfections is the turn from "what they are" to "what is being done for beings."

### 3.3 Prajñā as guide: the five without prajñā are blind, and with prajñā are named "gone to the far shore"

The six perfections enacted, others benefited — is it then complete? It still lacks an eye. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*, by the figure of the blind and the guide, marks the pivot among the six:

> 五波羅蜜亦如是，離般若波羅蜜，如盲無導，不能趣道、不能得一切種智。憍尸迦！若五波羅蜜得般若波羅蜜將導，是時五波羅蜜名為有眼；般若波羅蜜將導，得波羅蜜名字。[^13]
>
> *"So too the five perfections: apart from the perfection of prajñā, they are like the blind without a guide, unable to approach the Way, unable to attain omniscience. Kauśika! If the five perfections gain the perfection of prajñā as guide, then the five are named 'having eyes'; with prajñā as guide, they gain the name 'perfection.'"*

These five — giving, discipline, patience, vigour, meditation — apart from prajñā are "like the blind without a guide": they can walk, but cannot see the road, cannot reach the far shore, cannot attain omniscience; only with prajñā guiding (leading the way) are the five "named 'having eyes,'" and only then do they accomplish the name "perfection (gone to the far shore)." That is, what makes the first five "perfections (conduct gone to the far shore)" rather than merely worldly good deeds is just this eye of prajñā. Giving without prajñā is only the merit of giving, not giving "perfected"; with prajñā guiding it, giving reaches the far shore.

This answers to the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s merit-and-wisdom portions cited at 2.3: giving, discipline, patience as merit-portion, prajñā as wisdom-portion, vigour and meditation through both — the merit-portion perfections, without the wisdom-portion (prajñā) for guide, are merit without wisdom, like the blind walking the road. It also tallies with the sequence of the ten practices: prajñā (the Practice of Excellent Manifestation, sixth) comes after the first five (giving, discipline, patience, vigour, meditation), occupying just the pivotal place of "guiding the first five to completion, bringing them to the far shore." That the practice-position practises the six perfections and benefits others, yet does not lapse into defiled good and can become conduct "gone to the far shore," lies precisely in this prajñā of non-attainment for its eye.

This too answers the spine, consistent through `sramana` and this axis, of "insight as guide": the śrāvaka's fruit is measured not by experience but by severed fetters (the path of seeing) (`sramana`-P01); the bodhisattva's position-non-regress is held not by experience but by the view's not turning (BODHI-P03); the bodhisattva's conduct is brought to the far shore not by the quantity of merit but by prajñā as guide (this paper). Subtraction and accumulation, self-benefit and other-benefit — what runs through them all is this one eye of "insight as guide."

A contrast: a party on the road — however strong their legs, however heavy their loads, however firm their will, if there is no one who can see the road to lead them (a sighted one leading the blind), the whole party may in the end go the wrong way, straying further and further. The first five perfections are like leg-strength, loads, will (the conduct of merit-provisions); prajñā is like the one who alone can see the road and leads. Without the leader, the harder they walk, the further from the far shore they may go (blind without a guide, unable to approach the Way); with the leader in front, that same party's same exertion truly heads for the far shore (having eyes, gaining the name "perfection"). The "exertion" of giving, discipline, and the rest is real merit; but for that merit to "reach the far shore," prajñā must lead in front.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*'s figure of the blind and the guide shows the six perfections to be no flat list — the five apart from prajñā are like the blind unable to approach the Way, and with prajñā as guide are "having eyes," gaining the name "perfection (gone to the far shore)"; joined with the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s merit-and-wisdom portions (prajñā the wisdom-portion, the rest merit or through both), this establishes prajñā as the guide-head of the six. The practice-position practises the six and benefits others, with the prajñā of non-attainment for its eye, and so becomes conduct gone to the far shore — consistent with the axis's spine of "insight as guide."

Taken together, the three layers — the abode completes the one, the practice uses the one: the Ten Practices are self-benefit's completion turning into benefiting others' conduct (3.1); the six perfections, enacted in the practice-position, turn from a list of virtues into recognizable conduct that is, in the act, benefiting others (3.2); and the six, with prajñā as guide — the five apart from prajñā blind, with prajñā named gone to the far shore (3.3) — jointly establish the thesis: **the Ten Practices are the bodhisattva's position of going out to benefit others, their substance the concrete enactment of the six perfections, their direction expressly the benefiting of beings, and, with the prajñā of non-attainment for eye, made into conduct gone to the far shore.**

## IV. Interdisciplinary Convergence

(This paper deliberately omits the interdisciplinary-convergence section; the rationale and explicit skip-declaration appear in Section VI. In brief: the paper's main axis is the Mahāyāna doctrine of conduct, "the six perfections enacted as the conduct of benefiting others" and "prajñā as guide"; contemporary models of "altruistic behaviour," "virtue in practice," or "wisdom-guided action," though superficially analogous, cannot sustain a three-layered convergence — likeness / fundamental difference / irreducibility — over the senses of "only to benefit beings, not hoping for one's own fruit," "the five apart from prajñā blind, with prajñā named gone to the far shore," and "the merit-and-wisdom portions" without lapsing into forced analogy. So, per the series' editorial convention, it is cut, and the skip declared here.)

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

This section clears away four misreadings; the first two bear on the boundary of assignment, and must be marked honestly.

**⚠️ Correction 1: assigning the latter four practices to the latter four perfections (means, vow, power, knowledge) is the classification of Chengguan's commentary, not a per-practice assignment in the sutra; this paper does not force it.** The traditional "six perfections matched to ten practices" mostly assigns the latter four practices — Non-Attachment, Hard-to-Attain, Excellent Dharma, Truth — to the four perfections of means, vow, power, knowledge (the latter four when the six are opened into ten); this reading issues from the classification of Chengguan's *Avataṃsaka Commentary*, while the Chapter on the Ten Practices itself does not assign a perfection per practice to these four. This paper takes the "six perfections enacted" angle (the six as principal axis), and so argues only from what the sutra directly shows — the first six practices each assigned to the six perfections (with the Practice of Excellent Manifestation assigned to prajñā being an assignment-by-mark, see Correction 2); as for the latter four practices, this paper reads their sense from what the sutra gives (the Practice of Excellent Dharma as dharma-giving altruism, "a cool dharma-pool"; the Practice of Truth as the close of the ten, "word and deed according, following the Tathāgata in learning, wisdom accomplished"), and does not assign them to the latter four perfections. The per-item definitions of the latter four perfections (means · vow · power · knowledge) are found in the *Yogācārabhūmi* (the full list of the ten and the definitions of the latter four, T30n1579_p0565c19) and the *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* (*upāya-kauśalya · praṇidhāna · bala · jñāna*, judged as the non-discriminating subsequent wisdom, T31n1593_p0126b19), belonging to the treatises' development of the "six opened into ten" structure, serving chiefly the Attainment axis and the later positions (the Ten Grounds); this paper does not unfold them. The assignment of the latter four practices is the author's to ratify; this round does not ratify it.

**⚠️ Correction 2: the Practice of Excellent Manifestation = prajñā is an assignment-by-mark, not a word-for-word assignment in the sutra.** The first five practices (Joy, Benefiting, Non-Opposition, Indomitability, Freedom-from-Confusion) assigned to giving, discipline, patience, vigour, meditation are such that the sutra's wording *is* the matter of that perfection — the Practice of Joy throughout speaks of giving, the Practice of Benefiting of guarding pure precepts, Non-Opposition of constantly cultivating the dharma of patience, Indomitability of cultivating all kinds of vigour, Freedom-from-Confusion of accomplishing right mindfulness with the mind undistracted; the assignment direct, with all but no room for exegesis. The Practice of Excellent Manifestation assigned to prajñā is otherwise: the literal wording speaks of "the three deeds pure, abiding in non-attainment, knowing the three deeds as wholly without own-being, without bondage" (2.3); it is because this "non-attainment" is the mark of prajñā's emptiness-wisdom that exegesis assigns the Practice of Excellent Manifestation to prajñā. This paper takes that assignment, for "non-attainment" is indeed the signature of prajñā; but it must be marked that this is an assignment-by-mark ("matching the perfection by the mark"), not, as with the first five, a name-and-matter that is word-for-word the perfection. Let the reader know the nature of this assignment differs from the first five.

**⚠️ Correction 3: variant names set side by side, no substitution from other recensions.** Within the names of the ten practices, the 80-fascicle text itself has wording-differences: the general listing reads "Practice of Indomitability, Practice of (Freedom-from-)Confusion," while the per-practice headings read "Practice of Indomitability" and "Practice of Freedom-from-Confusion" (橈 a variant of 撓; "free-from-confusion" being "leaving-confusion," one referent). And other recensions (the 60-fascicle *Avataṃsaka*, separate translations) have for some practices variant names such as "Practice of Non-Anger / Practice of Reverence," not used in the 80-fascicle here. This paper throughout follows the 80-fascicle (T0279) wording, sets co-referential variants such as "free-from-confusion / leaving-confusion" side by side without forcing uniformity, and does not substitute other recensions' names for the 80-fascicle's. Should a reader meet a practice under another name elsewhere, let it be known that it is another recension's, or that source's, selection.

**❌ Misreading 4: "the six perfections enacted in the practice-position" = the six begin at the Ten Practices, or are confined to them.** The six perfections run through the bodhisattva's whole course, not owned or begun by the Ten Practices alone. The abode-position bodhisattva already cultivates the six throughout (the six being the bodhisattva's common provision); the ten-ground positions too each have one perfection in special ascendancy, as the Chapter on the Ten Grounds sets, at the first Ground of Joy, "of the ten perfections, the perfection of giving is in ascendancy; the rest are not un-cultivated, but according to capacity and portion"[^14], the later grounds following this formula to assign one perfection in ascendancy per ground. Plainly the six run through the bodhisattva path from start to finish, not "first cultivated at the Ten Practices" or "no longer cultivated past them." The mark of the practice-position is not "beginning to cultivate the six," but "taking the enactment of the six as this position's principal conduct, expressly turned to benefiting others, with prajñā as guide to bring them to the far shore" — the six densely, altruistically, wisdom-guidedly enacted in this position. Let one not, because this paper says "the six enacted in the practice-position," misread the six as confined to the Ten Practices.

## VI. Conclusion

**Essential restatement.** The Ten Practices (the middle of the three worthy ranks) are the bodhisattva's position of going out to benefit others, their substance the concrete enactment of the six perfections, their direction expressly the benefiting of beings. On the textual side, the *Avataṃsaka*'s Chapter on the Ten Practices lists the full ten (taught by the buddhas of the three times), the opening Practice of Joy marking giving as "not hoping for fruit, not seeking fame, not coveting gain; only to benefit all beings," the Practice of Excellent Dharma as "a cool dharma-pool for all beings" (the turn to benefiting others), and the first six practices' wording being one by one the action of the six perfections (giving, discipline, patience, vigour, meditation, and the Practice of Excellent Manifestation as prajñā by assignment-of-mark); the treatises set the backbone — the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* lists the six and, by the blind-and-guide figure, sets prajñā as guide; the *Yogācārabhūmi* sorts the merit-and-wisdom portions; the *Saṃgraha* assigns the six one by one to benefiting beings so that they are tamed. On the doctrinal side, three layers support it: the abode completes the one, the practice uses the one — the Ten Practices are self-benefit's completion turning into benefiting others' conduct (3.1); the six, enacted in the practice-position, turn from a virtue-list into conduct that is, in the act, benefiting others (3.2); the six, with prajñā as guide — the five apart from prajñā blind, with prajñā named gone to the far shore (3.3). Honest marks: the Practice of Excellent Manifestation = prajñā is an assignment-by-mark; the latter four practices to the latter four perfections is Chengguan's classification, which this paper does not force; names follow the 80-fascicle side by side; the six run through the whole course, not confined to the Ten Practices (Section V).

**Guidance for practice.** Here the practitioner should grasp two things. First, benefiting others is not deferred until self-cultivation is complete. Let no one hold the mind "once I have perfected the six, then I will go benefit people" — that is precisely to invert the sequence. The work of the practice-position is, from the first, to "enact the six perfections within the conduct of benefiting others": your one act of giving now — without regret or grudging, not hoping for fruit, only for the other — is, in the very act, the perfection of giving in practice; your one patient bearing of insult without a vengeful mind is, in the act, the perfection of patience in practice. The six are not two stages of first-perfect-then-benefit, but the one thing whose enactment is benefiting others. Second, in doing all good, one must have the prajñā of non-attainment for eye. With only leg-strength (the merit-conduct of giving, discipline, patience, vigour, meditation) and no eye to lead the way (prajñā), the merit, though real, walks like the blind and may not reach the far shore; so in the doing of good, illumine it with the wisdom of non-attainment — not hoping for fruit, not grasping the marks of giver, receiver, gift; this "non-attainment" is the eye that makes good conduct a "perfection." The six are no preserve of eminent monks but conduct performable in daily use; and what makes daily good reach the far shore is prajñā.

**Open questions.** First, this paper deliberately cut the Section IV interdisciplinary convergence, for the reason above. Second, the assignment of the latter four practices (Non-Attachment, Hard-to-Attain, Excellent Dharma, Truth) to the latter four perfections (means, vow, power, knowledge) — Chengguan's classification — and the structure of "the six opened into ten" (the *Yogācārabhūmi*'s and *Saṃgraha*'s definitions of means, vow, power, knowledge), this paper, taking the six-perfection angle, has neither assigned nor unfolded; once the author ratifies the latter four practices' assignment, or a later round of commentarial source-gathering is assembled, a separate paper or the Attainment axis should treat them. Third, the practice-position densely enacts the six and accumulates the good of benefiting others — how is this accumulated good to be settled, its direction turned? BODHI-P06 will treat the Ten Dedications (the last of the three worthy ranks): the threefold dedication (dedicating to beings, to bodhi, to the real), turning the good the practice-position has accumulated, and tied to "practice-non-regress" — the turn from "the conduct of going out to benefit others" to "the turning of conduct toward the fruit."

**The seam (SEAM).** This paper meets the Attainment-axis `attainment-signatures/bodhisattva` at the two faces of the "six perfections": the Realization axis (this paper) tells the "conduct" of the six (their enactment — how the six are acted out in benefiting others); the Attainment axis reads the "fruit-virtue of mastery" the six accomplish (the bodhisattva mastery the ripening of the six signs). The treatise-anchors on the perfections cited here are, in the source units, mostly also marked for the Attainment axis, precisely because of one and the same six perfections, the Realization reading their enactment, the Attainment reading their mastery. It must be strictly distinguished from the Attainment-axis `tathagata`'s fruit-ground: the six perfections practised at the practice-position are causal-stage conduct, and the mastery they accomplish is causal-stage, a bodhisattva-portion mastery, not the mastery of the Tathāgata's fruit-ground omniscient knowledge. The doctrine of the seam is the author's to ratify; this paper does not speak for the Attainment axis.
