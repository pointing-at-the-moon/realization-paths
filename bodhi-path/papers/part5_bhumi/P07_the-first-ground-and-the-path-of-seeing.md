# The First Ground and the Path of Seeing

**The Ground of Joy · The Pass of Entering the Noble from the Worthy · Severing the Discriminated Afflictions on the Path of Seeing; the Great Turn from Suppression to Severance**

*中文主題：初地見道——賢入聖與見道斷分別惑*

## Abstract

The preceding paper, BODHI-P06, brought the bodhisattva to the close of the three worthy ranks — the Ten Dedications full, practice-non-regress established, the bodhisattva poised at the threshold of the noble ranks; yet thus far not one affliction has been severed outright: the three worthy ranks (abodes · practices · dedications), throughout, are positions of "suppression," holding afflictions down by understanding without cutting their root. This paper argues: the first ground (the Ground of Joy) is precisely the pass of "entering the noble from the worthy," its pivot the "path of seeing" — here the bodhisattva first realizes the noble's place, first directly contemplates suchness by the wisdom of the equality of all dharmas, and on the path of seeing severs outright the "discriminated afflictions" (the bonds of view that are the path-of-seeing's to sever); this "severance" is not the three worthy ranks' "suppression" but the first cutting of the afflictions' root, whence "joy" ("first realizing the noble's place, much joy arises"). In the *Avataṃsaka*'s Chapter on the Ten Grounds and the *Daśabhūmika-sūtra* (Xuanzang's rendering "the Ground of Supreme Joy," the variant set side by side), the ten grounds from Joy to Dharma-Cloud are listed, taught in common by the buddhas of the three times; Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna* glosses the first ground as "first realizing the noble's place, much joy arises" (the first of entering-noble-from-worthy), classes its obstacle as "the ordinary being's self-mark obstacle," and judges plainly that "the bonds of view are severed at the first ground, on the path of seeing," while the three realms' "afflictions of the path of cultivation" "thin and recede gradually across the grounds," taking "on the path of seeing" and "on the path of cultivation" as the backbone of the ground-path. From this the paper argues three things: entering-the-noble as "first realizing the noble's place" (3.1); the path of seeing as "severance," not "suppression" (3.2); and the first ground's path-of-seeing severance of the discriminated afflictions versus the arhat's path-of-seeing severance — "one and the same grammar of severance, a different exit" (3.3: the same severing, yet the two vehicles head for quiescence while the bodhisattva, not abiding, climbs on). An honest mark: pairing "discriminated afflictions" with "the bonds of view severed on the path of seeing" is the classification of later Yogācāra (the *Cheng weishi lun* line), not the express word of T0279/T0287/T1522 (which say "the bonds of view" against "the afflictions of the path of cultivation"); this paper marks that classificatory nature, the equation to be ratified by the author; the Joy / Supreme-Joy variant is set side by side, no side taken. The conclusion: the first ground's path of seeing is a great turning-point of the whole bodhisattva path, from "suppression" to "severance," from worthy into noble; what is severed is only the discriminated afflictions — the innate afflictions await gradual severance across the grounds of cultivation (opening BODHI-P08).

**Keywords**: first ground, Ground of Joy, Ground of Supreme Joy, path of seeing, entering the noble from the worthy, severing the discriminated afflictions, bonds of view, path-of-seeing severance, the ordinary being's self-mark obstacle, suppression versus severance, same severance different exit, the Ten Grounds, Avataṃsaka Ten Grounds chapter, Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna, Yogācāra classification

## I. The Question

The last paper brought the bodhisattva to the very end of the three worthy ranks. The Ten Dedications full, practice-non-regress established, the accumulated good wholly dedicated to the three places with no self-fruit to regress into, the bodhisattva stands poised at the threshold of the noble ranks (BODHI-P06). Yet if we turn back and press one question — across the ten faiths, ten abodes, ten practices, ten dedications, has the bodhisattva ever actually *severed* a single affliction? — the answer is: not yet. The three worthy ranks are, throughout, positions of "suppression" — by the understanding won through hearing, reflection, and cultivation, they press the afflictions *down* so they do not arise in act, but they have not cut their root. To suppress is like pressing grass with a stone: the grass lies flat, but the root remains; let conditions gather, and it rises again. The whole of the three worthy ranks is "suppressed but not severed."

Hence a sharp turning-question presses: when does the bodhisattva turn from "suppression" to "severance"? When does he truly enter the "noble" from the "worthy"? This question is answered precisely at the beginning of the ten grounds — the first ground (the Ground of Joy). The pass the canon gives is named the "path of seeing." The path of seeing is the position of first directly contemplating suchness by the wisdom of the equality of all dharmas, of personally seeing the real mark of things; here, "on the path of seeing," the bodhisattva for the first time severs outright the "discriminated afflictions" (the bonds of view that are the path-of-seeing's to sever), cutting the root of affliction for the first time. This severance is sharply distinct in kind from the three worthy ranks' suppression: suppression presses down, severance cuts the root. Vasubandhu glosses the first ground's name: "first realizing the noble's place, much joy arises, hence it is named the Ground of Joy" — it is named "joy" precisely because this is the bodhisattva's first personal realization of the noble's place, his first true severing of the affliction-root, a joy without precedent.

The thesis of this paper: **the first ground (the Ground of Joy) is the bodhisattva's pass of "entering the noble from the worthy," its pivot the "path of seeing" — here he first realizes the noble's place, first directly contemplates suchness by the wisdom of equality, and on the path of seeing severs outright the discriminated afflictions (the bonds of view that are the path-of-seeing's to sever); this "severance" is not the three worthy ranks' "suppression" but the first cutting of the affliction-root, whence "joy." And this first ground's path-of-seeing severance of the discriminated afflictions and the śrāvaka arhat's path-of-seeing severance are "one and the same grammar of severance, a different exit" — both sever the path-of-seeing's afflictions, yet the two vehicles head for quiescence and take the fruit, while the bodhisattva, not abiding, climbs on through the grounds.** This takes the baton from BODHI-P06: P06 established the bodhisattva's "practice-non-regress" at the close of the three worthy ranks, suppression at its utmost, poised at the noble; this paper says that bodhisattva, poised at the threshold of the noble ranks, on the first ground's path of seeing turns for the first time from suppression to severance, from worthy into noble.

By placement, the first ground is the first of the "ten noble" ranks (the ten grounds), and the last leap of the whole causal-stage triad of "ordinary (the outer-ordinary ten faiths) — worthy (the thirty minds of the three worthy ranks) — noble (the ten grounds)." The Ten Dedications full is the capstone of the "worthy"; the first ground's path of seeing is the dawn of the "noble." From here on, what the bodhisattva does is no longer "suppressing afflictions while accumulating provisions" but "severing afflictions ground by ground while heading for buddha-wisdom" — the work of severance formally begins at the first ground's path of seeing.

One boundary of sources must be stated honestly up front, detailed in Section V. This paper uses, throughout, the paired terms "discriminated afflictions (the path-of-seeing's to sever) / innate afflictions (the path-of-cultivation's to sever)" to mark what the path of seeing severs; yet it must be known that to pair "the bonds of view that are the path-of-seeing's to sever" with "the discriminated (parikalpita-arisen) afflictions," and "the afflictions that are the path-of-cultivation's to sever" with "the innate (sahaja-arisen) afflictions" — this set of equations is the classification of later Yogācāra (the *Cheng weishi lun* line), not the verbatim express word of this round's three texts (the *Avataṃsaka* T0279, the *Daśabhūmika-sūtra* T0287, Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna* T1522). The express word of the three texts reads "the bonds of view (severed at the first ground, on the path of seeing)" against "the afflictions of the path of cultivation (thinning and receding gradually across the grounds)" (§2.3). This paper argues from the three texts' plain judgment of "the path of seeing severs the bonds of view, the path of cultivation severs the rest," and where it marks what is severed by the word "discriminated afflictions," it marks that this is a later Yogācāra classification; the final ratification of the equation is the author's, on the basis of school and treatise. The Yogācāra (*Cheng weishi lun* line) classificatory texts lie beyond this round's designated source, to be supplied in a later round of source-gathering.

The argument proceeds in three moves. Section II lays out the canonical basis: the full list of the ten grounds (the *Avataṃsaka* line and the *Daśabhūmika*'s variant set side by side); the first ground's name-meaning, "first realizing the noble's place, much joy arises" (the first of entering-noble-from-worthy); the canonical evidence for severing the bonds of view and the first obstacle of the self-mark. Section III is the core argument, in three layers: entering-the-noble as "first realizing the noble's place"; the path of seeing as "severance," not "suppression"; one grammar of severance, a different exit. Section IV deliberately omits interdisciplinary convergence, for the reason in Section VI. Section V offers middle-way corrections, marking honestly the Yogācāra discriminated/innate classification, the Joy/Supreme-Joy variant, and the misreading "the first ground severs all afflictions." Section VI concludes, with the cross-reference index, and looks ahead to BODHI-P08 (the second to seventh grounds and the cultivation-severance of the innate afflictions) and the Attainment-axis seam.

## II. The Canonical Basis

### 2.1 The full list of the ten grounds: Joy to Dharma-Cloud, taught in common by the buddhas of the three times (the *Avataṃsaka* line and the *Daśabhūmika* variant side by side)

The bodhisattva's noble ranks are ten. The *Avataṃsaka*'s Chapter on the Ten Grounds lists their names and number:

> 何等為十？一者歡喜地，二者離垢地，三者發光地，四者焰慧地，五者難勝地，六者現前地，七者遠行地，八者不動地，九者善慧地，十者法雲地。佛子！此菩薩十地，三世諸佛已說、當說、今說。[^1]
>
> *"What are the ten? First, the Ground of Joy; second, the Ground of Purity; third, the Ground of Light; fourth, the Ground of Radiant Wisdom; fifth, the Ground Hard to Conquer; sixth, the Ground of Manifestation; seventh, the Far-Going Ground; eighth, the Immovable Ground; ninth, the Ground of Good Wisdom; tenth, the Ground of the Dharma-Cloud. O son of the Buddha! These ten bodhisattva grounds the buddhas of the three times have taught, will teach, and now teach."*

The ten in order: Joy, Purity, Light, Radiant Wisdom, Hard to Conquer, Manifestation, Far-Going, Immovable, Good Wisdom, Dharma-Cloud. Of the same rank as the ten abodes, ten practices, and ten dedications, these ten grounds too are "taught, will be taught, now taught by the buddhas of the three times" — a fixed noble-rank set the buddhas teach in common. This paper treats its first: the Ground of Joy (the first ground).

The same ten grounds the *Daśabhūmika-sūtra* (Xuanzang's rendering) also lists, the first ground's name only slightly differing:

> 何等為十？一名極喜地，二名離垢，三名發光，四名焰慧，五名難勝，六名現前，七名遠行，八名不動，九名善慧，十名法雲。唯諸佛子！此名一切菩薩十地。[^2]
>
> *"What are the ten? First named the Ground of Supreme Joy; second, Purity; third, Light; fourth, Radiant Wisdom; fifth, Hard to Conquer; sixth, Manifestation; seventh, Far-Going; eighth, Immovable; ninth, Good Wisdom; tenth, Dharma-Cloud. O sons of the Buddha! These are named the ten grounds of all bodhisattvas."*

Xuanzang's version reads "the Ground of Supreme Joy," the *Avataṃsaka* line "the Ground of Joy"; the two name the same first ground, the remaining nine identical. This paper, at the listing, follows the *Avataṃsaka* line as "the Ground of Joy" (to keep with the axis's prior papers and the *Avataṃsaka*-line naming standard), and marks that Xuanzang renders it "the Ground of Supreme Joy," setting the two side by side, selecting no uniform name for the author (detailed in Section V).

### 2.2 The first ground's name-meaning: "first realizing the noble's place, much joy arises" — the first of entering-noble-from-worthy

Why is the first ground named "Joy"? Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna*, glossing the general meaning of the ten grounds, expounds each name; of the first ground it says:

> 成就無上自利利他行，初證聖處多生歡喜，故名歡喜地。[^3]
>
> *"Accomplishing the unsurpassed practice of benefiting self and others, first realizing the noble's place, much joy arises — hence it is named the Ground of Joy."*

The key lies in the four words "first realizing the noble's place." "The noble's place" is the noble rank, the suchness-real realized on the path of seeing; "first realizing" is the first personal realization — this is the bodhisattva's first true stepping into the noble rank, his first personal sight of the real mark. Precisely because it is "first realizing," because it is the first entering-noble-from-worthy and the first true severing of the affliction-root since beginningless time, "much joy arises," and so it is named the Ground of Joy. "Joy" here is not vague gladness but the dharma-joy welling from the qualitative change of "first realizing the noble's place." This phrase "first realizing the noble's place" is the very pivot of "entering the noble from the worthy" — in one stroke it marks the first ground as the beginning of the noble rank, the pass from worthy into noble.

### 2.3 The path of seeing severs the bonds of view, the path of cultivation severs the rest: the first obstacle of the self-mark, the two times as backbone

What does the first ground "sever"? Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna* gives a plain judgment. First, the treatise lists the ten grounds as each countering one obstacle, and what the first ground counters is the first of the ten obstacles:

> 何故定說菩薩十地？對治十種障故。何者十障？一者凡夫我相障；二者邪行於眾生身等障；三者闇相於聞思修等諸法忘障；四者解法慢障；五者身淨我慢障；六者微煩惱習障；七者細相習障；八者於無相有行障；九者不能善利益眾生障；十者於諸法中不得自在障。[^4]
>
> *"Why are the bodhisattva grounds set firmly at ten? Because they counter ten kinds of obstacle. What are the ten obstacles? First, the ordinary being's self-mark obstacle; second, the obstacle of wrong conduct toward beings' bodies and the like; third, the obstacle of darkness, forgetting the dharmas of hearing-reflecting-cultivating and the rest; fourth, the obstacle of conceit toward understood dharmas; fifth, the obstacle of conceit-of-self in bodily purity; sixth, the obstacle of subtle affliction-habit; seventh, the obstacle of fine-mark habit; eighth, the obstacle of applied effort within the markless; ninth, the obstacle of inability to benefit beings well; tenth, the obstacle of non-mastery within the dharmas."*

The first of the ten obstacles is "the ordinary being's self-mark obstacle" — the obstacle of the ordinary being's grasping the mark of self. What the first ground breaks is just this "self-mark obstacle": passing from ordinary into noble, the first thing severed is the mark of the discriminated self-grasping. And note the order of these ten obstacles, from coarse (the self-mark) gradually into fine (the markless · non-mastery) — this order of "coarse gradually into fine" is the very rubric of the ten grounds' severance-process (the fine afflictions of the second to seventh grounds are treated in the next paper, BODHI-P08).

Second, and most crucially, Vasubandhu plainly judges the boundary of "the path-of-seeing's to sever" and "the path-of-cultivation's to sever":

> 論曰：一切欲縛轉復微薄者，斷一切修道欲、色、無色界所有煩惱，及彼因同無明習氣，皆悉微薄遠離故。諸見縛者，於初地中見道時已斷。如經「是菩薩一切欲縛轉復微薄，乃至諸見縛者先已除滅」故。[^5]
>
> *"The treatise says: 'all the bonds of desire turn ever more attenuated' means that the afflictions of the three realms — desire, form, formless — that are the path of cultivation's to sever, together with their cause, the co-arisen ignorance-habit, all thin and recede. As for the bonds of view, they were severed at the first ground, on the path of seeing. As the sūtra says, 'this bodhisattva's bonds of desire turn ever more attenuated, and the bonds of view were already extinguished beforehand.'"*

This passage is the pivotal express text of the ground-path architecture. Vasubandhu judges: "the bonds of view" (what the path of seeing severs) were already severed (already "extinguished beforehand") "at the first ground, on the path of seeing"; while the three realms' (desire · form · formless) "afflictions of the path of cultivation" (what the path of cultivation severs) "thin and recede gradually across the grounds." One severed (the bonds of view, severed outright at the first ground's path of seeing), one gradually attenuated (the cultivation-afflictions, severed gradually across the grounds of cultivation) — the distinction "the path of seeing severs the bonds of view, the path of cultivation severs the rest" is here plainly established. What this paper calls "the first ground's path-of-seeing severance of the discriminated afflictions" rests precisely on this plain judgment that "the bonds of view were severed at the first ground, on the path of seeing" (the Yogācāra classificatory nature of marking the bonds of view by "discriminated afflictions" is detailed in Section V).

Third, the treatise takes the two times, "on the path of seeing" and "on the path of cultivation," as the backbone of the realization-process:

> 五者證入，於一切法平等智見道時中善……六者不放逸入，於修道時中遠離一……[^6]
>
> *"Fifth, the entering of realization, on the path of seeing, within the wisdom of the equality of all dharmas …; sixth, the entering of non-negligence, on the path of cultivation, departing from …"*

The treatise ties "the entering of realization" to "on the path of seeing, within the wisdom of the equality of all dharmas," and "the entering of non-negligence" to "on the path of cultivation" — the two times, "the path of seeing" and "the path of cultivation," set side by side, are the backbone of the ten grounds' ground-path architecture. The first ground's path of seeing (this paper) belongs to the "entering of realization on the path of seeing," directly contemplating suchness by the wisdom of equality and severing the bonds of view; the second ground and above (the next paper, P08) belong to the "entering of non-negligence on the path of cultivation," gradually severing the cultivation-afflictions.

## III. The Core Argument

Section II presented three things: the full list of the ten grounds (Joy to Dharma-Cloud, the variant side by side); the first ground's name-meaning, "first realizing the noble's place, much joy arises" (the first of entering-noble-from-worthy); and the express evidence for severing the bonds of view and the first obstacle of the self-mark (the path of seeing severs · the path of cultivation thins · the two times as backbone). This section, in three layers: entering-the-noble as "first realizing the noble's place" (3.1); the path of seeing as "severance," not "suppression" (3.2); one grammar of severance, a different exit (3.3).

### 3.1 Entering-the-noble as "first realizing the noble's place": first personal realization, hence joy

The worthy and the noble differ by one word: "realization." All that the three worthy ranks (abodes · practices · dedications) cultivate is "understanding" and "suppression" — by the wisdom-understanding won through hearing, reflection, and cultivation, they establish the view, suppress the afflictions, accumulate the provisions; but that is, throughout, "cultivating *toward* the noble's place," not yet "personally realizing the noble's place." The first ground's difference lies just in the two words "first realizing" (§2.2): on the path of seeing, the bodhisattva for the first time no longer merely "hears of" or "infers" suchness, but, by the wisdom of the equality of all dharmas, "directly sees" the real mark of suchness. This "first realizing the noble's place" is the watershed of worthy and noble — before, study outside the gate (worthy); now, the first stepping into the inner hall, the personal sight of the sanctum (noble).

And "joy" wells precisely from this "first realizing." Vasubandhu names the first ground not "the Wisdom-Ground" or "the Pure-Ground" but "Joy," because the texture of this moment is the dharma-joy of first realizing the noble's place across beginningless eons — like one long dwelling in a dark room who suddenly sees the daylight, like one who long heard of a land and now first arrives in its very country. This joy is not a heightening of emotion but the light borne by the qualitative change of "first realizing" itself. So the name "Ground of Joy" marks, in a single double-edged stroke, the first ground's two faces: it is "first realizing the noble's place" (the pass of entering-noble-from-worthy), and it is "much joy arising" (the dharma-joy this realization carries).

A contrast: one long enamoured of a certain landscape, who over the years has read every travelogue, map, and photograph of it, can speak of it fluently (the understanding of the worthy ranks); yet he has never personally been there. Until one day he first truly sets foot on that soil — the sole touching real ground, the eye meeting the real scene — and the tremor and joy of that moment is wholly different in kind from reading ten thousand travelogues. The former is "understanding" (knowing what it is); the latter is "realization" (personally living its reality). The first ground's path-of-seeing "first realizing the noble's place, much joy arises" is just this dharma-joy of "first personal arrival": the bodhisattva, for the first time since beginningless time, personally treads the noble ground.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the first ground is named "Joy," its doctrinal pivot in "first realizing the noble's place" (Vasubandhu, §2.2) — the three worthy ranks "understand · suppress" toward the noble, while the first ground for the first time directly contemplates suchness by the wisdom of equality, personally realizing the noble's place: the watershed of entering-noble-from-worthy. "Joy" is the dharma-joy welling from this qualitative change of "first realizing," and so the name "Ground of Joy" doubly marks the first ground as the beginning of the noble rank and the joy of this realization.

### 3.2 The path of seeing as "severance," not "suppression": the first cutting of the affliction-root

The qualitative change of entering-noble-from-worthy, brought down to the afflictions, is "suppression" turning into "severance." The three worthy ranks' suppression of afflictions is "pressing down" — by the force of wisdom-understanding they hold the afflictions down so they do not arise in act, yet their root is not removed; so the three worthy ranks, though they long suppress afflictions, have severed not one. The first ground's difference is that it is "severance": Vasubandhu plainly judges that "the bonds of view were severed at the first ground, on the path of seeing" (§2.3) — the bonds of view that are the path-of-seeing's to sever are here severed, extinguished, not pressed down but rooted out. This is the bodhisattva's first true "severing" of affliction since beginningless time, and so it is sharply other in kind than the three worthy ranks' "suppression."

The difference of suppression and severance is not a difference of degree but of kind. Suppress however long and deep, the affliction-root remains, able to rise when conditions gather; sever, and the root is removed, the severed affliction never again arising. What the first ground's path of seeing severs is "the bonds of view" — that class of affliction arising by discrimination, by wrong view (this paper marks it "discriminated afflictions," its Yogācāra classificatory nature detailed in Section V). The mark of this class of affliction is "deluded about principle" — a topsy-turvy misconstrual of suchness-the-real; once one sees the path, directly contemplating suchness, this principle-deluding affliction is severed then and there, as darkness meets light. So the path-of-seeing's severance is clean and sharp, an "outright severing": at the very moment of seeing suchness, the discriminated bonds of view are extinguished in a thought.

A contrast: weeding. The three worthy ranks' suppression of afflictions is like pressing grass with a great stone — while the stone is there the grass lies flat, seemingly clean, yet the grass-root is still in the soil, and move the stone (let conditions gather) and it grows back. The first ground's path-of-seeing severance is like cutting the grass and removing its root — the root gone, this stalk grows no more. The thirty minds of the three worthy ranks long pressed the grass with the stone (suppressing without severing); at the first ground's path of seeing, the bodhisattva for the first time bends down and pulls up the root of a whole class of grass (severing the discriminated bonds of view). Pressing grass and rooting it out may both look like "no grass showing," but in kind the one is a temporary suppression, the other a permanent severance. The first ground's "joy" has within it just this exhilaration of "rooting out, truly, for the first time."

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the first ground's path-of-seeing "severance" and the three worthy ranks' "suppression" differ in kind, not degree — suppression presses while the root remains (able to rise when conditions gather), severance removes the root (it arises no more). Vasubandhu judges that "the bonds of view were severed at the first ground, on the path of seeing" (§2.3), the bodhisattva's first true severing of the affliction-root; the bonds of view severed belong to the "principle-deluding" afflictions, severed in a thought when suchness is seen. The three worthy ranks long suppress (the stone on the grass); the first ground first severs (rooting out) — this is the qualitative turn from suppression to severance.

### 3.3 One and the same grammar of severance, a different exit: the same severing of the bonds of view, yet the two vehicles head for quiescence, the bodhisattva climbs on

A final question, and the most crucial of this axis: is the first ground's path-of-seeing severance of the discriminated afflictions the same thing as the śrāvaka's path-of-seeing severance? The answer is "the grammar is the same, the exit is different" — and this is the very showing of the bodhisattva path's independence (BODHI-P01) at the level of severance.

First, "the grammar is the same." The śrāvaka's path of seeing, too, by undefiled wisdom directly contemplates the four truths and severs the afflictions that are the path-of-seeing's to sever (the view-afflictions); the bodhisattva's first ground path of seeing, too, by the wisdom of equality directly contemplates suchness and severs the bonds of view that are the path-of-seeing's to sever. As regards the grammar of severance "the path of seeing severs the view-afflictions," the two are of one rank: both, on the path of seeing, by the wisdom of direct contemplation, sever outright the principle-deluding afflictions. This axis does not deny this sameness — the bodhisattva who has entered the grounds does indeed sever afflictions, and his severing of the bonds of view and the śrāvaka's severing of the view-afflictions are, in the *grammar* of severance, of the same form (this is the concrete fulfilment, at the path-of-seeing position, of what BODHI-P01 called "the same afflictions may fall, yet the measure and goal differ").

Then, "the exit is different," and here is the watershed. The śrāvaka severs the view-afflictions, goes on to sever the cultivation-afflictions, and his "exit" is to head for quiescence and take the fruit — severing them all, he realizes arhatship, enters nirvāṇa, the liberation of this one body (this is what the axis's first division, *sramana*, expounds, and what the Attainment-axis's arhat-mastery reads as fruit). The bodhisattva's first ground path-of-seeing severance of the bonds of view has a wholly different "exit": he does not take this severance as the provision for "taking realization, taking fruit" (BODHI-P01 already refuted the taking of realization), does not head for quiescence, does not abide in nirvāṇa, but takes this severance as the "beginning of ascending the noble," going on to the second ground and above, severing afflictions ground by ground, heading for buddha-wisdom and acting for others' benefit. The same severance of the path-of-seeing's afflictions: the śrāvaka "exits" by it toward nirvāṇa and stops; the bodhisattva "enters" by it toward the grounds and climbs. The affliction severed is of the same class, but the gate walked is in opposite directions — one the exit at the terminus, one the entrance at the start.

A contrast: two travelers alike pass through a perilous mountain pass (alike severing the path-of-seeing's afflictions). Having crossed it, one settles into the inn just outside the pass and takes "at last, the pass is crossed" for the journey's completion (the two vehicles head for quiescence, take severance as realization, stop at self-fruit); the other crosses the same pass but does not halt, taking the crossing as "the true climb has only just begun," and presses on toward the higher summit (the bodhisattva does not abide, takes severance as the beginning of ascending the noble, climbs on through the grounds). The pass is one and the same, the crossing is one and the same, but one takes it for the exit at the end, the other for the entrance at the start. The first ground's path of seeing, for the bodhisattva, is just this "entrance at the start": severing the bonds of view not to take the fruit, but to ascend the noble and continue.

> ✅ Doctrinally sound: the first ground's path-of-seeing severance of the discriminated afflictions and the śrāvaka's path-of-seeing severance of the view-afflictions are "the same grammar, a different exit" — both, on the path of seeing, sever the principle-deluding afflictions outright by the wisdom of direct contemplation (the grammar is the same; the axis does not deny this sameness), yet the two vehicles take severance as heading for quiescence and the fruit, stopping at self-fruit (the exit is the terminus), while the bodhisattva takes severance as the beginning of ascending the noble, not abiding but climbing on through the grounds toward buddha-wisdom and others' benefit (the exit is the start). This is the fulfilment, at the level of path-of-seeing severance, of BODHI-P01's bodhisattva-path independence: the same affliction severed in the same way, yet the measure and goal differ. (Note: the Yogācāra equation of "discriminated afflictions" with "the path-of-seeing's bonds of view," and its classificatory nature, is detailed in Section V.)

Taken together, the three layers — the first ground's entering-noble is pivoted on the qualitative change of "first realizing the noble's place" and the joy it wells (3.1); this change, brought down to the afflictions, is "severance" first replacing "suppression," rooting out the discriminated bonds of view (3.2); and this severance is one grammar with the śrāvaka's path-of-seeing severance yet a different exit — the same severing, yet the two vehicles head for quiescence, the bodhisattva climbs on (3.3) — jointly establish the thesis: **the first ground (the Ground of Joy) is the bodhisattva's pass from suppression to severance, from worthy into noble, its path-of-seeing severance of the discriminated afflictions both of one grammar with the two vehicles' severance and, by "not abiding but climbing on," of a different exit — opening the ten noble grounds' work of severance.**

## IV. Interdisciplinary Convergence

(This paper deliberately omits the section of interdisciplinary convergence; the reason and the declaration of the omission are given in Section VI. In brief: the spine of this paper is the Mahāyāna noble-stage doctrine of "directly contemplating suchness on the path of seeing, turning from suppression to severance, entering noble from worthy." Contemporary models of "sudden insight / awakening experience," of "phase transition / critical turning," of "irreversible cognitive reorganization" may, on the surface, be likened in respect of "the suddenness of a qualitative change," yet they cannot sustain a three-fold convergence free of forced analogy on the three meanings: that "the direct contemplation of suchness is a direct-perceptual personal realization, not a cognitive state"; that "the severance of the bonds of view is the permanent removal of the affliction-root, not a reversible psychological shift"; and that "the same severance with a different exit is a difference in the direction of the soteriology." Above all, the meaning that "the path-of-seeing's severance is the noble stage's irreversible direct contemplation of suchness" has no counterpart in any model reducible to a psychological or physical process. Hence, per the series' editorial convention, the section is cut, and so declared here.)

## V. Middle-Way Corrections

This section clears away three misreadings; the first concerns the documentary nature of this paper's core terminology and demands special honesty.

**⚠️ Correction 1: Equating "discriminated afflictions (severed on the path of seeing) / innate afflictions (severed on the path of cultivation)" with "the bonds of view / the afflictions of the path of cultivation" is a later Yogācāra classification, not the verbatim wording of this round's three texts.** This is the boundary this paper must mark first. The explicit wording of the three texts on which this round rests — the *Eighty-fascicle Avataṃsaka* (T0279), the *Daśabhūmika-sūtra* (T0287), and Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna* (T1522) — reads "the bonds of view were severed at the first ground, on the path of seeing" against "the afflictions of the path of cultivation (of the realms of desire, form, and formlessness) … grow gradually thinner and are gradually left behind across the grounds" (§2.3). What the three texts speak of is the pair "bonds of view (severed on the path of seeing) / afflictions of the path of cultivation (severed on the path of cultivation, thinning gradually)"; they do not produce the paired Yogācāra classificatory terms "discriminated-arising / innate-arising." To equate "the bonds of view severed on the path of seeing" outright with "discriminated-arising afflictions," and "the afflictions severed on the path of cultivation" outright with "innate-arising afflictions" — this pair of equations is the classificatory ascription of later Yogācāra (the *Cheng weishi lun* tradition). This paper continues to use the term "discriminated afflictions" for ease of prose and to thread the whole axis's scheme of stages (as this axis's PLAN also adopts), because it does mark the reality of "the principle-deluding bonds of view severed on the path of seeing"; but it must be marked: the three texts' explicit wording reaches only to "bonds of view / afflictions of the path of cultivation," and the equation "bonds of view = discriminated afflictions" is a later Yogācāra ascription. The reader should know: the distinction "the path of seeing severs the bonds of view, the path of cultivation severs the remaining afflictions" has the explicit wording of the three texts (§2.3), while the ascription "bonds of view = discriminated afflictions" belongs to the Yogācāra teaching-mode. Such Yogācāra classificatory text lies beyond this round's designated sources (T0279 / T0287 / T1522), to be supplied after a separate round of Yogācāra-treatise source-gathering (the *Cheng weishi lun* and the like); the final adjudication of its equation is for the author to make in accordance with school and treatise.

**⚠️ Correction 2: The translated names of the first ground, "Ground of Joy / Ground of Supreme Joy," are set side by side as variant renderings, with no side taken.** The name of the first ground reads, in the *Eighty-fascicle Avataṃsaka* (Śikṣānanda's translation), "Ground of Joy" (歡喜地), and in the *Daśabhūmika-sūtra* (Xuanzang's translation), "Ground of Supreme Joy" (極喜地) (§2.1) — these are variant renderings of one and the same ground (Skt. *pramuditā*), not a distinction of different grounds. This paper, in the formal listing of names and in its prose, follows the Avataṃsaka tradition with "Ground of Joy" (to keep consistent with this axis's earlier papers and the Avataṃsaka-series naming standard), and marks here that Xuanzang renders it "Ground of Supreme Joy"; the two renderings stand side by side, and this paper does not decide in the author's stead which name to adopt uniformly. Should the reader meet the first ground rendered "Ground of Supreme Joy" elsewhere, let it be known as a variant rendering of the same ground.

**❌ Misreading 3: The first ground's path of seeing already "severs all afflictions," and is no different from buddhahood.** This is a common misunderstanding and must be cleared. What the first ground's path of seeing severs is only the discriminated bonds of view that are "the path-of-seeing's to sever" (§2.3, "the bonds of view were severed at the first ground, on the path of seeing"); as for the innate, subtle afflictions that are "the path-of-cultivation's to sever" (the afflictions of the path of cultivation across the three realms), Vasubandhu plainly judges that they "grow gradually thinner and are gradually left behind across the grounds" (§2.3) — that is, they are severed gradually only through the path-of-cultivation positions of the second ground and above up to the tenth. So the first ground is the "first" of the noble stages, the "starting-up" of the process of severance, by no means the "consummation" of severance. To misread "first realizing the noble's place" as "ultimate realization," and "the severance of the Ground of Joy" as "severing all," is to erase the whole process of the cultivation of the second to seventh grounds (the next paper, BODHI-P08) and of the eighth ground and above, and to conflate the first noble of the causal stage with buddhahood. The first ground's joy is the joy of "first realizing," not the joy of "the ultimate"; the bodhisattva here mounts only the first stair of the noble, with nine grounds of road still ahead.

## VI. Conclusion

**Essential restatement.** The first ground (the Ground of Joy) is the bodhisattva's pass of "entering noble from worthy," and its pivot is the "path of seeing": here the bodhisattva first realizes the noble's place, first directly contemplates suchness by the wisdom of the equality of all dharmas, and on the path of seeing severs the discriminated afflictions (the bonds of view that are the path-of-seeing's to sever); this "severance" is not the "suppression" of the three worthy ranks but the first rooting-out of the affliction-root, and so the ground is named Joy ("first realizing the noble's place, much joy arises"). On the textual evidence, the *Eighty-fascicle Avataṃsaka*'s "Chapter on the Ten Grounds" and the *Daśabhūmika-sūtra* formally list the ten grounds from Joy (Supreme Joy) to Dharma-Cloud, spoken by the buddhas of the three times alike; Vasubandhu's *Daśabhūmika-vyākhyāna* glosses the first ground as "first realizing the noble's place, much joy arising," judges the first obstacle to be "the obstacle of the ordinary being's self-mark," and plainly judges that "the bonds of view were severed at the first ground, on the path of seeing" while "the afflictions of the path of cultivation grow gradually thinner across the grounds," with "the time of the path of seeing / the time of the path of cultivation" as its frame. On the doctrine, three layers bear the thesis: the entering-noble-from-worthy is pivoted on the qualitative change of "first realizing the noble's place" and the joy it wells (3.1); this change, brought down to the afflictions, is "severance" first replacing "suppression," rooting out the bonds of view (3.2); and this severance is one grammar with the śrāvaka's path-of-seeing severance yet a different exit — the same severing, the two vehicles head for quiescence, the bodhisattva climbs on (3.3). It must be honestly marked: the pairing of "discriminated / innate" with "bonds of view / afflictions of the path of cultivation" is a later Yogācāra ascription; "Joy / Supreme Joy" stand side by side as variant renderings; the first ground does not sever all afflictions (Section V).

**Guidance for practice.** Though the first ground's path of seeing is a noble-stage state beyond the ordinary being's reach, its doctrine offers the practitioner two things. First, to discern clearly the difference of "understanding · suppressing" from "realizing · severing." Daily effort lies mostly in "understanding" and "suppressing" — holding the afflictions down by right view so they do not arise in act; this is the work of the worthy ranks, most precious, yet one must know it is "suppression without severance," and must not mistake suppression for severance and breed conceit. The true severing of the affliction-root awaits the path of seeing that directly contemplates suchness; before that, honestly to suppress afflictions, to amass the provisions, and not to deceive oneself as already realized — this is the worthy ranks' own proper portion. Second, to take a measure of nourishment from the single name "Joy." The bodhisattva first realizes the noble's place and great joy arises — this hints that the verifying mark of true progress in practice is dharma-joy, not self-affliction or self-satisfaction. If, in the midst of effort, a corresponding dharma-joy wells up in the practitioner, it may be known as a sign of accord with the path; yet one must also warn oneself by the caution of Section V, not mistaking a momentary dharma-joy for the realization-measure of "first realizing the noble's place."

**Open questions.** First, this paper deliberately cut the Section IV interdisciplinary convergence, for the reason given above. Second, the Yogācāra ascription (the *Cheng weishi lun* tradition) of this paper's core terms "discriminated afflictions / innate afflictions" lies beyond this round's designated sources (T0279 / T0287 / T1522) and is not matched to textual evidence; after a separate round of Yogācāra-treatise source-gathering is supplied, this paper's argument on the ascription of severance should be reinforced, and the final adjudication of its equation "bonds of view = discriminated afflictions" is for the author to make in accordance with school and treatise. Third, the first ground's path of seeing severs only the discriminated (path-of-seeing's-to-sever) afflictions; the innate (path-of-cultivation's-to-sever) afflictions await gradual severance through the path-of-cultivation grounds. BODHI-P08 will treat the second to seventh grounds: the gradual severance of the innate afflictions in the path-of-cultivation positions, the process of the ten obstacles "from coarse into subtle," and the further fulfilment, in the path-of-cultivation positions, of "the same grammar of severance, a different exit" (the seventh, the Far-Reaching Ground, "able to pass beyond the world-transcending path of the two vehicles of the world," is precisely the plain evidence of "the different exit").

**The dual-axis seam (SEAM).** This paper meets the Attainment-axis `attainment-signatures/bodhisattva` at the two faces of "the first ground": the Realization-axis (this paper) tells the "walking" of the first ground's path of seeing — how, turning from suppression to severance, one directly contemplates suchness by the wisdom of equality and severs the bonds of view, entering noble from worthy; the Attainment-axis then reads the fruit-virtue this path of seeing accomplishes — the one portion of bodhisattva-mastery signed by "first realizing the noble's place" (the suchness realized at the first ground, the mastery of the first portion of the dharma-body). It must be sharply distinguished from the fruit-stage of the Attainment-axis's `tathagata`: the first ground is the causal stage, the first of the ten nobles, its realization and severance alike the affair of a causal-stage bodhisattva's portion, severing only the discriminated afflictions, not the ultimate of the tathāgata's fruit-stage. The doctrine of the seam is for the author to adjudicate; this paper does not speak in the Attainment-axis's stead.
