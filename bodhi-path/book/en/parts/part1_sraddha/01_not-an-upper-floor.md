## 1.1 Not an Upper Floor

Sooner or later, anyone drawn to the Great Vehicle is stopped by a question: shouldn't I first become an arahant, and only then speak of the bodhisattva path?

Behind the question stands a picture in wide circulation. It paints the road to buddhahood as a single long ladder: the four lower rungs are the śrāvaka's four fruits—stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner, arahant; climb these four, reach the top, then "turn from the lesser to the greater" and continue from there along the bodhisattva path to buddhahood. In that picture, the bodhisattva path is a second story built on top of arhatship: the ground floor must be finished before anything can rise above it. Many arrange their aspirations by this picture without a second thought—seek one's own deliverance first, and store the resolve to liberate beings in an attic marked "later."

But that is not where the canon sets the gate.

The chapter on the Arising of the Mind in the Yogācārabhūmi is where the treatises answer, head-on, what counts as entering the bodhisattva path. The treatise does not circle; it defines by the two words "arising of the mind":

> Further, once bodhisattvas have first aroused the mind, they are thereby said to have entered unsurpassed awakening and to be counted among the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle. ... Further, only after arousing the mind can bodhisattvas progressively and swiftly realize unsurpassed, perfect awakening—never without it; therefore the arising of the mind is the very root of unsurpassed awakening.

"Arousing the mind," said in full, is arousing bodhicitta—the resolve to seek unsurpassed awakening for the liberation of all beings. Watch the three verbs in this passage. Once the mind has arisen, one is said to have *entered* unsurpassed awakening—the threshold sits at the arising itself; to arouse is to cross. Once the mind has arisen, one is *counted among* the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle—identity, too, sits at the arising; to arouse is to be enrolled. And the arising is the *root* of unsurpassed awakening—without it there is no gradual realization at all; it is the zero-point from which the whole road is measured. Take the three verbs together: the treatise ties "entering the Great Vehicle" cleanly to the arising of the mind. In the entire passage, not one word mentions "first attain arhatship."

How does this mind arise? The chapter on the Ten Abodes in the Avataṃsaka-sūtra, at the very first of the bodhisattva's abodes—the Abode of Arousing the Mind—is concrete: a bodhisattva sees the World-Honored One, majestic in form and of great power; or hears the vast Buddha-dharma of the Tathāgata; or "sees beings undergoing intense sufferings"—and arouses bodhicitta, seeking all-knowledge. Note what the mind is aroused *for*: all-knowledge, the Buddha's own unobstructed wisdom, sought for the sake of beings—not one's own exit alone. Once this single thought arises, one enters the Abode of Arousing the Mind. Of the fifty stations of the bodhisattva, the entrance mark of the very first is the arising of bodhicitta itself—not "four fruits completed."

And where does the thought come from? The Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra answers directly:

> Compassion is the root of the Buddha-way. Why? The bodhisattva sees beings tormented by the sufferings of aging, sickness, and death, sufferings of body and mind, sufferings of this world and the next—and gives rise to great kindness and great compassion to rescue them from such suffering, and only then arouses the mind seeking unsurpassed, perfect awakening. ... And by the power of great compassion, though long due for nirvāṇa, he does not take its realization.

The first half is plain: seeing beings suffer, great compassion arises, and then the mind. The last clause carries enormous weight; read it again—"though long due for nirvāṇa, he does not take its realization." A person rooted in compassion reaches the very place where nirvāṇa is long since due to be realized—and does not take it. That sentence is the character of the entire bodhisattva path; we will return to it below.

### Two Roads, Not Two Stages of One

Someone may press: even if the gate stands at the arising of the mind, might one then walk the same subtractive road of severing fetters, merely through a different entrance? The canon closes that path too.

In the Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra there is an exchange that strikes the exact point. The question: since the bodhisattva also fully cultivates the thirty-seven factors of the way—the śrāvaka's own curriculum—why does he not fall into the śrāvaka's fruits? The answer: "fully" means fully contemplating and knowing *without taking realization*; and because he does not abandon beings, and because that resolve is conjoined with the wisdom of ultimate emptiness, he does not fall to the śrāvaka's ground. On one and the same field of contemplative wisdom, the śrāvaka contemplates and takes realization, entering the śrāvaka's station; the bodhisattva knows it through and through and does not take it—held by the vow not to abandon beings, he does not drop in. The same treatise traces the fork to its source: arahants and pratyekabuddhas, *at their first arising of the mind*, sought only to escape the sufferings of aging, sickness, and death, without great vows, without great kindness and compassion; bodhisattvas, *from the first arising of the mind*, make vast vows, bear great compassion, seek every merit and the true character of all dharmas. The cut that parts the two roads falls at the first thought of aspiration—not at the height of any fruit.

The chapter on the Ten Grounds in the Avataṃsaka has a simile that says this most transparently. A prince is born in the royal house, borne by the queen, complete with the royal marks; from birth he stands above the entire body of ministers—by the power of the royal line, not by any power of his own. A minister, however towering his talent and his deeds, is never "promoted" into princehood by merit. So too, says the sūtra, the bodhisattva: "at the first arising of the mind, by the aspiration for the great Dharma, he surpasses all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas." The surpassing is a matter of standing, possessed from the moment of arising—not a late overtaking earned at some fruit. And the simile closes on the echo of that sentence again: "Therefore the bodhisattva, though he courses in the limit of reality, does not take its realization."

By now three sentences stand in a row before us: "though long due for nirvāṇa, he does not take its realization"; "fully contemplating and knowing, without taking realization"; "though he courses in the limit of reality, he does not take its realization." Three sentences, one structure: **arriving at the very place of realization—and not taking it.** Here is the deepest parting of the two roads. The aim of the śrāvaka path is to take realization—nirvāṇa realized, defilements exhausted, no further becoming; its measure is subtraction, counting which fetters are severed. The aim of the bodhisattva path is non-abiding: though contemplation is complete, though nirvāṇa is long due, by great compassion and great vows he does not take what could be taken—so that through measureless eons of birth and death his heart does not sink in weariness, so that he goes on liberating beings, goes on accumulating the two provisions of merit and wisdom, practicing the six perfections of giving, discipline, patience, energy, meditation, and wisdom. Its measure, accordingly, is not subtraction but accumulation: what mind has been aroused, what provisions amassed, for whose sake the realization within reach remains untaken.

A comparison. Two people each pay off their debts in full. One pays off in order to be free of debt once and for all—settle and leave the table. The other pays off so that, credit restored, he can keep borrowing, keep building, keep feeding more people—he could perfectly well stop there and enjoy his ease (long due for nirvāṇa), yet he does not stop (and does not take its realization), because what he wants was never "no debt" but the enterprise itself. The act of repayment is identical; the directions of the two lives are opposite. So with the bodhisattva's severing of afflictions and the śrāvaka's severing of fetters: on the grounds the bodhisattva, too, severs (a matter for Part Five), and the act can look alike; but in the śrāvaka's ledger, exhaustion means *settled and departed*, while in the bodhisattva's ledger, severing means *the obstacle cleared, the journey continued*. Different ledgers, different roads.

### What Card the Gate Reads

Now "why not an upper floor" can be said to the bottom.

The gate of the four śrāmaṇya-phalas reads the card of severing: without the three fetters severed, no one is called a stream-enterer—a gate of qualification, each stage strictly awaiting the one before, no shortcuts. The gate of the bodhisattva path reads the card of arising: a person who has severed not a single fetter, yet has aroused bodhicitta, is counted by the canon "among the bodhisattvas of the Great Vehicle"; conversely, a person perfect in the four fruits who has not aroused the great mind is not automatically counted a bodhisattva on the strength of his fruit. The criterion lies in the mind, not in the fruit.

Another comparison. A driver's license is a matter of qualification: test by test, stage by stage; fail one, and the next is closed. Resolving to become a physician is another kind of thing entirely: the resolve stands at the moment of the vow, with no relation whatsoever to how many licenses one holds. "You must first pass every driving test before you are qualified to resolve on medicine"—anyone hears the absurdity. Yet "you must first become an arahant before you are qualified for the bodhisattva path" is the very same sentence. The arising of the mind is a vow, not a further examination; it enters in a single thought and waits on no prior stage.

And there is a deeper layer still. The chapter on Lineage in the Yogācārabhūmi says the bodhisattva has a lineage of his own—a native capacity for buddhahood—and that it is "unsurpassed and supreme, exceeding all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas." It draws a further distinction: the lineage of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas can realize only the purification of the affliction-obstruction—afflictions severed, birth and death ended; the bodhisattva lineage can realize the purification of the affliction-obstruction *and* of the knowledge-obstruction—even the subtlest unknowing that veils all-knowledge is to be exhausted. The fruit sought differs; the capacity relied upon differs. This is no longer a difference of "how far along one road," but a difference in the very nature of the vehicle. The prince outranks the ministers by blood, not by deeds; the bodhisattva surpasses the two vehicles by lineage and by the arising of the mind, not by accumulated fruits.

This is why the chapter on the Merit of the First Arising of the Mind in the Avataṃsaka dares to go as far as it does. Suppose someone makes offerings to measureless beings and moreover teaches them, one by one, to attain the fruits of stream-entry, once-returning, non-returning, arhatship, even the way of the pratyekabuddha—the merit of all this, set beside the merit of a bodhisattva's first arising of the mind, "does not reach one part in a hundred, one part in a thousand," down to the finest fraction. Why so vast a gap? The sūtra answers itself: because the Buddhas, at their first arising of the mind, arouse it not merely so that beings may attain the four fruits and the pratyekabuddha's way, but "so that the lineage of the Tathāgata not be cut off"—that the Buddha-line continue without end. With such an aim, the merit of this thought simply does not lie within the scale of the four fruits. If the bodhisattva path were truly the sequel to arhatship, its first arising would be at most the sequel's preface—how could a preface outweigh the consummate merit of the entire main text? The only reading that holds is this: from the very beginning, this thought does not lie on that axis at all.

### Setting Two Things Straight

Two things must be set straight here, lest the chapter be misread.

First, the independence of the path is no disparagement of the śrāvaka. To say the bodhisattva path does not take arhatship as its prior stage is in no way to say arhatship is unreal or inferior. Quite the opposite—"not taking realization" has the force of a vow precisely because the nirvāṇa is genuinely there to take: were one not truly "long due for nirvāṇa," then "not taking its realization" would be empty talk. The śrāvaka path truly ends birth and death; the subtractive scale of the four fruits is real and unerring—the previous volume of this series, *The Four Fruits of the Path*, has said so head-on. The afflictions the bodhisattva severs are as truly severed as the śrāvaka's fetters. The two roads differ in aim and in measure, not in one being true and the other false. To hear "I walk another road" as "your road is wrong" is to mishear.

Second, an honest accounting is owed. The sentence "the bodhisattva path is not a continuation of arhatship" is not, as a sentence, found in any scripture. Search the sources through, and no such verbatim negation appears. The canon expresses the point with a family of sentences all facing one way—"passing beyond the grounds of the śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha, he enters the bodhisattva position"; "he does not fall to the śrāvaka's ground"; "fully contemplating and knowing, without taking realization"; "though he courses in the limit of reality, he does not take its realization"; "at the first arising of the mind he surpasses all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas." This chapter reads that family as "an independent path, not a continuation"—a synthesis well grounded in the texts; but the reader should know it for a reading, not a quotation.

### The Gate Opens at the Arising of the Mind

Gather the chapter into one sentence: the gate of the bodhisattva path opens not on the rooftop of arhatship but at the single thought of arousing bodhicitta. The gate reads the card of arising, not the card of severing—so it is entered in one thought and waits on no prior stage. The road aims at the accumulation of not-taking-realization, not the subtraction of taking it—so the two are not two stages of one road but two roads. And the thought belongs to a vehicle held by another lineage altogether—so this is no promotion, but another vehicle.

A practitioner taking stock should therefore change the question. Not "must I first become an arahant, and then turn from the lesser to the greater"—that question traps a person in the ladder-anxiety of qualifications, as if the Great Vehicle were a ticket redeemable only after collecting every stamp of the Lesser. Ask instead: "this thought of mine—does it seek escape from my own suffering, or does it seek unsurpassed awakening for the liberation of beings?" That question returns a person to the arising itself. Once bodhicitta has arisen, you are on the road.

And yet—to have entered the gate is not to stand firm. The arising opens the gate; the first stretch inside is called the Ten Faiths, and that stretch, of all fifty stations, is the one most given to falling back. Can one who has aroused the mind still regress? And if one regresses, did one ever truly enter? That is for the next chapter.
