## 2.2 Growing Up in the Buddha's House

To enter the abodes is to get through the door. But the Ten Abodes are not a point; they are ten halls in succession. In the chapter, the bodhisattva Dharma-Wisdom, sustained by the Buddha's majestic power, lists the ten names in one breath:

> The bodhisattva's abodes are ten, which the buddhas of the past, the future, and the present have taught, will teach, and now teach. What are the ten? The Abode of First Arousing the Mind, the Abode of Governing the Ground, the Abode of Cultivation, the Abode of Noble Birth, the Abode of Endowment with Means, the Abode of the Rectified Mind, the Abode of Non-Regress, the Abode of the Innocent Child, the Abode of the Prince, and the Abode of Consecration. These are called the bodhisattva's ten abodes, taught by the buddhas of the past, the future, and the present.

Arousing the Mind, Governing the Ground, Cultivation, Noble Birth, Endowment with Means, the Rectified Mind, Non-Regress, the Innocent Child, the Prince, Consecration—ten names. The last chapter looked at the head of this list (Arousing the Mind) and at the seventh (Non-Regress); this chapter reads the whole string together. And read together, a thread rises to the surface of its own accord.

Look at the last four names: **Noble Birth**, the **Innocent Child**, the **Prince**, the **Consecration**. Born into a noble house; the innocent child; the prince; the anointing. This is unmistakably a line of royal blood coming of age—born into the royal family, a child in his innocence, grown into a prince, and at the last receiving the rite of consecration. "Consecration" is no vague auspicious word: in ancient India it was the great rite of investiture by which a crown prince was installed and a king enthroned—water of the four great seas poured upon the prince's crown, whence he succeeded to the throne and was fit to do a king's work. That the Ten Abodes close on the "Abode of Consecration" borrows exactly this rite: the bodhisattva, grown within the Buddha's house into a son of the Dharma-King, is anointed to succeed to the Dharma-King's seat.

Why is this thread a legitimate reading? Because the ten abodes share one place and one standing. The last chapter quoted the chapter's governing sentence: "The bodhisattva dwells in the family of the buddhas of the three times." All ten abodes, without exception, are dwelt in within the house of the buddhas of the three times; and all who dwell there, without exception, are called sons of the Buddha. A house is a place of upbringing—and dwelling in one house, the ten abodes are not ten isolated points but one child of the family passing, in this house, through ten stages from entering, to growing, to coming into his own. The first six—the entering resolve of Arousing the Mind, the tilling of the mind's soil in Governing the Ground, the taking-up of practice in Cultivation, then Endowment with Means, the Rectified Mind, the view's standing-fast in Non-Regress—are the early reach of this line; in the last four, the names themselves speak the arc: birth, childhood, princehood, crown. The Ten Abodes are the whole of a Buddha-son's growing up in the Buddha's house.

One honest sentence must be said here first. What this chapter reads is the **arc**, not a station-by-station exposition of all ten. The middle abodes—Governing the Ground, Cultivation, Noble Birth, Endowment with Means, the Rectified Mind, the Innocent Child, the Prince—each have their own inner attainments and curricula, set out in detail by the great commentators; this book does not unfold them one by one here, for that would require matching the fine print of the commentaries item by item—another labor for another place. This chapter relies on the two things the scripture itself supplies: the full **list of names**, and the governing **frame** of "Buddha-family, sons of the Buddha." It is like reading a table of contents: when the chapter titles themselves run "Birth—Childhood—Investiture as Heir—Coronation," then even without a close reading of every chapter one may rightly say, this is the biography of a child of a royal house, raised to inherit. The names of the Ten Abodes are just such a table of contents, the arc already showing in the titles; this chapter reads the arc, and does not pretend to the close reading. (One side-note: this sūtra writes the fifth as "Endowment with Means" and the ninth simply as "the Prince," where other texts have "Means Fully Endowed" and "the Dharma-Prince"—same stations, variant names; let them stand side by side, no need to choose.)

### What It Looks Like, Fully Grown

The arc ends at the Abode of Consecration. The chapter describes its mark:

> This bodhisattva attains the accomplishment of ten kinds of knowledge. What are the ten? Shaking countless worlds, illuminating countless worlds ... bringing countless beings to discipline. These are the ten. Sons of the Buddha! This bodhisattva's body and bodily deeds, his spiritual transformations, his knowledge of the past, knowledge of the future, knowledge of the present, his perfecting of buddha-lands—the realm of his mind and the realm of his knowledge cannot be known; even the Dharma-Prince bodhisattva cannot know them.

Two things weigh here. First, the bodhisattva of the Consecration "attains the accomplishment of ten kinds of knowledge"—shaking countless worlds, illuminating countless worlds, sustaining countless worlds, even bringing countless beings to discipline. These ten are no abstract titles; they are the working powers of one who has received office—as a new king, once anointed, truly holds the authority that commands the four quarters. Second, and still more telling of the station's depth, is the gap at the end: this bodhisattva's bodily deeds and transformations, his knowledge of the three times, "the realm of his mind and the realm of his knowledge cannot be known—**even the Dharma-Prince bodhisattva cannot know them**." Even the ninth abode's Dharma-Prince cannot fathom the tenth. Between the invested and the not-yet-invested stands a door the prior station cannot cross.

A comparison: the crown prince and the crowned king. The Abode of the Prince is the heir already named—exalted in standing, his title fixed, yet not enthroned. The Abode of Consecration is the king who has passed through the coronation and taken the throne—from then on his writ runs where the heir's never could; hence "even the Dharma-Prince cannot know." The same house, the same sonship; yet the coronation rite draws a leap of power and of office between them. That the Ten Abodes end on the Consecration marks exactly this leap: the upbringing of the abode-position here graduates, here receives its office.

### Anointed Is Not Buddha

The misreading most to be guarded against grows precisely out of these splendid words. "Consecration," "ten kinds of knowledge," "even the Dharma-Prince cannot know"—read this far, and it is easy to suppose the Abode of Consecration is already buddhahood, already the end. The line must be drawn cleanly: **the Abode of Consecration, though the consummation of the last of the Ten Abodes, remains a causal station, still the first of the three worthy ranks—not the fruit of buddhahood.**

Three reasons. First, by station: the Consecration is the end of the second ten among the fifty. Above it still stand the Ten Practices, the Ten Dedications, and the Ten Grounds—of the three worthy ranks (abodes, practices, dedications), only the first stretch is done; the Ten Grounds' path of seeing and path of cultivation has not yet begun, to say nothing of equal and sublime awakening beyond the grounds. Second, by the name: the consecration here is the anointing of the Dharma-***Prince***—the investiture of a son of the Dharma-King within the causal stage; the name lies near the Tathāgata's final estate, but the station lies far from it. Third, the sentence "even the Dharma-Prince cannot know" tells of a **gap between stations**—a later abode deeper than an earlier one—which is to say it still stands inside the comparison of abode with abode: a height within the ten, not an exit from the ten into buddhahood.

So the "knowledge" of the ten kinds is the knowledge of the abode-stage, the power of the causal stage; it may not be flatly equated with the Tathāgata's omniscient knowledge at the fruit. To read the Consecration as buddhahood is to mistake the investiture that ends an upbringing for the consummation that ends the way—like mistaking the prince's coronation for the close of the dynasty. A coronation is the **beginning** of a king's reign, not its end: invested, he must now govern. So with the Abode of Consecration: the abode-stage upbringing complete, this invested son of the Buddha must now go out of the house door and do the work of the Dharma-King's house—the liberation of beings.

### Growing at Home, Without Hurry

Gather the chapter into one sentence: the Ten Abodes are the Buddha-son's season of upbringing in the Buddha's house—from the entering of the Abode of Arousing the Mind to the investiture of the Consecration; the names themselves disclose the arc of noble birth, innocent childhood, princehood, and anointing; the arc's end is the ten knowledges fit for the Dharma-King's work; and this consummation is the consummation of the causal stage, not the finality of the fruit.

For the practitioner, this stretch carries a very practical reassurance. One who has crossed the threshold of position-non-regress and still finds himself unremarkable often frets in secret: I have entered—why am I still like this? The Ten Abodes give the placement: you are in the season of **growing**. A child of the royal house, from cradle to coronation, passes through a long, solid childhood and youth—unspectacular, and not a day of it dispensable. The work of this stretch is not to hurry after marvels but to grow up honestly at home: till the mind's ground, take up the practice, ripen the view. And the Consecration's ten knowledges carry their own reminder: what the abode-stage matures toward is the working power to do the Dharma-King's work—the strength to benefit beings—not a private summit of self. One grows up in order to go out and work.

And the first work outside the door? The six perfections. Done how? The Ten Practices. That is Part Three.
