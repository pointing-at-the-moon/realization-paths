## 5.1 The First Cut

Let the question the transition carried in be asked outright: from the Ten Faiths through the abodes, the practices, and the dedications—thirty stations—has the bodhisattva ever truly *severed* a single affliction?

The answer is: never. The three worthy ranks are, throughout, positions of **suppression**—with the understanding ripened by hearing, reflection, and cultivation, the afflictions are pressed down so they do not stir; but their root is never cut. Suppression is a stone laid on grass: while the stone sits, the grass lies flat and all looks clean; the root waits in the soil, and move the stone, the grass returns. Thirty stations of the worthy ranks, suppressed however long and however deep, remain in nature a pressing, not a removing.

Then where does "suppress" turn to "sever," "worthy" to "noble"? The canon's answer: the first ground, the Ground of Joy—the path of seeing. First, the full roster. In the Chapter on the Ten Grounds, the bodhisattva Vajragarbha lists the ten:

> What are the ten? First, the Ground of Joy; second, the Ground of Stainlessness; third, the Light-Emitting Ground; fourth, the Ground of Blazing Wisdom; fifth, the Hard-to-Conquer Ground; sixth, the Manifest Ground; seventh, the Far-Reaching Ground; eighth, the Immovable Ground; ninth, the Ground of Good Wisdom; tenth, the Dharma-Cloud Ground. Sons of the Buddha! These ten grounds of the bodhisattva the buddhas of the three times have taught, will teach, and now teach.

Joy, Stainlessness, Light-Emitting, Blazing Wisdom, Hard-to-Conquer, Manifest, Far-Reaching, Immovable, Good Wisdom, Dharma-Cloud—ten grounds, once again "taught, to be taught, and now taught" by all the buddhas. (Xuanzang's translation of the Daśabhūmika renders the first as "the Ground of Supreme Joy"—the same ground, a variant rendering; let the two stand together.) This chapter walks the first: the Ground of Joy.

### Why "Joy," of All Names

The name of the first ground Vasubandhu glosses with utter economy in his treatise on the Daśabhūmika:

> Accomplishing the unsurpassed practice of benefiting self and others, first realizing the noble's place, much joy arises; hence it is named the Ground of Joy.

The weight sits in four words: "first realizing the noble's place." What the three worthy ranks cultivated all along was *understanding*—view built, afflictions suppressed, provisions amassed by hearing, reflection, and cultivation; but all of that was cultivation *toward* the noble's place, work done outside the door. The first ground differs in a single word: *realizing*. On the path of seeing, for the first time, the bodhisattva no longer hears of suchness or infers it—he **sees** it, by the wisdom of the equality of all dharmas. Before, he was reading travelogues; now he has set foot on the land.

A comparison: a man has loved a far country for years—read every travel account, every map, every photograph; he can speak of it chapter and verse (the worthy ranks' understanding, knowing *about*). Then one day he truly stands on that ground for the first time—real earth under his feet, the actual scene filling his eyes—and the jolt of that moment is different in kind from ten thousand travelogues. That is realization, knowing *by presence*. "First realizing the noble's place, much joy arises"—the first setting of foot, in beginningless time, upon noble ground. The joy is not a surge of mood; it is the light that the change of kind itself gives off. Which is why this ground is not called the Ground of Wisdom or the Ground of Purity, but, of all names, Joy.

### Where the Knife Falls

"First realizing," brought down onto the afflictions, means: severance replaces suppression for the first time. Vasubandhu's pivotal judgment:

> The bonds of view are severed at the first ground, at the time of the path of seeing.

The "bonds of view" are the afflictions that arise from discrimination and wrong views—confusions about the *principle*, inverted takes on how things are. Their signature is exactly this: confused about the principle, they die the moment the principle is seen—as darkness meets light. So the severance of the path of seeing is an **outright severance**: in the instant suchness is directly beheld, the discriminated bonds of view are cut entire, clean through. This is the bodhisattva's first true severing of an affliction's root in all beginningless time: not pressing the grass down, but bending, for the first time, and pulling a whole class of grass up by the root.

But in the same passage Vasubandhu draws a second line: the three realms' "afflictions of the path of cultivation," together with the habit-energies of ignorance, only "thin and recede gradually"—a thinning across the grounds, not an extinguishing at a stroke. In other words, the first ground's knife cuts only the class that is "the path of seeing's to sever"; there is another class—arising from beginningless habituation, stirring of themselves, confused about *things* rather than principle—that the knife cannot reach, and that the later grounds must grind away. The later Yogācāra masters gave the two classes a pair of names: what the path of seeing severs they called the *discriminated* afflictions; what the path of cultivation severs, the *innate*. One honest note: that pair of names is the later masters' matching; the treatise's own words are "bonds of view" and "afflictions of the path of cultivation." This book uses "discriminated" and "innate" for ease of telling, remembering that the names are a later judgment while the division itself is express text.

Vasubandhu also lists ten obstacles, one countered by each ground; the first is named "the ordinary being's obstacle of the mark of self"—the ordinary being's grasping at a self. The first ground's cut falls exactly on this coarsest obstacle; from here the ten run finer and finer, which is the next chapter's business.

### One Knife, Two Ledgers

Now the weightiest question in the volume: is the first ground's severance the same thing as the śrāvaka's severance on *his* path of seeing?

Answer: **the same grammar, a different exit.** First the sameness. The śrāvaka's path of seeing also severs, outright and by direct insight, the afflictions that are the path of seeing's to sever; the bodhisattva's first ground also severs the bonds of view, outright, by the wisdom of equality beholding suchness. As a *format* of severance—"the path of seeing severs the afflictions of views"—the two roads are of one grammar. This book does not deny the sameness: the bodhisattva on the grounds truly severs, and severs by the same grammar as the śrāvaka. Part One said the two separate tracks could share some of the grammar of severance; here that grammar formally connects.

Then the difference, where the waters part. The śrāvaka severs the afflictions of views, goes on to sever those of cultivation, and his exit is realization: severance complete, the fruit of arhatship attained, nirvāṇa entered, the knife sheathed and the table left—the ledger the previous volume, *The Four Fruits of the Path*, kept from end to end. The bodhisattva's first-ground severance exits elsewhere entirely: he does not cash this severance for a realized fruit, does not head for quiescence, does not abide in nirvāṇa; he takes the cut as the *beginning of the noble ranks*, and carries the knife on—ground after ground, severing toward the Buddha's wisdom, working for beings. A comparison: two travelers cross the same perilous mountain pass. One, across it, takes a room at the inn beyond the pass and counts "through at last" as the journey's fulfillment; the other crosses the same pass and does not stop—to him, crossing is where the real climb begins, and he goes on toward the higher summits. One pass, one crossing; one takes it for the exit at the end, the other for the entrance at the beginning. One knife, two ledgers: on the ledger of subtraction, severance complete means accounts settled; on the ledger of accumulation, severance means the obstacle cleared and the journey continued.

### First Realization Is Not Final

Last, a misunderstanding to clear: does the first ground's path of seeing "sever all afflictions"—is the first-ground bodhisattva already as a buddha?

No. What the first ground severs is only the one class, the discriminated bonds of view; the innate, subtle afflictions, Vasubandhu plainly judges, must "thin and recede gradually across the grounds"—ground after ground from the second to the tenth, final only at buddhahood. The first ground is the *first* of the noble stations, the *start* of the severing, in no way its completion. The joy of the Ground of Joy is the joy of first realization, not of finality; the bodhisattva here mounts the first step of the noble stair, with nine grounds ahead.

Two takings for the practitioner. First, keep "understanding and suppressing" distinct from "realizing and severing": daily practice mostly works in the former—right view holding the afflictions down; that is the worthy ranks' work, precious beyond price, but know it for suppressed-not-severed, and do not let held-down pass for cut-out, breeding the conceit of attainment. Second, take one measure from the name "Joy": real progress on the way shows itself as the joy of the Dharma—not as self-mortification, and not as self-congratulation. If such joy rises in your practice, it is a sign of accord with the way; and remember the last paragraph all the same—a moment's joy is not the realization of the noble's place.

The first cut has fallen; the discriminated afflictions are gone. But the habits that never depended on discrimination—the ones born in the bone, that the knife cannot reach? For those: grind. Next chapter.
