## 5.3 The Effort Falls Away

From the first arousing of the mind to the seventh ground, the bodhisattva has been forcing. Suppression takes deliberate attention; severance takes added effort; even the seventh ground's markless practice carries the word "application" in its own name. And so the question that has hung over the whole road finds its answer at the eighth ground: there comes a day when the forcing stops—practice without application, spontaneous, of itself. The eighth ground's name is the Immovable.

The ground has a gate. The Chapter on the Ten Grounds states it without remainder:

> Leaving all discriminating notions of mind, thought, and consciousness, grasping at nothing, like space itself; entering the space-like nature of all dharmas—this is called attaining the forbearance of the non-arising of dharmas. Sons of the Buddha! The bodhisattva who accomplishes this forbearance at once enters the eighth, the Immovable Ground.

The gate's name is "the forbearance of the non-arising of dharmas." Begin with the word in it most easily misread—"forbearance." This is not the forbearance of gritted teeth; it is acceptance, accordance, the settled allowing of what is so: the mind toward the truth that "dharmas never arise and never cease" has wholly consented, settled, ceased to waver. The Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra gives the defining formula: "contemplating that all dharmas neither arise nor cease ... such that in the wisdom of true reality nothing can break it and nothing can move it—this is called the dharma-forbearance of non-arising." At the first ground's path of seeing, the bodhisattva already beheld suchness; but through the seventh ground, the mind's abiding in that reality still needed application to maintain—like standing steady only with a hand on the rail. At the eighth, toward the non-arising of all dharmas the mind abides **unbreakable, unmovable**—no hand on the rail; it stands of itself. And the positional force of this forbearance the text leaves no room to mistake: "accomplishing this forbearance, he **at once** enters the eighth, the Immovable Ground." The forbearance completes; the gate opens. The forbearance of non-arising is the key to the Immovable Ground.

### The Hand Lets Go, and the Practice Does Not Stop

Inside the gate, what is the condition? The chapter:

> Abiding in the Immovable Ground, he at once casts off all practice-with-application and attains the dharma of non-application; the busy striving of body, speech, and mind is all stilled; he abides in the practice of ripened fruit.

All applied practice cast off, the dharma of non-application attained—the striving of body, speech, and mind stilled, and the practice not stopped. Xuanzang's translation of the Daśabhūmika says it in one phrase: "leaving all applications, he arrives **spontaneously, of itself**, at the nature of non-application." Spontaneity—without deliberation, of its own accord, running without rest. Vasubandhu's gloss of the name "Immovable" has two clauses: "because it cannot be broken," and "named the ground of non-application, because it well raises the prior path." The first says nothing can mar or shake it; the second is finer still—"well raises the prior path": the path cultivated through the first seven grounds no longer needs pushing; here it rises of itself, runs of itself.

So "Immovable" is a word with two faces, to be read together. One: *nothing can move it*—the consent to reality is now unbreakable; the steadiness of non-regress. Two: *it moves without being pushed*—application cast off, the practice runs unforced. And the two faces make each other: precisely because the mind stands on reality past all shaking, no application is needed to hold it there, and so it runs of itself. The Immovable is no dead stillness; it is exactly that lively steadiness which nothing can shake and which no one needs to push.

A comparison: mastery. The beginner at cycling, at swimming, at an instrument forces everything—deliberate at every step: thus the hand, thus the foot, vigilant thought by thought, and one lapse spills it all. And at the far end of mastery? Deliberation ends; hand and foot move of themselves; the body knows—what we call doing without thinking, accomplished of itself. The eighth ground's casting off application is exactly this change of kind, from the beginner's effortful every-step to the master's unthought flow: the manifold exertions of seven grounds, like the beginner's forcing; the eighth, like mastery's spontaneity—"the striving of body, speech, and mind all stilled," and the practice never pausing.

But beside that comparison a warning sign must be posted. The master's effortlessness and the beginner's refusal of effort are heaven and earth apart—one is practice carried to its limit until application drops away; the other is no practice at all, dressed up as ease. The world is full of those who hear "non-application" and "spontaneity" and fetch them as footnotes for laziness: not practicing, and calling it spontaneity; not keeping discipline, and calling it naturalness. That is to mistake the fruit of consummate cultivation for the failure of cultivation never begun—alike in look, opposite in fact. The eighth ground's non-application is built out of seven grounds of application; without those seven grounds of forcing, there is no this ground of not-forcing.

### The Ten Masteries, and the Last of the Three Non-Regresses

Of the bodhisattva who has entered the Immovable Ground, the sūtra lists ten masteries: mastery over life, over mind, over wealth, over action, over birth, over vow, over understanding, over will, over wisdom, over dharma. (Xuanzang's renderings differ slightly—lifespan, livelihood, resolve, spiritual power, and so on—one set of masteries, variant translations; let them stand together.) Vasubandhu judges that the ten masteries counteract ten terrors, one for one: the terror of death, of the stain of afflictions, of poverty, of evil action and evil destinies, of not getting what is sought, even the terror before the majesty of great assemblies—wherever the mastery reaches, the terror goes out. Non-regressing, therefore masterful; masterful, therefore unafraid.

And the eighth ground's non-regress is the last of the three. The Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra judges it plainly: "the dharma-forbearance of non-arising is itself the stage of *avaivartika*"—avaivartika, the non-regressing—and of one who abides there, "life after life he ever obtains the fruit and the spiritual powers, never losing them, never regressing." The Daśabhūmika adds the inner mark: "all activity of mind, thought, and consciousness no longer manifests"; the afflictions of the lower grounds "never again stir." Position-non-regress (the Ten Abodes) secured *who one is*; practice-non-regress (the Ten Dedications) secured *what one is doing*; the eighth ground secures the *thought*—thought after thought according with the Dharma, the discriminating activity of mind and consciousness no longer arising, life after life unlost. This book keeps the classifiers' name for it, "mindfulness-non-regress," with the usual honest note: the sources' own words are "avaivartika," "never losing, never regressing"; the threefold proper naming—position, practice, mindfulness—is the later Tiantai and Yogācāra classification, and the masters differ on whether this last is set at the seventh ground or the eighth; this book follows the Avataṃsaka line and sets it at the eighth. With this, the three non-regresses are all in hand: from position to practice to thought, three locks fastened layer upon layer across thirty-odd stations.

### Non-Regressing Is Not Arrived

By now the eighth ground is high enough to take the breath: the forbearance of non-arising, spontaneous non-application, ten masteries, mindfulness-non-regress. And then the chapter records a scene of utmost weight—at the moment the bodhisattva attains this forbearance, the buddhas appear before him and exhort him with their own voice:

> Good son! The ten powers, the fearlessnesses, the eighteen distinctive dharmas of the buddhas that we possess—you have not yet attained them. You should, for the accomplishing of these dharmas, press on with vigour, and not let go of this gate of forbearance.

The buddhas say it themselves: the Buddha's ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, the eighteen distinctive dharmas—**you do not yet have them**. Press on; do not take this forbearance for enough. That one sentence nails the eighth ground to its rightful place: the causal stage. Of the ten masteries the treatise writes with care—"the **bodhisattva** attains the **bodhisattva's** ten masteries," the word doubled; and the Avataṃsaka keeps a separate roster elsewhere, "the ten masteries of the buddhas, the world-honored ones"—two lists, two names, not one thing. The bodhisattva's ten masteries are the masteries of the road; the Buddha's are the masteries of arrival. Near names, far stations—a boundary this book's twin volume on the attainment side will have much more to say of; here it is enough to set the marker firm.

A comparison: a train has come onto a one-way track that cannot run backward. From here it will never go back—that is real, a settled security (mindfulness-non-regress). But "never going back" is not "arrived": there are stations ahead (the ninth ground, the tenth), and the terminus (buddhahood) is not yet reached. The eighth ground's height is the height of "on the irreversible track, running steady and unforced"; the buddhas' exhortation is exactly the call: "the terminus is not yet—do not fall asleep on the train." Non-regressing and not-yet-complete stand together without strain: that is the truth of the noble still on the causal stage—high but not at the summit, steady but not yet done.

Two takings for the practitioner. First, the direction of effort: the consummation of effort is not "ever more forcing" but ripeness past the need of force—for those long at the work, progress is verified by the fading of effort's traces, not the growing of effort's quantity. And keep the warning sign in view: non-application is practiced into being, never lounged into. Second, honesty of self-placement: even the eighth-ground bodhisattva the buddhas admonish—"you have not yet attained; press on." How much more we? Not to take a moment's accord for finality, not to take mid-road steadiness for home—that is the honesty that keeps faith with the Immovable Ground.

The Buddha's ten powers, at the eighth ground, "not yet attained." From "not yet" to "complete"—how many steps remain? Two: the ninth ground, and the tenth. The last chapter.
